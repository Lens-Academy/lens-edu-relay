## Test:
#### Text
content::
Chapter 6 sends a refrigerator blueprint back a thousand years. A medieval blacksmith could build it — but would be astonished that it produces cold air, because the underlying physics (gas compression and expansion) was unknown to them. The chapter uses this to illustrate what it means to face something that knows more about reality than you do. It then applies this logic to a superintelligent AI, and explores the domains where humanity is most exposed.

#### Question
feedback:: true
content:: In your own words, what does the refrigerator thought experiment demonstrate about what a superintelligent AI might be capable of?

assessment-instructions::
**1** — Cannot explain the thought experiment, or confuses it with a different argument. *Example: "It shows that AI will learn from old technology."*

**2** — Can describe the refrigerator story but cannot connect it to AI capability. *Example: "A medieval blacksmith wouldn't understand how a refrigerator works. So we won't understand AI either."*

**3** — Correctly explains the core principle: superior knowledge of physical laws enables devices that appear impossible to those who lack that knowledge — and a superintelligence would have this advantage over humanity in domains we don't understand. *Example: "The blacksmith builds the refrigerator but is astonished by cold air, because they don't know the temperature-pressure law. A superintelligence would know things about physics, biology, or the human brain that we don't — and could design technology that defeats us in ways we wouldn't understand even after seeing them happen. We'd be in the blacksmith's position."*

**4** — As above, plus connects to the specific vulnerable domains Chapter 6 names — biology and human psychology — and explains why those are especially exposed. *Example: Adds "The chapter highlights biology and the brain as areas where human science is particularly thin. We don't know how memories are encoded or how reasoning works at the neural level. A superintelligence that understood those domains could construct attacks that exploit rules we don't even know exist — like optical illusions, but for memory or reasoning."*

**5** — As above, plus articulates the epistemological consequence: inability to describe the mechanism doesn't undermine the prediction. The blacksmith's confusion about the refrigerator doesn't mean cold air is impossible. *Example: Adds "This also answers 'I can't imagine a specific mechanism, so I'm not convinced.' The blacksmith who built the refrigerator couldn't explain why it produced cold air — but the cold air was real. We don't need to describe the exact attack vector for 'a superintelligence with capabilities we don't understand would defeat us' to be a valid conclusion."*
