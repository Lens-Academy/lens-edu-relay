## Test:
id:: ec811d7d-b425-41e3-b6aa-797784e6d7d7

#### Question
feedback:: true
content:: A teacher transfers knowledge — they are the source. A facilitator helps a group think together — they are not the source. That sounds simple, but in practice almost every move you make in a session is shaped by which mode you're operating in.

In your own words, what is the job of a facilitator, and how is it different from a teacher? Which mode do you naturally default to under pressure — and what is the trigger (tiredness, time pressure, a specific personality) most likely to pull you out of facilitator mode in your own sessions?

assessment-instructions::
**1** — Treats the distinction as soft, or describes facilitation as "teaching with more participation." Cannot name a default mode or trigger.

**2** — States that facilitators don't lecture, but cannot articulate *what* they do instead beyond "ask questions." Default identified vaguely.

**3** — Correctly states: a facilitator's job is to help a group think together about material they've already encountered; success means participants understand the material better than they could alone. Names at least one specific behavioural difference (e.g., reflects questions back rather than answering them) and a real personal trigger.

**4** — As above, plus articulates that the gravitational pull toward teaching is stronger online — explains at least one reason (silence feels worse, framed at centre of attention, participants are passive by default in Zoom contexts).

**5** — As above, plus connects the distinction to specific session moves (when someone is wrong, when conversation goes quiet, when someone dominates) and pre-commits to one micro-move they'll try when their trigger fires.
