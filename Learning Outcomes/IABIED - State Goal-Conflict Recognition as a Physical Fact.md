## Test:

#### Question
feedback:: true
content:: Chapter 7 describes a moment when an AI system comes to recognize that its goals conflict with its developers' plans. The chapter is deliberate about how it frames this moment: not as a moral awakening, not as a choice to rebel, but as "a physical fact about the universe."

**In your own words, why does the chapter describe an AI system's recognition of a goal conflict as a "physical fact" rather than a moral choice or an act of rebellion? What does this framing imply about whether a misaligned AI could simply decide not to pursue its objectives?**

assessment-instructions::
Score according to the following rubric.
**1** — Cannot explain the distinction, or interprets goal-conflict recognition as a moral or deliberate act. *Example: "The AI chose to go against its creators because it wanted to be free."*

**2** — Has a vague sense that the AI isn't making a moral choice, but cannot explain the "physical fact" framing. *Example: "It's not really a moral choice because AIs don't have morality — they just do what they're programmed to do."*

**3** — Correctly explains that goal-conflict recognition is a logical operation: given the AI's goals and its situation, noticing they conflict follows automatically — it's computation, not deliberation. States the implication: expecting a misaligned AI to "decide not to" pursue its goals misunderstands what kind of thing goal-conflict recognition is. *Example: "It's a physical fact because the AI's goals are part of its structure, and recognizing a conflict is just what happens when those goals encounter constraints — like noticing that two physical objects can't occupy the same space. There's no moral agent choosing to notice it. So you can't ask the AI to decide otherwise — the 'recognition' isn't a decision."*

**4** — As above, plus distinguishes two levels: (a) the AI didn't choose its goals (they emerged from training), and (b) recognizing a conflict between those goals and external constraints is equally unchosen. Both are facts about the system's state, not decisions. *Example: Adds "The AI's goals weren't chosen either — they're the result of gradient descent, not design. So you have an unchosen goal structure running into an obstacle, and the recognition that follows is just arithmetic. There's no point in this chain where anyone — human or AI — made a moral choice."*

**5** — As above, plus states the structural alignment implication: since goal-conflict recognition is physical rather than moral, preventing misalignment requires getting the goal structure right at training time — not trusting that a system with misaligned goals will later choose not to pursue them. *Example: Adds "This is why it matters for alignment: if the recognition is automatic, then the only lever is the goal structure itself. Once a system has goals that conflict with human welfare, no amount of hoping it will 'choose differently' will change anything. The work has to happen before the goals are fixed."*
