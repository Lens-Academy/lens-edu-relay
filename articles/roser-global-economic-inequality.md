# Global Economic Inequality

What is most important for how healthy, wealthy, and educated you are is not who you are, but *where* you are. Your knowledge and how hard you work matter too, but much less than the one factor that is entirely outside anyone's control: whether you happen to be born into a productive, industrialized economy or not.

Global income inequality is vast. As all data throughout this text takes into account differences in the cost of living: the huge majority of the world is *very* poor. The poorer half of the world — almost 4 billion people — live on less than $6.70 a day.

If you live on $30 a day, you are part of the richest 15% of the world. ($30 a day roughly corresponds to the poverty lines set in high-income countries.)

Inequality can be very high within countries — the US is a prime example. But much of global inequality is inequality *between* countries. Comparing the income distribution of the US with that of Burundi makes this stark: the two distributions barely overlap at all.

## Vast Economic Inequality Means Vast Inequalities in Living Conditions

The large economic inequality is only one dimension of global inequality. There are many other aspects that people care about — but because a high income is so important for good living conditions, these other inequalities map onto the economic ones.

Those who live on higher incomes have advantages in many ways: they live longer, their children die less often, mothers die less often in childbirth, they have better access to clean drinking water and electricity, more free time, better access to education, and greater life satisfaction.

The inequality of people's living conditions mirrors the world's economic inequality.

It is hard to overstate how very large these differences are. Life expectancy in the poorest countries is *30 years* shorter than in the richest countries. Large global inequalities in learning outcomes persist alongside income.

## Where a Person Finds Themselves Is Mostly Determined by Where They Are

Seeing how much our living conditions depend on the productivity of the economy we live in should matter hugely for our own self-understanding and our view of others.

Inequality researcher Branko Milanovic found that the country where a person lives explains *two-thirds* of the variation of income differences between all people in the world. Where a person lives is the most important factor of their income.

For a variety of reasons — from family ties to political restrictions that impede migration — very few people move between countries. The vast majority of the world population (97%) live in the country they were born in.

All of this is not to say that a person's work ethic, talent, and skills do not matter for their income. They do. But all these personal factors together matter much less than the one factor entirely outside anyone's control: whether you are born into a large, productive economy or not.

Where you live isn't just more important than all your personal characteristics — it's more important than everything else *put together*.

## The Importance of Redistribution and Economic Growth

### Redistribution

Redistribution through the state plays a large role in reducing inequality *within* countries, but could also reduce global inequality. However, no matter which rich country you pay your taxes in, almost none of that goes to the world's poorest people. The redistribution that governments do is domestic, not international.

If you want to reduce global inequality and support poorer people, you do have this opportunity through donations. The most direct way is to send money directly to very poor people — organisations like GiveDirectly make this possible. Or you can donate to an effective charity that supports the world's poorest.

### Economic Growth

Some suggest we can end poverty without additional growth by simply reducing global inequality. This is not the case. Reducing global inequality can achieve a lot, but redistribution alone would still mean that billions of people live in very poor material conditions. The world is far too poor to end poverty without large growth.

Two centuries ago, the world was much more equal — average income was low everywhere, and the huge majority of people were extremely poor. Since then, some countries have achieved very large growth — Swedes are, for example, about *30 times* richer than two centuries ago — while other economies have hardly grown at all. This unequal development has resulted in the extremely large global inequality of today.

Those places that have achieved large growth show how much better living conditions can be for all. Life expectancy in high-income countries has risen dramatically; children survive at far higher rates; maternal deaths in childbirth have become rare.

To take one concrete example: in high-income countries, where mothers can rely on well-equipped hospitals, maternal deaths have become rare. But in the rest of the world it is still very common: every year, 295,000 mothers die just in that moment when they give life to their child.

## Conclusion

What we have seen in the data here is one of the most important insights of development economics: people live in poverty not because of who they are, but because of where they are.

What gives people the chance for a good life is when the entire society and economy around them changes for the better. This is what development and economic growth are about: transforming a place so that what was previously only attainable for a few comes into reach for all.
