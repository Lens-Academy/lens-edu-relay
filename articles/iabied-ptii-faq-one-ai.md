## In part because it's realistic

AlphaGo was essentially unmatched when released. ChatGPT occupied a similar position upon launch.

While industry analysts occasionally note that competitors weren't significantly far behind, small differences between comparable systems can produce dramatically divergent outcomes. A nuclear chain reaction yielding 0.98 neutrons per neutron differs minimally from one producing 1.02 neutrons per neutron—yet one dissipates while the other explodes. Chimpanzee and human brains share substantial similarities, yet their planetary impact differs enormously.

In actual AI development, OpenAI released a functional chatbot before competitors. Other organizations worked on somewhat comparable systems and eventually caught up, but one system crossed a qualitative threshold first.

There appears to be an important boundary—possibly multiple boundaries—between contemporary AI systems and artificial intelligences whose reasoning "integrates" sufficiently to develop novel technologies independently.

The argument doesn't require such a gap for machines. We could have written differently. But evidence suggests such a gap probably exists.

## In part because it's easier to write

Perhaps no qualitative gap separates today's language models from superintelligence. Perhaps multiple AI firms will incrementally advance their systems in parallel. Perhaps no capability constellation exists permitting one AI to surge ahead, as humans surpassed other animals. Possibly true, but a story structured this way would require unnecessary details about AI factions and internal politics—likely distracting and somewhat irrelevant to later narrative stages. Whether one AI or coordinated multiple AIs execute empowerment strategies fundamentally matters less than the outcome itself.
