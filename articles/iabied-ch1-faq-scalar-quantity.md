## Is "intelligence" a simple scalar quantity?

**No. But there are levels AI hasn't reached.**

The authors acknowledge the critique that intelligence isn't one-dimensional. They note that "intelligence isn't a simple scalar quantity" and that simply adding more computing power doesn't always yield proportional intelligence gains. Capabilities can hit bottlenecks and plateaus.

However, they argue this complexity doesn't prevent AI from vastly surpassing humans. Human intelligence has significant limitations, yet it enabled moon landings. Similarly, humans dramatically outperform chimpanzees despite both having comparable brain anatomy.

The key distinction: "Biological brains have limitations that aren't present in AI." An artificial superintelligence could have its own constraints while still achieving a qualitative gap over humanity—the same pattern visible between humans and other primates.

The authors use this analogy: animal intelligence isn't a scalar measure, yet humans clearly exceed chimpanzees. If incremental improvements in brain size and structure created that gap, similar progression in AI could produce comparable divergence.

They emphasize that acknowledging intelligence's complexity doesn't undermine concerns about superhuman AI development. The existence of limits and complications is compatible with crossing critical capability thresholds that separate species in fundamental ways.++}
