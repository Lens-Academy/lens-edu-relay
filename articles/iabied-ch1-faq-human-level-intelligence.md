## Is "human-level intelligence" a meaningful concept?

**Yes, in many cases.**

Humans have constructed advanced technological civilizations while chimpanzees have not, suggesting meaningful distinction exists. Though chimpanzees communicate and use tools impressively, there remains practical value in referencing human capability as a benchmark.

### The Civilization Threshold

The text emphasizes a critical distinction: humans crossed a threshold enabling civilization-building, iron-smelting, and space exploration—capabilities completely absent in other primates. This represents "some kind of dam" releasing vast intelligence rather than gradual improvement.

### Limitations of the Concept

If encountering alien civilizations equally technologically advanced, one might excel at chess while struggling with mathematics. Such differences complicate claims about "human-level" superiority across all domains.

### Practical Utility Despite Imprecision

The authors argue precision definitions aren't prerequisite for meaningful discussion. We cannot perfectly define "intelligence" yet can observe its real-world effects—just as fire's chemistry remained unknown before controlled use.

### The Real Threshold

Rather than debating exact definitions, the concept captures "whatever quality makes humans capable of building and maintaining a technological civilization" versus what chimpanzees cannot achieve.

The framework distinguishes between "superhuman AI" (exceeding top human performance across wide task ranges) and "superintelligent AI" (vastly outstripping human capability entirely). These distinctions matter more than semantic arguments about "general intelligence."
