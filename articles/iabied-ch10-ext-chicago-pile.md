# The Tale of Chicago Pile-1

In 1942, Chicago Pile-1 was built under Enrico Fermi's direction beneath Stagg Field at the University of Chicago. It comprised 45,000 graphite blocks, 4.9 tonnes of uranium metal, and 41 tonnes of uranium dioxide — the first sustained nuclear chain reaction.

Safety standards were minimal by modern measures. General Groves had requested the experiment occur outside Chicago, but construction delays prevented this. Arthur Compton avoided seeking university approval, reasoning "the president would have had to say no."

High school dropouts stacked bricks for wages before military service. The uranium sat in a rubber cube rather than metal vessel, with no containment building. James Conant reportedly "turned white" upon learning these details.

Yet catastrophe didn't occur. Fermi possessed deep theoretical understanding of exponential chain reactions. He recognized the critical distinction between neutron multiplication factors below and above 100 percent. Below that threshold, an inert pile results. Above it, radioactivity escalates exponentially.

On December 2, 1942, Fermi predicted the control rod withdrawal would cause radioactivity to "climb and continue to climb." The reaction doubled every two minutes for twenty-eight minutes — a 16,000-fold increase — exactly as anticipated.

Fermi understood the narrow margin between functional reactors and explosions: roughly 0.65 percent separates industrial power generation from detonation. He stopped at criticality, refusing to proceed further.

The core lesson transcends nuclear physics: understanding prevented disaster. Stereotypical safety theater — inspectors, regulations, credentials — would have proven insufficient without theoretical knowledge. Fermi's predictive framework, grounded in physics principles, enabled safe operation where bureaucratic oversight alone would fail.

This historical example illuminates contemporary challenges in artificial superintelligence development, where theoretical understanding remains inadequate for the stakes involved.
