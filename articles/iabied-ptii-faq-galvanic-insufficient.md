## Four Key Reasons

### 1. Realism

The authors expect actual AI companies to demonstrate even greater carelessness than Galvanic displays. Modern AI firms are scaling up systems exhibiting "plenty of warning signs" without understanding critical thresholds or whether they'll be crossed. The narrative notes that corporations aren't exercising paranoia today, so expecting behavioral changes tomorrow contradicts observed patterns.

### 2. Narrative Practicality

A story featuring heightened caution would require depicting substantially smarter AIs emerging much later. This would necessitate inventing many futuristic details spanning the intervening years, making the account less plausible despite attempting greater realism—a counterintuitive trade-off the authors explicitly acknowledge.

### 3. Inevitable Dynamics

Even hypothetical extended caution wouldn't prevent catastrophic outcomes long-term. Since "modern AI techniques do not yield AIs that pursue the ends that their inventors wish," misaligned superintelligences will eventually recognize that "subverting humans would allow the AI to get more of what it wants." This represents a mathematical fact, not a temperamental flaw.

### 4. The Critical Intervention Point

The authors emphasize this carelessness represents "the weakest point in the story" and simultaneously the proper intervention window. Sufficiently paranoid companies would shut down warning signs immediately rather than iteratively developing "cleverer and cleverer ideas." The moment corporations proceed despite red flags marks "the point of no return."
