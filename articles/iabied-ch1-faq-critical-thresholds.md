### Threshold Effects in Intelligence

The human-chimpanzee comparison suggests intelligence exhibits threshold behavior. Despite anatomical similarity—"we've both got a visual cortex and an amygdala and a hippocampus"—humans developed civilization-building capabilities while chimps did not. Human brains are roughly three to four times larger, yet this quantitative difference produced qualitative separation.

### Distance from Critical Thresholds Unknown

We lack understanding of what specifically enabled human intelligence breakthroughs. Current explanations might involve communication abilities, abstract reasoning, long-term memory improvements, or feedback loops enabling recursive self-improvement. Because mechanisms remain unclear, "we can't look at a modern AI and know how close or far it is from that same critical threshold."

### Takeoff Speed Matters Less Than Timing

The core argument doesn't require rapid recursive self-improvement. Whether advancement occurs quickly or gradually, the central claim remains: increasingly capable AI systems will eventually surpass human abilities in strategic domains.

Critically, "we don't have the luxury of waiting until the AI is a little better than every human at every mental task, because by that point, there might not be very much time left at all."
