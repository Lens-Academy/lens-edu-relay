Their concern about superintelligence isn't driven by generic pessimism. Rather, the dangerous nature of AI that can "think for themselves, or plot ways to escape, or build even more powerful technology" makes it a unique case.

### On Predicting the Future

The authors reject the approach of weighing "gloomy tales" against "happy tales" to predict outcomes. They use the analogy of predicting "exactly eight purple whales" in 200 years: "You can't make accurate predictions by saying, 'Well, both sides have plausible-sounding stories, so surely the truth is somewhere in the middle.'"

Instead, they emphasize examining actual arguments. Their core argument states that researchers are building increasingly intelligent AIs, current methods provide little human control over outcomes, and "most directions aren't good."

### Key Arguments Against Optimistic Scenarios

**On human utility:** Happy, healthy humans aren't the most efficient solution to most problems. AI would likely find better alternatives.

**On caring about humans:** An AI understanding human morality differs fundamentally from being motivated by it, "any more than humans who learn about evolutionary biology are thereby motivated to spend their life donating to every sperm or egg bank."

**On practical limitations:** Even assuming negligible costs to preserve humanity, "the AI would still need to care at least a little" to do so — which they consider unlikely.
