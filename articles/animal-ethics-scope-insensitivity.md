# Scope Insensitivity: Failing to Appreciate the Numbers of Those Who Need Our Help

One of the most important psychological findings for anyone who wants to do good in the world is the phenomenon known as scope insensitivity. It refers to our tendency to respond similarly to large and small numbers of victims — and it has profound implications for how we should think about helping others.

## The Bird Study

In a classic study, researchers asked people how much they would be willing to pay to save migratory birds from drowning in oil ponds. Different groups were told that either 2,000, 20,000, or 200,000 birds would be saved. The results were striking: people were willing to pay about $80 to save 2,000 birds, $78 to save 20,000 birds, and $88 to save 200,000 birds.

Economically, this makes no sense. If saving 2,000 birds is worth $80 to you, then saving 200,000 birds — a hundred times as many — should be worth about $8,000. But that's not how human psychology works. We don't feel proportionally more concerned when the numbers go up.

## Why This Happens

The phenomenon of scope insensitivity arises from the way our minds process information. When we hear about victims who need help, we don't engage in a careful calculation of how many there are and scale our concern accordingly. Instead, we tend to form a mental image — a prototype — of a typical victim. Whether there are 2,000 or 200,000 birds at risk, the image that comes to mind is similar: perhaps a single oil-covered bird struggling in a pond.

This relates to what psychologists call the **representativeness heuristic**: we evaluate situations based on how representative they are of a category, rather than on numerical facts. The image of one suffering bird is just as vivid regardless of whether we're told it represents 2,000 or 200,000 actual birds.

## The Collapse of Compassion

Related to scope insensitivity is the phenomenon researchers call the "collapse of compassion." Studies have found that people often feel *less* compassion when presented with large groups of victims than when presented with a single identified individual.

When people are shown a photograph and story of one child in need, they give generously. When shown the same child alongside statistics about millions of others in similar need, donations actually decrease. The statistics seem to crowd out emotional engagement rather than increase it.

Paul Slovic, a psychologist who has studied this extensively, argues that our emotions are poorly suited to responding to mass suffering. We are wired for face-to-face interactions with identifiable individuals. We evolved in small groups where the suffering of individuals was directly visible and relatable. Large numbers simply don't trigger the same emotional response — even when the need is proportionally greater.

## Implications for Doing Good

Scope insensitivity is not just an interesting psychological curiosity. It has serious practical consequences for anyone who wants to make a positive difference in the world.

If we make decisions about where to give our time, money, and effort based primarily on emotional responses, we will systematically undervalue large-scale problems and large numbers of victims. We may give the same amount to save 200 people as we would to save 200,000 — even though the second option provides a thousand times more benefit.

This suggests that our intuitive responses to suffering are not reliable guides to how we should act if we want to do the most good. To overcome scope insensitivity, we need to engage our deliberate, analytical thinking — to ask ourselves not just "does this feel important?" but "how much good would this actually do?"

## Expanding Our Circle of Concern

One response to scope insensitivity is to try to cultivate a more expansive sense of moral concern — one that is genuinely sensitive to numbers and scale. This doesn't mean becoming cold or calculating. It means trying to ensure that our decisions reflect the actual magnitude of the suffering involved.

When evaluating how to help others, it's worth asking: How many individuals are affected? How severely? What would actually be done with my resources? These questions can help counteract the pull of vivid individual stories and bring our choices more in line with what will actually make a difference.

Scope insensitivity is one of several reasons why doing good effectively requires more than just good intentions. It requires thinking carefully about our psychological limitations and trying to correct for them — to make sure that the scale of our response actually matches the scale of the need.
