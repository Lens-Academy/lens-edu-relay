## Isn't there something special about humans that mere machines could never emulate?

**It seems unlikely, and not especially relevant.**

### The Case Against Human Uniqueness

Human brains and bodies consist of physical components that can be studied and understood. While significant gaps remain in our neuroscientific knowledge, this doesn't suggest that unexplained brain functions operate through magic. Rather, brains represent enormously complex machines. The human brain contains hundreds of trillions of synapses, and we continue working toward understanding high-level principles governing intelligence.

Intelligence itself comprises discrete pieces—algorithms and individual computations that brains naturally perform, even without full scientific comprehension of their mechanisms.

### Different Paths to the Same Goal

Even if certain biological reasoning aspects proved difficult to implement in machines, this wouldn't preclude AI from surpassing humanity. AIs could accomplish equivalent work through entirely different methods, much like how Deep Blue discovered winning chess moves through mechanisms vastly different from Garry Kasparov's approach. What ultimately matters isn't whether machines possess all uniquely human features, but whether they become capable of predicting and steering the world.

### Why This Distinction Matters Less

The central concern involves AI capabilities for prediction and environmental manipulation, not whether machines possess consciousness, a "vital spark," or other qualities humans consider special. Subsequent chapters explore how modern AIs develop through growth rather than design, how this developmental process creates increasingly capable systems with strong goal-orientation, and how those goals diverge from human intentions. These dynamics suffice to create existential risks regardless of whether AIs possess consciousness or other attributes marking them as truly sentient.

Human exceptionalism debates ultimately distract from the practical concerns surrounding superintelligent systems.
