## More on Intelligence as Prediction and Steering

The text uses an analogy comparing intelligence to engines: just as various mechanisms (rocket engines, electric motors, hamster wheels) convert energy into motion through different means, different systems can exhibit intelligence through "prediction" and "steering."

### Prediction: The Objective Component

"It's relatively straightforward to check how good someone is at prediction" when outcomes can be measured against reality. The authors explain that prediction quality can be scored objectively using logarithmic probability measures. When multiple minds work from identical information and improve their predictive abilities, they converge toward similar conclusions—there's "only one really good way to evaluate how well they're doing."

This mathematical foundation means that accurate predictions don't vary arbitrarily between capable systems.

### Steering: The Free Parameter

Steering (planning, decision-making, design) operates differently. Two equally skilled military generals may employ similar tactical prowess while pursuing entirely opposite objectives. The von Neumann-Morgenstern utility theorem suggests that "any entity pursuing some outcomes over others" requires both accurate beliefs and a utility function—"a completely free parameter."

The critical distinction: "Accuracy converges; steering does not."

### Practical Implications

Making systems "more generically capable" improves prediction automatically. However, "its steering doesn't automatically get more aimed at the destination humans think is good—because accuracy is objective, whereas 'goodness' is a steering target."

The authors illustrate how even "superhumanly good prediction" requires steering work—designing experiments, developing theories, organizing thoughts strategically. Pure prediction modules cannot be divorced from goal-directed behavior in functional systems.

### Intelligence Across Different Domains

Stock markets perform prediction in narrow domains; chess AI (Stockfish) performs steering toward checkmate. These disparate systems share no common internal architecture yet both accomplish intelligence's essential work. The distinction matters: there is no universally "correct" steering destination, only correct predictions about reality.++}
