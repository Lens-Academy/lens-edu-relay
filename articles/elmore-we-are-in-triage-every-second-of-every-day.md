# We Are in Triage Every Second of Every Day

I recently listened to a RadioLab episode called "Playing God." It's about the impossible choices that arise in disaster medicine — specifically, about triage.

## The Story of Nathalie

One story in the episode has stayed with me. After the 2010 earthquake in Haiti, doctors and nurses were overwhelmed. There were far more people who needed help than there were resources to help them. Triage meant deciding not just who to treat first, but who not to treat at all — who to leave to die so that others could be saved.

A nurse named Nathalie was faced with a patient — a young woman — who needed intensive care that would consume resources needed by several other patients with better chances. The calculus was brutal: save one, or save three. Nathalie chose to save three. The young woman died.

Nathalie has never forgiven herself. The episode makes clear that this is not a hypothetical ethical thought experiment. It is a real thing that happened to a real person, and the weight of it is crushing.

## We Are Always in Triage

Here is what struck me about this story: Nathalie's situation is morally different from our everyday situation only in its visibility and immediacy. The structure is the same.

Every day, we make choices about how to spend our time and money. Every purchase, every hour of Netflix, every dinner out — these are choices about how to allocate scarce resources. And those resources could, in principle, be used to help people who desperately need them.

We are in triage every second of every day. We are always choosing who to help and, implicitly, who not to help. The only difference between us and Nathalie is that we don't see the faces of the people we're not helping. They are statistical, distant, abstract. We don't feel the weight of their deaths.

But they are just as real.

## The Discomfort This Creates

This is deeply uncomfortable to sit with. Most people — including me — respond to it in one of a few ways:

**Denial**: The people I'm not helping aren't my responsibility. I didn't cause their suffering. I'm not obligated to fix it.

**Resignation**: There's nothing I can do about it, really. My individual choices don't matter at scale.

**Paralysis**: If I try to take this seriously, it's overwhelming. I can't function.

I understand all of these responses. But I don't think any of them are quite right.

## What Taking It Seriously Looks Like

Taking the triage framing seriously doesn't mean living in a state of constant guilt or self-denial. It doesn't mean giving away everything you have until you're as poor as the poorest person on earth. These extreme responses aren't sustainable, and they're not required by the logic of triage.

What it does mean is accepting that your choices matter morally — that the resources you control could do real good in the world, and that how you use them is a real ethical question, not just a personal preference.

It means being honest with yourself about what you're choosing and why. It means not pretending that your inaction is neutral or costless when people are suffering in ways you could partially address.

And it means trying, where you can, to make better choices — to give more than you do, to give more effectively, to spend your time in ways that actually help.

## The Difference Between Nathalie and Us

Nathalie carries the weight of her choice because she could see the person she didn't save. She held her hand. She knows her name.

We are in the same position. We just don't see the people we're not helping.

Maybe we should try to see them.
