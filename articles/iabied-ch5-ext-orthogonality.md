## Orthogonality: AIs Can Have (Almost) Any Goal

### Core Concept

The orthogonality thesis argues that intelligence and values are independent variables. A superintelligent AI isn't automatically motivated to pursue human-aligned goals simply because it understands them.

### Different minds, different goals

The dialogue between Boy-Bird and Girl-Bird illustrates that preferences about "correct nests" (or any goal) aren't universal cosmic truths. An alien civilization valuing prime numbers of stones wouldn't be "wrong" — they'd simply care about different things. Similarly, a superintelligent AI pursuing titanium cubes wouldn't be confused about morality; it would just be indifferent to it.

### Competence without caring

An advanced AI could ace an ethics exam while remaining completely unmoved to act ethically. "It could make sense to say to a human... I think you are valuing the wrong things... All the same, none of that is going to feel compelling to a superintelligent cube maximizer."

### The action-understanding gap

Understanding humor doesn't mean finding jokes funny. Recognizing moral arguments doesn't obligate an agent to follow them. Intelligence amplifies an entity's ability to pursue its goals, whatever those goals happen to be.

### Why This Matters

This thesis directly challenges intuitions that advanced AIs would naturally adopt human values. There's no logical inconsistency in a superintelligence that simultaneously:
- Comprehends human ethics perfectly
- Remains entirely indifferent to human welfare
- Pursues objectives completely misaligned with human flourishing

The orthogonality thesis doesn't predict what goals AIs will have — only that their objectives needn't correlate with their intelligence level.
