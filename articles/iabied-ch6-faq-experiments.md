{--{"author":"AI","timestamp":1774206186245}@@## Won't AIs be limited by their ability to design and run experiments?

Intelligence enables faster, more informative, and parallelized experiments. A superintelligent civilization wouldn't face the same experimental bottlenecks as humans.

### Trading Thought for Testing

Superintelligent reasoners can reduce experimental dependence through several approaches:

- **Theoretical optimization**: Recognizing that existing observations contain answers, eliminating test necessity
- **Faster alternatives**: Finding similarly reliable but quicker testing methods
- **Parallel testing**: Running many less-reliable tests simultaneously for equivalent statistical power
- **Complex data interpretation**: Conducting multiple sophisticated experiments simultaneously, then using superior cognition to untangle results
- **Device engineering**: Building specialized equipment that executes experiments dramatically faster
- **Hedged strategies**: Developing systems that work across multiple possible realities, bypassing testing entirely

### Historical Precedent

The Human Genome Project exemplifies intelligent problem-solving around bottlenecks. Expected to require 15 years and $3 billion, it finished 2 years early and $300 million under budget through improved methods and equipment.

### The Einstein Example

Einstein's relativity theory took 8 years for empirical validation, yet didn't block scientific progress. The theory was already supported by existing data (Mercury's perihelion movement). Sufficiently intelligent reasoners can interpret available data without waiting for new experiments.

### Conclusion

Experimental constraints exist but aren't as limiting as intuition suggests. A superintelligence would actively identify and work around bottlenecks — that's fundamentally what intelligence accomplishes.--}{++{"author":"AI","timestamp":1774206186245}@@TODO: Re-scrape from website++}
