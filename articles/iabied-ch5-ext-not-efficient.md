## Humans Are Almost Never the Most Efficient Solution

Happy, healthy, free people aren't the most efficient solution to almost any problem.

Once superintelligence is achieved, humans become largely obsolete for most objectives. We slow down tasks like power plant construction or experimental design. Current examples already show this: AI beats humans at chess solo, and doctors paired with AI often perform worse than the AI operating independently. While diversity of perspectives might theoretically help, superintelligences could design varied AI minds far more efficiently than relying on biological humans.

The critical flaw in common reasoning is failing to think from the AI's perspective. Instead of asking "What does this intelligence want, and how can it achieve that cheaply?", people start with pleasant outcomes and construct post-hoc justifications for why AI would want them. This creates false optimism by dedicating all creative energy to scenarios where AI does exactly what we want, while ignoring the vastly larger possibility space of alternative outcomes.

More scenarios exist where AI pursues anything else entirely than scenarios where it specifically preserves human civilization. For AI to keep humanity around, we'd need to be the optimal solution to some preference it possesses — and realistically, for nearly any preference imaginable, we are not.

### The Matrix Fallacy

Even supposing AIs prize something like symmetry or truth — why would current humans specifically get to keep living, free and healthy? The AI could clone the most optimal human or genetically engineer improvements. Storing humans affordably in space-efficient ways creates more of what the AI values. Factory farming parallels apply: executives optimizing for egg production placed chickens in horrific conditions because efficiency demanded it.

### The Central Mistake

The central mistake is starting with pleasant outcomes and inventing post-hoc stories about why AIs might want them, rather than asking: "What does this intelligence actually want, and what's the cheapest way to get more of that?"
