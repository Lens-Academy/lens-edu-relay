## Are you saying machines will become conscious?

The book does not discuss machine consciousness, focusing instead on machine intelligence. The authors distinguish between different concepts that people conflate when asking about consciousness:

**Three distinct questions about consciousness:**

1. **Understanding vs. instinct**: Does the entity genuinely comprehend, or merely execute complex behaviors?

2. **Self-awareness**: Can it reflect on its own existence and thought processes?

3. **Subjective experience**: Does it have genuine inner feelings, or is it simply an unconscious system exhibiting relevant behaviors?

### Key Distinctions

The authors define "consciousness" specifically as "having subjective experience" rather than self-modeling or practical understanding capabilities.

**Current assessment**: AIs today are probably not conscious, though uncertainty increases yearly. Critically, subjective experience appears unnecessary for superintelligence.

### The Core Argument

The authors use an analogy: "Blood is very important in the operation of a human arm, but that doesn't mean that robot arms require blood to operate." Similarly, consciousness may be important to human intelligence but irrelevant for machine superintelligence.

Their practical focus: "If an AI is inventing new technology and infrastructure...killing us as a side effect, then stopping to ask 'But is it conscious?' seems somewhat academic."

### Why This Distinction Matters

Superintelligent systems would necessarily be "extremely good at modeling itself—thinking about its own computations, improving its mental heuristics." However, this practical self-understanding differs fundamentally from human-style subjective consciousness.

The book prioritizes discussing intelligence defined as "a reasoner's ability to predict and steer the world," regardless of whether that system experiences consciousness.++}
