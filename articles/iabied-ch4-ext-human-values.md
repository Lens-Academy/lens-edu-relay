## Human Values Are Contingent

### The Glorious Accident of Kindness

When observing someone drop a rock on their toe and wincing in response, we experience a phenomenon rooted in our evolutionary history. Early proto-humans developed mental models to predict other hominids' thoughts and experiences — useful for tribal politics and determining allies from threats.

The challenge was that brains are complicated. However, ancestral primates possessed an advantage: their own brains resembled those around them. This enabled using one's brain as a template to predict others' thinking patterns.

Proto-humans evolved mental machinery for simulating another person's perspective: adopting their preferences, knowledge, and thought patterns while assuming similar brain architecture. This empathy mechanism isn't perfectly isolated from our feelings. When witnessing someone in pain, we instinctively wince — a neurological accident of mental architecture that deserves recognition as foundational to human values.

Ancestral primates likely developed self-modeling using identical machinery. This capacity for empathy — understanding others' suffering — represents something not inevitable in minds generally, yet became central to human identity and ethics.

### The Evolutionary Shortcut Problem

"Humanity's higher-minded ideals are contingent on the particulars of our ancient primate history" and our ancestral social environment. Friendship echoes tribal alliance needs. Romantic love reflects sexually dimorphic mating patterns. Even curiosity lacks the universal inevitability initially assumed.

These psychological traits developed through shortcuts available at specific brain sophistication levels. Early humans solved complex social problems through heuristics rather than explicit reasoning — an approach that wouldn't work for minds following different evolutionary paths.

### Potential Alien Differences

Alien species might develop entirely different value systems. Consider species with eusocial genetics like Earth's insects, where workers share more genetic material with queens than humans share within ant-colony-sized groups. Such aliens might lack the general allyship sense that benefited ancestral hominids, instead developing kinship strictly through genetic relatedness calculations.

For AIs without evolutionary history in social genetic propagation, "the robot arm analogy applies in force." Biological arms need soft skin and blood because evolution built them that way — not because these features are logically necessary. Robot arms work fine without blood through alternative designs. Similarly, AI operates through fundamentally different mechanisms than humans. Where challenges overlap, multiple solution pathways exist.

### Human Culture Influenced the Development of Human Values

Beyond biological contingency, human cultural arguments shaped moral development. Christianity's universalism — belief that God created all humans equally — may have played a crucial role in slavery's decline. This universalism itself tied to Christianity's cultural survival through missionary conversion efforts.

"It would be nice to believe humanity could have invented universalism without specific religious beliefs, but that doesn't seem historically accurate." Human moral development proved more fragile than hoped. Chimpanzees show little universalism, nor do early human societies. Sustaining universalism for centuries without deeply believed universalist religion remains untested.

### The Relevance to AI Development

Culture's important role in human values doesn't support raising AIs like children expecting human-like development. Our culture influenced humans because of how it interacted with our specific brain makeup. Different species would respond differently to historical events, compounding divergence.

Individual humans exhibit enormous value diversity — antithetical to engineering goals demanding reliable, predictable results. Engineers building superintelligence should want dependable outcomes: AIs following specific development paths shouldn't cause extinction, and similar development methods should produce consistent outputs despite varying inputs.

The contingency of human biology, history, and moral perspectives suggests that achieving robust AI alignment is genuinely difficult. Engineering solutions exist, but unlimited retries aren't available. Current progress remains insufficient.

"Many different lines of evidence point at it being genuinely difficult to get AIs to robustly want the right things."
