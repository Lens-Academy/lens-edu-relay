# Global Health

Good health is fundamental for a high quality of life, as it influences our ability to enjoy life and participate in daily activities.

This overview of global health emphasises data on health outcomes — such as disease and death — and the effects of healthcare investments.

## Life Expectancy

Life expectancy is a primary indicator of population health — formally, the "period life expectancy at birth," summarising death rates across all age groups in one particular year.

Historical trends show significant increases in global life expectancy over the last two centuries, reflecting improvements around the world.

**Long-run trends:** In the United Kingdom, life expectancy before 1800 was very low, but has since doubled. Similarly remarkable improvements took place in other European countries. In the early 1900s, life expectancy in both India and South Korea was less than 25 years — a century later, life expectancy in India had more than doubled, and in South Korea more than tripled.

**Current disparities:** In 2021, the life expectancy in several sub-Saharan African countries is less than 60 years, compared to more than 80 years in countries such as Japan.

**Within-country inequality:** Lifespan inequality — measured with a Gini coefficient comparing the spread of ages at death — has declined substantially over time in many countries. Wealthier nations generally exhibit lower levels of lifespan inequality.

Life expectancy continues to rise in many countries because death rates have continued to fall across all age groups, not just among children.

## Child Mortality

Child mortality measures the share of newborns who would not survive to their fifth birthday, given death rates at young ages in a population.

Historically, in pre-modern societies, child mortality rates ranged between 30% and 50%. In richer countries today, child mortality rates have dropped to around 0.5%.

Despite this progress, child mortality rates in low-income countries are substantially higher than those in high-income countries — on average, more than ten times higher in 2021.

**What is killing children?** Malaria and HIV/AIDS deaths rose through the 1990s, then declined from 2005 onwards as intervention programmes scaled up.

## Maternal Mortality

Maternal mortality refers to deaths of women during pregnancy, or within 42 days of ending the pregnancy — typically expressed per 100,000 live births.

A century ago, maternal mortality rates ranged between 500 and 1,000 deaths per 100,000 live births — meaning that for every 100 to 200 births, one resulted in the death of the mother.

Today, these rates have decreased to around 10 per 100,000 live births in high-income countries.

In contrast, the rate in several countries in sub-Saharan Africa in 2017 exceeded 500 maternal deaths per 100,000 live births — more than 60 times the rate in the European Union.

Every year, 295,000 mothers die in the moment they are giving life to their child. The vast majority of these deaths could be prevented with access to the healthcare available in high-income countries.

## Burden of Disease

The burden of disease accounts for both the mortality and the morbidity of disease — not just deaths, but also illness and disability.

The most common measure is Disability Adjusted Life Years (DALYs): one DALY represents one lost year of healthy life, either through premature death or through disease or disability.

In 2019, in some regions with the best health, the rate of DALYs was under 20,000 per 100,000 people. In contrast, in the worst-off regions in Africa, the rate exceeded 60,000 — more than three times higher.

## Infectious Diseases

Major infectious diseases including malaria, tuberculosis, HIV/AIDS, and diarrheal diseases continue to cause significant harm, particularly in low-income countries.

Progress has been made: deaths from HIV/AIDS declined substantially from their peak in the early 2000s following the expansion of antiretroviral therapy. TB detection and treatment have improved. Vaccination coverage has risen dramatically, particularly in Africa.

But substantial challenges remain. In many countries, access to essential medicines is limited, particularly in the public sector. When medicines are unavailable publicly, people turn to private providers — where costs are often prohibitive for the poorest.

## Health Returns to Healthcare Investment

A growing body of research highlights the effectiveness of healthcare investments in improving health outcomes.

**Healthcare and life expectancy:** Countries with higher healthcare spending per capita generally exhibit longer life expectancy. As countries spend more on health, life expectancy increases. However, the benefits are subject to diminishing returns — countries with lower initial spending can substantially improve life expectancy through relatively modest increases in healthcare investment.

**Healthcare and child mortality:** As healthcare spending increases, child mortality rates decline. Despite enormous gaps in spending — the US spends more on health in three days than a person in the Central African Republic does in a year — the relationship between increased spending and declining child mortality is surprisingly consistent across countries.

**Healthcare coverage:** The coverage of essential health services — vaccination, TB treatment, HIV treatment, pregnancy care, sanitation, access to medicines — has generally increased since 2000. Programs such as The Global Fund and PEPFAR have driven rapid scale-up in many countries. European countries expanded healthcare coverage between 1920 and 1980; China, Rwanda, and Vietnam achieved near-universal coverage within just a decade in the 21st century, demonstrating that significant improvements can be achieved quickly even from low starting points.

## Conclusion

The data on global health tells a story of remarkable progress and persistent challenge.

Globally, people live far longer and healthier lives than they did a century ago. Diseases that once killed millions have been controlled or eliminated. Vaccination has become widespread. Maternal mortality has plummeted in countries that achieved access to modern healthcare.

Yet enormous inequalities persist. The gap in life expectancy between the richest and poorest countries is 30 years. A child born in a low-income country is more than ten times more likely to die before age five than a child born in a high-income country.

This is not inevitable. The historical trajectory shows that health improvements can happen quickly when resources, institutions, and evidence-based interventions come together. The question is how to accelerate that process for the billions still waiting.
