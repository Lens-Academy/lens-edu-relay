# Introducing LEEP: Lead Exposure Elimination Project

We are excited to announce the launch of the Lead Exposure Elimination Project ([LEEP](https://leadelimination.org/)), a new EA organisation incubated by Charity Entrepreneurship. Our mission is to reduce lead poisoning, which causes significant disease burden worldwide. We aim to achieve this by advocating for lead paint regulation in countries with large and growing burdens of lead poisoning from paint.

## The Problem

Others in the effective altruism community have already identified that working on lead poisoning could be a high-impact opportunity. Through the Importance, Tractability, Neglectedness framework, we unpack the reasoning for prioritising lead exposure interventions, and for our approach of advocating for the introduction of lead paint laws.

### Importance

Lead poisoning has substantial health and economic costs, and lead paint is a primary contributor.

Even low-level lead exposure can lead to mental disability and IQ loss, as well as increased rates of mental illness and psychopathology, and significantly reduced lifetime earnings capacity. Lead also has effects on behaviour and criminal tendencies; in particular, it has a large impact on the prevalence of violent crime. In adults, lifetime lead exposure is an important risk factor for renal disease and cardiovascular disease, including hypertension and coronary artery disease.

Lead poisoning primarily affects children, and does so at a massive scale. UNICEF reports that **815 million children** have blood lead levels above 5 µg/dL — a sufficient level for neurodevelopmental effects and reduced IQ. The vast majority live in low- and middle-income countries. Put another way: **one in three children** are currently affected by lead poisoning to some degree.

In addition to disability, lead poisoning causes **1 million deaths per year** and accounts for **22 million DALYs** (disability-adjusted life years) every year — approximately 1% of the global disease burden.

In terms of lost earnings, lead poisoning impacts the world economy at approximately **$1 trillion per year** — a loss of 1.2% of world GDP. In low- and middle-income countries, these losses can amount to as much as 5–8% of GDP, suggesting that lead exposure can be a significant barrier to economic development and poverty reduction.

### Neglectedness

While all countries except one have banned leaded petrol, **61% of countries have no lead paint regulations whatsoever**. In many of these primarily low- and middle-income countries, the burden of disease from lead poisoning remains significant.

While some organisations — including IPEN, ToxicsLink, and Pure Earth — are working on this issue, many countries with significant lead burdens remain neglected. LEEP aims to fill this gap.

### Tractability

This is the most uncertain aspect, given the uncertainty around the success of policy change interventions. However, several factors support tractability:

- Experts suggest lead paint may be the most tractable source of exposure to address — easier to regulate than other sources including batteries, mining, foodstuffs, pipes, and cookware.
- There is broad agreement that legislation is the most effective way to reduce exposure to lead paint.
- Switching to unleaded paints is technically and economically viable for manufacturers.
- NGOs have already been successful in introducing new lead paint laws in 21 low- and middle-income countries, demonstrating a precedent for feasibility.
- Lead paint regulation faces virtually no significant opposition — there is no major lead paint lobby, and in some cases the paint industry has supported the introduction of regulation.
- Every dollar spent on lead paint hazard control is estimated to return $17–$221 in benefits.
- Lead paint is typically a non-partisan issue, making political opposition less likely.

## Our Plan

Our first priority is country selection — ensuring we target tractable, high-burden, and neglected countries. We have identified Malawi as the most promising country on this basis.

Over our first two months, we will:
1. Test the levels of lead in new paints on the market in Malawi
2. Build relationships with stakeholders and decision-makers

Depending on findings and progress, we will either pilot our advocacy campaign in Malawi to introduce lead paint regulation, or pivot to another promising country.

We plan to advocate for evidence-based policy change while rigorously tracking our counterfactual impact. We will publish our full findings so they can be used by other organisations working on lead poisoning or policy change more broadly.

In our preliminary cost-effectiveness analysis, we found that this intervention has the potential to be highly cost-effective — currently comparable to GiveWell's top charities. Ultimately, lead policy seems to be an area that has the potential to be *more* cost-effective than the most effective existing charities in global health and development.
