# Superforecasting in a Nutshell

*By Luke Muehlhauser*

Let's say you want to know how likely it is that an innovative new product will succeed, or that China will invade Taiwan in the next decade, or that a global pandemic will sweep the world — basically any question for which you can't just use "predictive analytics," because you don't have a giant dataset you can plug into some statistical models.

Is it possible to produce reliable, accurate forecasts for such questions?

Somewhat amazingly, the answer appears to be "yes, if you do it right."

[Prediction markets](https://science.sciencemag.org/content/320/5878/877.summary) are one promising method for doing this, but they're mostly illegal in the US, and various implementation problems hinder their accuracy for now. Fortunately, there is also the "superforecasting" method, which is completely legal and very effective.

## How Does It Work?

The basic idea is very simple. The steps are:

1. **Bother to measure forecasting accuracy at all.** Some industries care a lot about their forecasting accuracy and therefore measure it, for example hedge funds. But most forecasting-heavy industries do not even bother to measure their forecasting accuracy, for example the US intelligence community or philanthropy.

2. **Identify the people who are consistently more accurate than everyone else** — say, those in the top 0.1% for accuracy, for multiple years in a row. These are your "superforecasters."

3. **Pose your forecasting questions to the superforecasters, and use an aggregate of their predictions.**

Technically, the usual method is a bit more complicated than that, but these three simple steps are the core of the superforecasting method.

## How Well Does It Work?

A few years ago, the US intelligence community tested this method in a massive, rigorous forecasting tournament that included multiple randomized controlled trials and produced over a million forecasts on more than 500 geopolitical forecasting questions such as "Will there be a violent incident in the South China Sea in 2013 that kills at least one person?" This study found that:

1. **This method produced forecasts that were very well-calibrated.** Forecasts made with 20% confidence came true 20% of the time, forecasts made with 80% confidence came true 80% of the time, and so on. The method is not a crystal ball; it can't tell you for sure whether China will invade Taiwan in the next decade, but if it tells you there's a 10% chance, then you can be pretty confident the odds really are pretty close to 10%, and decide what policy is appropriate given that level of risk.

2. **This method produced forecasts that were far more accurate than those of a typical forecaster or other approaches**, and approximately 30% more accurate than intelligence community analysts who (unlike the superforecasters) had access to expensively-collected classified information and years of training in the geopolitical issues they were making forecasts about.

Those are pretty amazing results! And from an unusually careful and rigorous study, no less!

## Why Isn't This Used More Widely?

Despite the intelligence community funding this study specifically to discover ways to improve US intelligence estimates, very few people in the US intelligence and national security communities have even heard of the results, or even the term "superforecasting." When economist Tyler Cowen asked former CIA Director John Brennan if he was familiar with Philip Tetlock's superforecasters project, Brennan was not familiar.

## How Organizations Can Use Superforecasting

A large organization such as the CIA or the Department of Defense has enough people, and makes enough forecasts, that it could implement all steps of the superforecasting method itself. Smaller organizations, fortunately, can just contract already-verified superforecasters to make well-calibrated forecasts about the questions of greatest importance to their decision-making. In particular:

- The superforecasters who out-predicted intelligence community analysts are available through **Good Judgment Inc.**
- **Hypermind** offers aggregated forecasts from "champion forecasters" for corporate clients.
- **Metaculus** is also identifying forecasters with unusually high accuracy across hundreds of questions.

Open Philanthropy has commissioned forecasts from all three organizations. If you work for a small organization that regularly makes important decisions based on expected future developments, trying them out is worth considering.

## Key Takeaways

- Reliable, well-calibrated forecasting on difficult geopolitical and social questions is possible.
- The key is measuring accuracy, identifying consistently good forecasters, and aggregating their predictions.
- Superforecasters outperform intelligence analysts even without access to classified information.
- Most institutions that would benefit from this method have not adopted it.
- For EA purposes, superforecasting tools (like Metaculus, Manifold Markets, and similar platforms) are valuable for thinking carefully about uncertain futures — including the outcomes of interventions and cause area comparisons.
