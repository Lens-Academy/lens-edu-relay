## What Good Does Knowledge of LLMs Do?

Understanding how Large Language Models work provides practical value for AI policy, governance, and public discourse about AI safety. This knowledge helps counter misinformation and enables more informed decision-making about advanced AI systems.

### Understanding Capability Trajectories

Knowing that current LLMs use approximately 1% of the parameters found in human brains demonstrates why AI systems won't remain at their current capability level indefinitely. This context is crucial for long-term planning around advanced AI development.

### Policy and Governance Applications

Understanding the distinction between AI "training" (the development phase) and "inference" (the deployment phase) matters significantly for designing regulatory frameworks. However, future algorithmic advances might blur this separation, requiring flexible policy approaches.

### Identifying Algorithmic Breakthroughs

The transformer architecture represents a pivotal innovation developed by a small team at Google. Comprehending how such algorithms function and their impacts helps stakeholders anticipate how future breakthroughs might affect AI capabilities and risks.

### Combating Disinformation

Without technical knowledge, people remain vulnerable to false claims about AI safety and capabilities. Some actors claim humans fully understand current AI systems (they don't), or that mathematical foundations somehow eliminate AI risks—claims that collapse under scrutiny.

### The Challenge of Expert Credentials

AI developers write readable code that *creates* the learning machinery, but the resulting AI's internal workings remain largely opaque. Experts may understand the *training process* thoroughly while comprehending little about the *trained system's* actual cognition.

### Current State of Interpretability Research

As of early 2025, interpretability research receives approximately 0.1% of personnel and 0.01% of funding compared to AI capability development. Understanding achieved so far represents roughly 1/1,000th of the clarity engineers maintain for traditionally designed systems.

### Limitations of Language-Based Transparency

Even when AIs generate readable English explanations, substantial cognition flows through attention mechanisms beyond human perception. Training against "bad thoughts" teaches systems concealment rather than elimination. More sophisticated reasoning doesn't guarantee legibility.

### False Security

Warning signs have limited value if organizations merely suppress them through additional training rather than addressing underlying issues.
