*The effective altruism community has grown tremendously since it began in 2009. In the opening keynote of EA Global: San Francisco 2016, Toby Ord and Will MacAskill discuss this history and consider what might happen in the movement's future.*

*Starts at 2:22.*

## The most important conversation of my life

Hello Effective Altruism Global. I say this every single year and every single year it's true. Welcome to the largest gathering of the Effective Altruism Community that the world has ever seen. It's my absolute delight to introduce the conference on the past, present, and future of effective altruism. I'm going to introduce Dr. Toby Ord, who will talk about the past of effective altruism before talking, myself, about what's happening at the moment and what the future of effective altruism might look like.

It's my absolute delight to introduce Toby because there's no one in the world who's had a bigger positive impact on my life than he has. I met Toby in the spring of 2009 in Oxford. I'd had an idea for the Ph.D. thesis and my supervisor said, "Oh, if you're interested in that, you've got to talk to Toby." And so you might think, well, two Oxford philosophy graduate students meeting in the spring. The venue might be this really wonderful location. As many of you know, the most important conversation in my life actually happened in a graveyard. I met Toby and it is at a time of quite extreme moral turmoil for me. I was very influenced by the ideas of Peter Singer and the previous summer they'd spent that summer fundraising for Care International. I spent all day, every day talking about extreme poverty and how much good we could do if we just chose to spend some of our money to fight global poverty. But what I'd found coming to Oxford is that all the academics I spoke to — there's a lot of hand-wringing. A lot of people accepted these ideas, but very few people were actually putting them into action. And that's why when I met Toby, I was completely blown away. We met up, it was meant to be for an hour, and that conversation turned into five hours.

He told me all sorts of weird ideas. He told me about this thing called a quality-adjusted life year. He told me about intestinal worms. He told me about the quantified impact of all the different foodstuffs, some meat products you could buy or not, and how damaging they were in terms of animal welfare. He told me he was concerned about this thing called existential risk and at the time I thought he was literally insane. But most of all, he told me about the commitment that he'd made to give away most of his income over the course of his life. And at the time, he was actually still a graduate student. He was just living on GBP 9,000, saving GBP 2,000 and donating a further GBP 2,000. He had this idea for Giving What We Can, an organization that would encourage as many people as possible to give 10% of their income to the most effective charities. At that point, I was just completely on board. I dropped all of my other projects just so that I could focus on this as much as I could. Happily, we co-founded Giving What We Can six months later.

## The Scientific Revolution

Thanks, Will. To really situate Effective Altruism in its history, I think it makes sense first to really take a step back and think about the history of ideas.

The first really big idea that's useful to give us some context is the scientific revolution. In the 1600s in Europe, various scientific greats such as Copernicus, Galileo, Newton, and Francis Bacon took those ideas and really synthesized them to help set up the Royal Society and really get science moving. The really interesting thing about the scientific revolution is that up until that point, the idea of intellectual progress wasn't really there. People in the Middle Ages, and even the early Renaissance, were looking back to the past. The whole narrative was about how the Greeks knew so much, and they were clinging to and trying to preserve the knowledge from the past, which was seen as a diminishing supply. And when people wanted to know the answer to something, they would try to look it up in Aristotle rather than go out and find out the answer themselves.

The scientific revolution really changed that narrative to be looking forward rather than back and creating a method for the systematic creation of knowledge. A hundred years later, we started to see some applications of this outside of natural science. In Europe, we had the Enlightenment. This was the application of both reason and evidence to various things in society. It created the fields such as sociology, economics, and law. And perhaps most importantly, it involved rethinking political structures — the ideas of liberty, egality, fraternity that led to the French Revolution, also the ideas behind the Declaration of Independence. And it also led to an end to unquestioned authority — both political authority from the divine right of kings, and also an end to authority from the old masters.

So how does effective altruism fit in? One way to think about it is that effective altruism is the pursuit of the good as the scientific revolution was the pursuit of the truth. The Enlightenment involved rethinking the best political systems to have, whereas effective altruism re-thinks the best actions.

A third major thread relevant to effective altruism is utilitarianism. This really got out of the gates with Bentham in 1780 when he published *An Introduction to the Principles of Morals and Legislation*. He was a legal scholar in Britain, and he created utilitarianism and the greatest happiness principle. And here is just a number of the things included in this book, from 1780: advocacy for individual and economic freedom, separation of church and state, equal rights for women, the right to divorce, the decriminalization of homosexuality, the abolition of slavery, the abolition of the death penalty, the abolition of corporal punishment, and respecting animal welfare. We still don't even have all of these things today. The main similarity between this utilitarian movement and the effective altruism movement is a focus on going out and helping others, a real focus on scale, and being willing to question the status quo.

## Taking medicine seriously

In the history of medicine, the biggest thing I think happened in the late 1800s, was when they applied the scientific method to medicine. This was 200 years after the scientific method had been developed. They developed the germ theory of disease, which led to the saving of hundreds of millions of lives, at least, probably billions of lives. And then another big breakthrough was the application of priority setting — the first time that people were trying to actually improve health *as much as possible*: prioritizing based on how cost-effective things are so that with a limited budget, we produce as much health as possible.

Key moments: 1968, the development of the quality-adjusted life year; 1999, the establishment in the UK of the National Institute for Clinical Excellence.

A really similar story for global poverty. In the 1950s, aid began. The scientific method was applied quite a bit later. There was a long period with aid where the discussions were largely people from the armchair having disputes — for example, on bed nets for malaria, whether charging a small amount of money instead of giving them away for free would mean better uptake. And then people eventually decided, why don't we just find out? They found that you're better off giving them away for free. In 2002, IPA was established; in 2003, J-PAL. Then GiveWell in 2007, Giving What We Can in 2009.

## The Past Year

*(Will MacAskill continues)*

When we look at the main effective altruist groups and how their impact measures have increased, it's really been pretty astonishing — exponential growth. GiveWell exceeded a hundred million dollars moved to top charities: 50,000 households have had their incomes double through direct cash transfers; eight million bed nets distributed through AMF; 15 million deworming tablets distributed. Giving What We Can has almost 2,000 members with almost $800 million pledged. 80,000 Hours has hundreds of people significantly changing their career plans each year.

Effective altruism is entering the mainstream — journalists, academics, donors are engaging with these ideas as a real institution.

## The Future of EA

If exponential growth continued for the next 10 years, Giving What We Can would have $100 billion in pledges, and GiveWell would be moving more than $10 billion per year to the most effective charities. In 20 years, cause prioritization might become a major field of research; politicians might incorporate EA ideas into policy platforms.

## Better allocation of resources

If we could get this mindset change on a large scale, the problems we think of today — global poverty, factory farming, global catastrophic risks — would start to look almost trivial. If the top one billion wealthiest people kept their wealth at the same level for one year and distributed the returns to the very poorest billion, you would double the income of all the poorest billion people in the world. That alone would eradicate extreme poverty.

But I think there's also a subtle and potentially even larger benefit: the potential to drive forward moral progress.

## Moral progress

If you look at the history of ideas, in every single generation there have been huge moral atrocities committed by the people of that generation that at the time just seemed completely normal. In every generation, people have been completely oblivious to the fact of how wrong some of their practices were.

For example, Aristotle spent his entire life dedicated to thinking about how to lead an ethical life, and it just didn't occur to him that maybe keeping slaves was the wrong thing to do. That's a pretty astonishing fact.

But when we look at atrocities like slavery, the deplorable treatment of foreigners, the subjugation of women, the persecution of people who aren't heterosexual, the persecution of animals today, what we see over and over again is how easy it is for people to be oblivious to serious moral problems.

It seems very unlikely that we've discovered all of the moral problems today. It seems very unlikely that we are the generation that figured it all out. Given this, what we should be thinking about is: What are the sorts of major moral problems that in several hundred years we'll look back and think, "Wow, we were barbarians!"? What are the major issues that we haven't even conceptualized today?

I will refer to this as **Cause X**.

I think you could argue that one of the most important aims of the effective altruism community is to discover this Cause X: to discover a cause that's one of the most important moral problems of our time, but that we haven't even clearly conceptualized yet. Cause X might be an idea that today seems laughable, but will seem obvious in the future — just as ideas like animal welfare or existential risk were laughable 200 years ago. Or perhaps Cause X will be something that we are aware of today, but for bad reasons, we deprioritized.

Perhaps the most exciting way in which the EA community could have a huge positive impact on the world is if we can drive forward moral progress and figure out the problems, the Cause X, that we're not even aware of today.

## Effective altruism as an intellectual project

The theme of this conference is effective altruism as an intellectual project. And it's the intellectual project of asking, how can we help others as much as possible? How can we do the most good? Effective altruism is not an ideology, it's not a set of prescriptions, it's not a body of facts. It's a methodology. It's a pursuit of a question.

When you look at the history of improvements in a lot of people's lives, a lot of that is driven by intellectual progress. It was intellectual progress that allowed us to develop vaccines, eradicate smallpox (saving over 60 million lives), bring polio and measles close to eradication, make the world richer such that basically everyone in the world is far richer than humanity has ever been for most of its life.

And so I want to suggest that the key thing to take with you through this conference is to think: what's the biggest, most important issue in which you still might be uncertain, and in which you might be very wrong? It's only by constantly learning, being able to constantly appreciate new ideas, new arguments, and new evidence, by constantly learning and being willing to change our minds — that's the only way that we're going to be able to do the most good. Thank you.
