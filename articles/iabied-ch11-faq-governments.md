{--{"author":"AI","timestamp":1774206332268}@@# Won't the situation get better once governments get more involved?

## It depends on how (and how soon) they get involved.

Policymakers in Washington often believe AI companies control their systems, while industry figures claim regulation will solve the problem. Google's CEO notably stated the "underlying risk [of humanity being wiped out] is actually pretty high," yet argued humanity would rally to prevent catastrophe — essentially imagining someone else will address the danger he's helping create.

Political leaders similarly seem to assume technical experts will solve alignment challenges. References to winning "the AI race" presume technical problems get resolved first. As of mid-2025, Congress generally doesn't believe companies are actually pursuing superintelligence, with limited exceptions.

Everyone in positions of power appears to expect someone else to handle this issue.

For context on institutional response failures, see Chapter 12. As of August 2025, governments haven't mustered serious responses. Officials risk treating AI as normal technology requiring minimal regulation rather than recognizing its singular existential threat.
