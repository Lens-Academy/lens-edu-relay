## Is "general intelligence" a meaningful concept?

**Yes.**

Humans demonstrate remarkable versatility. The authors note that "peregrine falcons dive at 240 mph, sperm whales dive miles deep, but humans fly faster and dive deeper inside metal shells we designed." Our ancestors weren't selected for these specific abilities, yet we developed them through problem-solving capacity.

### Generality exists on a spectrum

Deep Blue (chess AI) couldn't play checkers or fetch milk. AlphaGo demonstrated greater generality by playing chess, Go, and Atari games—a meaningful progression showing that "some methods of intelligence are much more general than others."

### Learning capacity matters more than knowledge

The authors distinguish between memorizing specific routes (like Alice) versus internalizing a map (like Beth). This difference illustrates how general intelligence involves "mental gears that can be reused and adapted to lots of different scenarios."

### Why Formal Definition Isn't Required

The authors acknowledge we lack "a mature formal theory of generality," yet argue this doesn't undermine the concept's usefulness. They compare intelligence to fire: humans created it before understanding combustion chemistry. Similarly, we can recognize and build intelligent systems without perfect definitions.

### Practical Implications

The piece emphasizes that lacking precise definitions doesn't prevent real consequences. An undefined threat remains dangerous—much like "forest fires burn regardless of chemistry knowledge."
