# The Case for Reducing Existential Risk

*By Benjamin Todd, 80,000 Hours*

## Introduction

In 1939, Albert Einstein and Leó Szilárd wrote a letter to President Roosevelt warning of a new type of bomb of devastating power that Nazi Germany might be building. Roosevelt took their warning seriously and launched the Manhattan Project. We know how the story ends: the US built the bomb first.

This story illustrates how a small number of people, working on the right problem at the right time, can change the course of history. Today, we face a new set of risks that could be similarly transformative—risks that could permanently end or curtail humanity's future.

## What's at stake

A survey by researcher Spencer Greenberg found that over 30% of Americans think the risk of human extinction within the next century is less than 1 in 10 million. Most experts think it's far higher.

To understand what's at stake, consider what humanity might achieve if we survive and flourish. Philosopher Nick Bostrom estimates that if humanity survives long enough to colonise the galaxy, the number of people who could eventually live is around 10^23—hundreds of billions of billions. Carl Sagan put it this way: "If we are the custodians of what may be 500 trillion future people, the stakes are extraordinarily high."

Existential risks—risks that could permanently end or severely curtail humanity's long-term potential—therefore deserve special moral weight. Even small reductions in these risks could be extraordinarily valuable.

## The main risks

### Asteroid impacts

The most natural benchmark for thinking about catastrophic risk is asteroid impacts. Astronomers estimate there is roughly a 1-in-5,000 chance per century of an asteroid large enough to kill most of humanity hitting Earth. We've now catalogued most large near-Earth objects and can rule out impacts in the next century, so this risk is relatively well understood.

Other risks are much less well understood.

### Nuclear war

We've come closer to nuclear war than most people realize. During the Cuban Missile Crisis, a Soviet submarine—out of radio contact and under depth charge attack—nearly launched a nuclear torpedo. Only the refusal of officer Vasili Arkhipov prevented the launch. In 1983, Soviet early warning systems falsely showed an incoming US missile strike; officer Stanislav Petrov chose not to retaliate, correctly judging it to be a malfunction.

The probability of nuclear war in any given year may seem small, but over many decades the cumulative risk is significant. And a large-scale nuclear exchange could cause a "nuclear winter" killing billions.

### Climate change

Current climate projections suggest a roughly 10% chance of warming exceeding 6°C above pre-industrial levels if we follow a high-emissions trajectory. At that level, large parts of the planet could become uninhabitable. While climate change is unlikely to cause human extinction directly, the social and political instability from extreme warming could dramatically increase other risks.

### Engineered pandemics

The 1918 Spanish flu killed an estimated 50 million people when global population was under 2 billion. Advances in biotechnology make it increasingly feasible to engineer pathogens that are more transmissible or lethal. In 2006, a reporter for The Scientist ordered a small sample of smallpox-related DNA by mail—demonstrating how accessible dangerous biological materials are becoming.

Unlike natural pandemics, engineered pandemics could be deliberately optimized to kill. Some biosecurity experts believe a sufficiently advanced engineered pathogen could kill most of humanity.

### Artificial intelligence

Perhaps the most discussed risk among researchers is artificial intelligence. A 2016 survey of 352 AI researchers found a median estimate that there is a 50% chance of human-level AI within 45 years. The same researchers estimated, on average, a roughly 5% chance that the development of such AI leads to outcomes that are "extremely bad" for humanity.

5% may seem small, but for a risk of this magnitude, it's alarming. And some researchers put the probability much higher.

## How neglected are these risks?

One striking fact is how little attention and funding existential risks receive relative to their potential severity.

| Cause | Annual funding |
|-------|---------------|
| Climate change | >$300 billion |
| Global health | >$30 billion |
| Nuclear security | ~$1 billion |
| AI safety research | ~$10 million |
| Biosecurity | ~$1–2 billion |

AI safety research received roughly 30 times less funding than the video games industry spends on research. Yet if AI is even a 1% risk of causing an existential catastrophe, reducing that risk by even a fraction would be extraordinarily valuable.

This neglect is partly because existential risks are diffuse, unfamiliar, and occur in the future—making them psychologically less salient than immediate, visible problems.

## Toby Ord's estimate

Toby Ord, a senior research fellow at Oxford and author of *The Precipice*, has done some of the most careful work on existential risk. His overall estimate is that the probability of an existential catastrophe within the next century is around **1 in 6**.

This estimate encompasses risks from nuclear war (~0.1%), climate change (~0.1%), engineered pandemics (~0.3%), misaligned AI (~10%), and other/unforeseen risks. He is explicit that these are rough estimates under significant uncertainty, but argues the order of magnitude is plausible.

If Ord is in the right ballpark, reducing existential risk is one of the most important things humanity could work on.

## Why now matters

There's a case that we're living through an unusually critical period. The technologies that could pose existential risks—particularly AI and synthetic biology—are maturing within decades, not centuries. The decisions and institutions we build in the next few decades may determine whether humanity navigates these transitions safely.

This suggests a particular urgency for those who can work on these problems today. A small number of people, working on the right problems, could meaningfully reduce the odds of catastrophe—much as Einstein and Szilárd's letter changed the trajectory of the Manhattan Project.

## What can be done?

Reducing existential risk encompasses many different approaches:

- **Technical research**: AI alignment research, pandemic prevention technologies, biosafety
- **Policy and governance**: Treaties, regulations, international coordination
- **Institutional capacity**: Building organizations that can respond to emerging threats
- **Field building**: Expanding the number of researchers and resources dedicated to these problems

80,000 Hours believes that working on existential risk is among the highest-impact paths available to people with the right skills and interests. Even if you're uncertain about the precise probabilities, the potential scale of what's at stake—hundreds of trillions of future people—makes this a cause that deserves serious attention.
