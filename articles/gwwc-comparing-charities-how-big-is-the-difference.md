# Comparing Charities: How Big is the Difference?

It's tempting to think that it doesn't really matter where you donate as long as you donate somewhere. After all, aren't most charities doing some sort of good? And with so many problems in the world, does it really matter which ones you choose to focus on as long as you help in *some* way?

This is a fairly common point of view. But it misses something incredibly important:

**Where you choose to donate can lead to huge differences in impact.**

Our research team believes that many of us can easily 100× our impact by giving to charities that achieve *more* per dollar spent. Variations in impact can be so enormous that your choice of where to donate can quite literally save lives.

## A Couple Comparisons to Get Us Started

**Comparison #1**

- Donation A: $15 to Toys for Tots, which provides toys to children in need.
- Donation B: $15 to Evidence Action's Safe Water Program, which delivers water treatment solutions to at-risk communities.
- Difference: 1 toy delivered to one child in need vs. clean water for a year for ten children.

**Comparison #2**

- Donation A: $3,200 to the Navy SEAL Foundation's education branch, which provides scholarships to recipients.
- Donation B: $3,200 to GiveDirectly, which provides cash transfers to those in extreme poverty.
- Difference: Increase income for a scholarship recipient by $7,500 vs. double the income of three people currently living in extreme poverty.

**Comparison #3**

- Donation A: $1,500 to the Battersea Dogs and Cats Home, one of the UK's oldest and best-known animal shelters.
- Donation B: $1,500 to a corporate cage-free campaign programme, such as The Humane League.
- Difference: Pay half the cost for one dog/cat to be sheltered vs. reduce the suffering (through cage-free commitments) of around 15,000 egg-laying chickens.

Surprised at how big these differences are? You're not alone. In 2023, a small study found that donors vastly underestimate how much charities vary in impact.

## Why Are There Such Stark Differences in the Impact of Donations?

It's widely understood that *products* vary in their quality. But when it comes to charities, people are reluctant to acknowledge that there are large differences — after all, aren't all charities doing good work?

Most charities do have good intentions. But this doesn't mean charity isn't subject to the same types of variation as other areas. Organisations differ in what they do, how much it costs them to do it, and how far your dollar goes.

There's also an important difference between charity and business: *donors don't directly experience what their donation does*. While companies go bankrupt if they don't deliver good products, charities can continue to exist even if they don't make much of an impact, as long as they are good at marketing and fundraising. This means we should expect the charitable "marketplace" to be much less efficient, and for there to be bigger differences between charities than between products.

Yet many of us behave as though it were the opposite. We sift through reviews, compare costs, and "shop around" before making an important personal purchase, but tend to give intuitively when trying to improve the world.

## Five Ways You Can Multiply Your Impact

### #1. Choose Programmes Supported by an Independent, Impact-Focused Evaluation

An impact-focused evaluation is a deep dive into what a charity's programmes are accomplishing and how cost-effectively that translates to impact.

The cautionary tale of PlayPumps International illustrates why this matters. PlayPumps seemed like an innovative approach to bringing clean water to communities — children played on merry-go-rounds that doubled as water pumps. The initiative won a World Bank award, received millions in aid money, and attracted celebrity attention.

It wasn't until 1,800 PlayPumps had been installed that major issues came to light: children did not enjoy "playing" on the pumps, they required constant force to work, and children often injured themselves. One organisation estimated that children would have to "play" for 27 hours each day to meet the stated goal. Each pump cost about $14,000 to install — considerably more than the hand pumps they replaced.

For the cost of one PlayPump, Evidence Action's Safe Water programme *could have provided clean water to over 9,000 people for an entire year*.

### #2. Help Where You Can Help the Most

Because of vast income differences between countries, your money goes a lot further overseas. Americans living in poverty are likely at least 10× richer than those living in poverty in lower-middle-income countries — even after adjusting for differences in cost of living.

This matters because of diminishing returns to marginal consumption: money matters less when you have more of it. Doubling your income when you live on $5 a day is at least as meaningful as doubling your income when you live on $500 a day.

This principle also applies to animal welfare: while 99.6% of animals killed in the US are farmed animals, about 66% of donations to animal welfare go to animal shelters, and only 0.8% goes to farmed animal organisations.

### #3. Find the Most Cost-Effective Programmes Working on a Problem

Most people don't systematically search through all charities working on a problem. But impact-focused evaluators like GiveWell do.

GiveWell uses GiveDirectly — direct cash transfers to people in extreme poverty — as a *benchmark*, and only recommends charities it believes are at least twice as cost-effective as GiveDirectly-style cash transfers. This means that donating to a GiveWell top charity could be at least 20× more impactful than donating to a charity working to alleviate poverty in the US.

### #4. Look for Leverage Points

You can potentially have an even bigger impact by looking for ways to influence a larger resource base. This involves going one step away from direct intervention and supporting tractable ways to leverage more resources.

Examples: LEEP (Lead Exposure Elimination Project) leverages government resources by helping governments implement lead paint regulation. Deworming programmes leverage government, NGO, and investor resources. The Humane League leverages the resources of corporations through cage-free commitments.

Giving multipliers — organisations that do outreach about effective giving — are another example: evidence suggests $1 spent on some such organisations can cause $10 or more to be spent on high-impact charities.

### #5. Expand Your Circle of Concern

Most of us don't sit down to systematically consider what our values are, and whether our actions are reflecting them. However, this is where you may be able to find the biggest gain.

Upon reflection, maybe you realise you care about those suffering far away — not just people close to you physically. Or perhaps you realise that while you'd been focused only on helping *people*, you care about the wellbeing of other sentient beings too. Or you start thinking about all the people who will live after you — why is someone living 1, 100, or 1,000 years from now less worth helping than someone living now?

Expanding your circle of concern will likely lead you to support work that is larger in scale, and by many worldviews, can greatly increase your impact.

**Case Study: Comparing Toys for Tots vs. Evidence Action**

The financial statements for Toys for Tots indicate that in the fiscal year ending December 2022, the organisation spent $359,941,848 distributing 24.4 million toys to 9.9 million children in need — about $15 per toy.

Evidence Action estimates that its Safe Water Now programme provides clean water to people in need for just $1.50 per person per year. For $15, you could provide ten children access to clean water for a year.

**Case Study: Navy SEAL Foundation vs. GiveDirectly**

The Navy SEAL Foundation (rated "highly cost-effective" by Charity Navigator) increases income for a scholarship recipient by $7,500 for roughly $3,200 — about $1 in → $2.34 out.

GiveDirectly provides direct cash transfers to people living on less than $800 per year (adjusted for purchasing power). For $3,200, you could double the income of three people living in extreme poverty.

GiveWell-recommended charities go further still: for roughly $5,000, you could — in some countries — save a child's life.

**Case Study: Battersea Dogs & Cats Home vs. Cage-Free Corporate Campaigns**

Battersea Dogs and Cats Home spends roughly $3,000 per animal cared for per year. A $1,500 donation covers half the cost of caring for one animal.

Based on estimates by Rethink Priorities, for each dollar donated to highly effective corporate campaign work, the living conditions of approximately 10 egg-laying hens are significantly improved. For $1,500, you could remove one of the worst forms of suffering (battery cages) for around 15,000 chickens during their lives.
