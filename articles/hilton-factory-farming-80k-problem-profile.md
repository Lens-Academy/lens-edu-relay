## Summary

History is littered with moral mistakes — things that once were common, but we now consider clearly morally wrong, for example: human sacrifice, gladiatorial combat, public executions, witch hunts, and slavery.

In my opinion, there's one clear candidate for the biggest moral mistake that humanity is currently making: factory farming.

The rough argument is:

- There are *trillions* of farmed animals, making the scale of the potential problem so large that it's hard to intuitively grasp.
- The vast majority (we estimate 97.5%) of farmed animals are in *factory farms*. The conditions in these farms are far worse than most people realise.
- Even if nonhuman animals don't morally matter as much as humans, there's good evidence that they are conscious and that they feel pain — and, as a result, the poor conditions in factory farms are likely causing animals to experience severe suffering.

What's more, we think that this problem is highly neglected and that there are clear ways to make progress — which makes factory farming a highly pressing problem overall.

### Scale

We kill around 1.6 to 4.5 trillion animals a year. There's no consensus about how to compare this to the scale of issues that affect humans, because people disagree about the moral significance of animals. However, we believe there are good reasons to think that the suffering of animals on farms has moral significance. When we adjust for the best quantitative assessment we can find for how 1.6 to 4.5 trillion animals being killed per year compares to human suffering, this amounts to the equivalent of around 60 million to 800 billion humans going through that same experience. (Though this is a very under-researched question, so this conversion should be treated as extremely uncertain.)

### Neglectedness

This issue is highly neglected. We'd estimate there are approximately 3,000 people working on reducing harms from factory farming and around $400 million dedicated to this issue.

### Solvability

This seems moderately tractable and there are some plausible ways we could make progress.

## 1. How many animals are in farms?

Every year, we kill somewhere between 400 billion and 3 trillion vertebrates (e.g. cows, chickens, fish) — some are killed for sport and some are dissected for experiments, but the vast majority are either slaughtered for food or die in farms before they're old enough to be purposefully slaughtered.

There are probably around 120–210 billion vertebrates alive in farms at any given time.

If we include *invertebrates* (e.g. octopuses, insects, crabs, snails, shrimp) — which is more controversial because we have less evidence of their ability to feel pain — it's more like 1.6–4.5 trillion farmed animals killed per year, and around 350–700 billion animals alive in farms at any given time.

| Farmed animal | Alive in farms at any one time | Slaughtered per year |
|---|---|---|
| Chickens | 24 billion (10 billion broilers; 7–10 billion egg-laying hens) | 72–77 billion |
| Pigs | 1 billion | 1.5 billion |
| Cattle (cows) | 1.5 billion | 0.3 billion |
| Sheep and goats | 2.2 billion | 1 billion |
| Ducks | 1 billion | 3 billion |
| Fish | 100–180 billion | 100 billion |
| Shrimp | — | 210–530 billion |
| Crickets | 35–40 billion | 370–430 billion |

## 2. How do we treat these animals?

In general, the main issues are:

- Poor methods for pre-slaughter stunning, and for slaughter itself
- Extremely crowded and dirty living conditions
- High rates of disease and injury
- Particularly poor conditions during transport
- Occasional withdrawals of food and water for extended periods
- High rates of painful procedures, often carried out without anaesthetic

### How farmed chickens are treated

We've genetically selected broiler chickens to grow very fast; they may reach slaughter weight in as little as five weeks. This fast development causes fluid to build up in the abdomen (*ascites*), which compresses organs, causing pain and breathing difficulties. Their large sizes also cause difficulty moving, which causes muscle and bone pain.

Even on free-range farms, each bird often has only around 0.1 m² of space. Long periods of standing and lying in waste causes painful lesions and chemical burns. Heat stress is common due to temperatures of 30–40°C.

Breeder chickens are kept on the edge of starvation — feed is restricted to about 20–25% of what a broiler breeder pullet would eat with unlimited access.

Before slaughter, chickens must be "caught" and loaded into trucks. Often, half the flock is left without food or water for days until they are also collected ("depopulation"). In countries without regulations on humane slaughter, many chickens experience severe pain, and some are boiled alive when prior stunning steps fail.

Caged laying hens have around 0.07 m² of space, preventing typical movement, standing, preening, or turning. Cage-free hens are marginally better off but still suffer from keel bone fractures and stress-related pecking injuries. To prevent pecking and cannibalism, the tip of hens' beaks is routinely removed using a hot blade, without anaesthesia. Male chicks from the egg industry are killed by maceration at birth.

### How farmed pigs are treated

Most farmed pigs are kept indoors on high-density farms. Heat stress is common; pigs do not sweat and normally cool themselves through wallowing.

Piglets go through tooth cutting and tail docking without anaesthesia. Most male piglets are castrated without full anaesthesia. Breeding sows are kept in gestation crates — small pens so narrow that they cannot move or turn around (outside Sweden and the UK).

## 3. Do animals have moral status?

The key question is whether nonhuman animals are *moral patients* — beings whose suffering matters morally.

We believe there are strong reasons to think most farmed animals are sentient. They show responses to noxious stimuli, have nervous systems capable of pain processing, and express behavioural indicators consistent with subjective suffering. The evidence is strongest for vertebrates, and weaker but non-negligible for some invertebrates like crustaceans.

Even on views that discount animal welfare relative to human welfare, the sheer scale of animals affected — trillions per year — means the aggregate suffering is enormous.

## 4. Is there room for more funding and talent?

Yes. Factory farming is highly neglected relative to its scale. With only ~3,000 people and ~$400 million dedicated to this problem globally, there are many funding gaps and talent needs across:

- Corporate outreach campaigns (persuading companies to adopt better standards)
- Policy advocacy (banning cages, improving slaughter practices)
- Alternative protein research and development
- Welfare biology research
- Legal reforms for animal protection

## 5. How to help

**High-impact career paths include:**

- **Research** into animal consciousness, welfare science, or alternative proteins
- **Policy and advocacy** work at organisations like the Humane League, Animal Equality, or Compassion in World Farming
- **Movement building** for effective animal advocacy
- **Alternative protein development** (cultured meat, plant-based foods, precision fermentation)
- **Earning to give** and donating to effective animal welfare organisations

**Recommended organisations** (as assessed by Animal Charity Evaluators and Open Philanthropy) include: The Humane League, Mercy for Animals, Animal Equality, Good Food Institute, and Faunalytics.
