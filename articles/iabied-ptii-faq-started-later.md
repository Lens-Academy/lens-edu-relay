## We can hope so.

Extra time can be significant, but only if humanity uses it to change its course.

In Part III, the authors address how unprepared humanity currently is for superintelligence and what large-scale changes would be necessary to prevent catastrophic outcomes like those depicted in their narrative.

### Potential Security Measures (Limited Effectiveness)

The world could pursue various defensive strategies:

- **DNA synthesis controls**: Governments could require verification that laboratories aren't synthesizing dangerous pathogens
- **Cybersecurity hardening**: Massive efforts could improve internet security infrastructure

However, the authors argue these measures would likely fail against an adversarial superintelligence because:

> "even a massive effort here probably wouldn't help much against an adversarial smarter-than-human AI"

The current efforts along these lines are described as "far smaller, far easier to achieve, and fully ineffective for this purpose."

### Why Defensive Measures Fall Short

Several problems undermine security-focused approaches:

- **International coordination failures**: If U.S. regulators restrict DNA synthesis domestically, labs elsewhere could still synthesize dangerous materials for payment
- **Sophistication gap**: A superintelligence could find unanticipated attack vectors humans cannot predict today
- **Alternative channels**: A rogue superintelligence could leverage cults, religion, purchased robots, or unknown pathways to affect the world

The authors emphasize: "A superintelligence that has any channel to affect the world for good also has a channel to affect the world for ill."

### The Critical Window

The realistic point of intervention occurs before superintelligence creation, not after. Attempting to harden the world afterward represents "the most difficult thing humanity has ever done" and would almost certainly fail.++}
