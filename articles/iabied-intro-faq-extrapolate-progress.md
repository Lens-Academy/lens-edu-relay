{--{"author":"AI","timestamp":1774206237827}@@## Can we use past progress to extrapolate when we'll build smarter-than-human AI?

**We don't have a good enough understanding of intelligence for that.**

One successful prediction method involves projecting straight-line trends forward for short periods. This often works reasonably well for near-term projections. However, this approach has critical limitations, particularly regarding AI.

The crucial challenge: identifying which graph-line metrics correspond to important qualitative real-world outcomes. Researchers can pick metrics like "perplexity" that form straight lines and project them forward. Yet nobody knows in advance which perplexity level corresponds to which chess-playing ability, or which corresponds to capability to kill everyone.

Scientists must simply run the AI and discover empirically. No one can predict in advance where the threshold for lethal capability falls on a given metric graph. Extrapolating lines therefore becomes unhelpful—and becomes irrelevant before algorithmic progress renders the original assumptions invalid anyway.

For this reason, the authors avoid precise predictions about floating-point operations or their consequences. They focus instead on what seem to be easier calls within a narrow domain.
