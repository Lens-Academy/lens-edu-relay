# Preventing Catastrophic Pandemics

*By 80,000 Hours*

## Summary

We think there's more than a 1-in-10,000 chance of a biological event causing human extinction this century, and potentially as high as 1-in-100. The risk of a catastrophic but non-extinction pandemic is far higher.

This makes biosecurity one of the most pressing global priorities—and one that receives far less funding and talent than its scale warrants.

## Introduction

Pandemics have killed more people throughout history than any war. The Black Death killed an estimated 30–60% of Europe's population. The 1918 influenza pandemic killed more people in a single year than World War I killed in four.

Today, we face a new and more alarming threat: the deliberate engineering of pathogens designed to be more transmissible, more lethal, or both. Unlike natural evolution, deliberate engineering can produce pathogens that skip the usual trade-offs—potentially far more dangerous than anything that has emerged naturally.

## Historical precedents

### Natural pandemics

The scale of historical pandemics helps calibrate our sense of what's possible:

- **541–549 CE: Plague of Justinian** — Killed an estimated 25–50 million people across the Byzantine Empire, contributing to its decline.
- **1347–1351: Black Death** — Killed 30–60% of Europe's population, roughly 75–200 million people globally.
- **1492–1600: Columbian Exchange** — The introduction of Old World diseases to the Americas killed an estimated 50–90% of indigenous populations—perhaps 50 million people.
- **1918–1919: Spanish Flu** — Infected roughly 500 million people (one-third of humanity at the time) and killed 50–100 million.

None of these came close to causing human extinction, but they radically altered the course of civilization.

### Engineered pathogens and bioterrorism

The threat landscape for bioweapons includes:

- **Soviet Biopreparat**: The Soviet Union ran a massive, secret bioweapons program employing tens of thousands of scientists until the 1990s. They developed enhanced anthrax, plague, and smallpox strains.
- **Aum Shinrikyo**: The Japanese cult that conducted the 1995 Tokyo subway sarin attack also attempted—unsuccessfully—to develop and release anthrax and botulinum toxin.
- **Sverdlovsk anthrax leak (1979)**: A Soviet facility accidentally released anthrax spores, killing dozens of people. The Soviet government concealed the incident for years.

The decreasing cost and increasing accessibility of synthetic biology tools means that creating dangerous pathogens is becoming more feasible for smaller groups.

### Mirror bacteria

In December 2024, a letter signed by 38 scientists—including several Nobel laureates—warned of a novel biosafety risk: mirror-image life. Scientists have been working toward creating bacteria built from mirror-image molecules (the enantiomers of standard biological molecules). Such organisms could be resistant to all existing immune defenses, since they would be invisible to the enzymes that normally break down pathogens.

The scientists warned that the release of mirror bacteria, even accidentally, could be catastrophic—potentially outcompeting ordinary life in some ecological niches with no natural predators.

## Risk estimates

Several groups have attempted to quantify the probability of catastrophic pandemic events:

**Existential/extinction risk:**
- The Existential Risk Persuasion Tournament (XPT) estimated roughly 1-in-50,000 to 1-in-100 probability of an engineered pathogen causing human extinction by 2100.
- Our overall estimate is >1-in-10,000 probability of pandemic-caused extinction, potentially as high as 1-in-100.

**Catastrophic (but non-extinction) risk:**
- XPT estimated a 1–3% probability of an engineered pandemic killing >10% of humanity by 2100.
- A 4–10% probability of an engineered pathogen killing >1% of humanity by 2100.

These ranges reflect genuine uncertainty, not precision. But even at the low end, the scale is enormous.

## The relationship to AI risk

Some researchers argue that AI could dramatically accelerate biosecurity risks. Large language models trained on biological literature could lower the barrier for creating dangerous pathogens. On the other hand, AI tools could also accelerate pandemic detection and vaccine development.

More speculatively, some researchers worry about a future where AI could design novel pathogens autonomously—a scenario that would require both AI safety and biosecurity solutions.

## Why biosecurity is neglected

Despite the scale of the risk, biosecurity funding and talent remain extremely limited relative to other cause areas. Some reasons include:

- **Diffuse threat**: There's no clear constituency for pandemic prevention the way there is for treating specific diseases.
- **Coordination problems**: Biosecurity requires international cooperation, which is politically difficult.
- **Dual-use dilemma**: Some biosecurity research involves exactly the knowledge that could be misused.
- **Abstraction**: A pandemic that hasn't happened yet is psychologically less salient than one that is occurring now.

## Key technical approaches

Researchers in biosecurity work on a wide range of approaches:

**Early detection and surveillance**: Building pathogen-agnostic detection systems that can identify novel threats quickly, without needing to know what you're looking for in advance.

**Diagnostics and treatments**: Developing platform technologies (like mRNA vaccines) that can be rapidly adapted to new pathogens.

**Biosafety and biosecurity regulations**: Strengthening oversight of dangerous pathogen research, including through the Biological Weapons Convention (BWC).

**Protective equipment**: Improving personal protective equipment to reduce transmission even for highly contagious pathogens.

**Safe containment**: Developing better laboratory protocols and physical containment to reduce the risk of accidental release.

## How to help

Careers that contribute to reducing pandemic risk include:

- **Biosecurity policy**: Working with governments and international organizations on treaties, regulations, and pandemic preparedness planning.
- **Technical biosecurity research**: Developing detection systems, vaccines, and treatments.
- **Scientific communication**: Helping policymakers understand biological risks.
- **Institutional capacity**: Building organizations (like the Johns Hopkins Center for Health Security) that can maintain ongoing attention on biosecurity.
