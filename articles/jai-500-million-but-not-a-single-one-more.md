# 500 Million, But Not a Single One More

Smallpox is the only human disease that has ever been eradicated. Before that happened, it killed roughly 500 million people in the twentieth century alone. It had been killing people for at least three thousand years.

And then, in 1980, it was gone.

This is the story of how that happened.

---

## The Disease

Smallpox was caused by the variola virus. It killed about 30% of the people it infected. Those who survived were often left blind, or with severe scarring. In the twentieth century, it killed more people than all of that century's wars combined.

It was everywhere. It didn't care about borders or politics or income. It killed in Europe and Africa and Asia and the Americas. It killed rich people and poor people. It killed children especially — in some places, half of all children died of smallpox before reaching adulthood.

---

## The Idea That Changed Everything

Vaccination against smallpox was practiced in various forms for centuries. The practice of variolation — deliberately infecting people with material from smallpox pustules to induce a milder protective infection — was known in China and the Ottoman Empire by the 15th century, and was brought to England in the 18th century.

Edward Jenner's 1796 discovery that cowpox infection protected against smallpox led to the first true vaccine. By the 19th century, vaccination programs existed in many countries. But the disease persisted. Coverage was patchy. Cold chains didn't exist. The vaccine degraded in heat.

---

## The Decision to Eradicate

In 1958, a Soviet virologist named Viktor Zhdanov stood up at the World Health Assembly and made a proposal that many thought was absurd: a global campaign to eradicate smallpox entirely.

This was not a modest suggestion. It would require coordinating health systems across dozens of countries, including adversaries in the Cold War. It would require manufacturing and distributing hundreds of millions of doses of vaccine to places with no refrigeration and no roads. It would require political will sustained over years, across changing governments and priorities.

The WHO eventually agreed to try. The Intensified Eradication Programme began in 1967.

---

## The Campaign

Karel Raška, a Czech epidemiologist, developed a strategy called "surveillance and containment." Rather than trying to vaccinate every person on earth — which was logistically impossible — teams would watch for outbreaks and surround them with vaccination rings, cutting off the virus's path to new hosts.

Donald Henderson ran the WHO programme. He was relentless, obsessive, and infuriating to work with by many accounts. He pushed, cajoled, and sometimes browbeat governments and health ministries into cooperation. He maintained the campaign through wars, famines, and political upheavals.

The work was done by thousands of people whose names are not famous: local health workers who walked for days to reach remote villages; doctors who worked in heat and conflict; vaccinators who tracked down the last remaining chains of transmission.

---

## The Last Case

On October 26, 1977, a Somali hospital cook named Ali Maow Maalin developed a fever. He was the last person in the world to catch smallpox in the wild.

He survived.

On December 9, 1979, a global commission formally certified that smallpox had been eradicated. On May 8, 1980, the World Health Assembly declared it official.

---

## What This Means

Smallpox killed at least 300 million people in the 20th century. Possibly 500 million. The historical estimates vary, but even the low end is almost incomprehensible.

And then it stopped. Not because the disease ran out of hosts, or because of some lucky accident. It stopped because a group of people decided to stop it, and coordinated a massive, sustained, global effort to do so.

This happened during the Cold War. It required cooperation between the United States and the Soviet Union. It required health workers traveling to places where wars were ongoing. It required political will sustained over more than a decade.

---

## The Reason to Tell This Story

It's easy to feel that the world's problems are too large, too entrenched, too complicated for any coordinated human effort to address. Smallpox is a counter-example.

Half a billion people died of smallpox in the twentieth century. And then it stopped. The last case was in 1977. The children who were born after 1980 don't need to be vaccinated. The suffering simply ended.

This was done by people. Not by gods or historical forces or luck. By people who decided a problem was solvable and set about solving it.

That is a remarkable thing to have happened. It is worth remembering.
