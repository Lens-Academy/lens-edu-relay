*This is a transcript of a talk given at EA Global Boston 2017. A written version is available at the Center on Long-Term Risk.*

---

I'm going to talk about risks of large-scale severe suffering in the far future, or S-Risks. To illustrate what S-Risks are about, I'd like to start with a fictional story from the British TV series Black Mirror. In this fictional scenario, it's possible to upload human minds into virtual environments. Sentient beings can effectively be stored and run on very small computing devices. Matt's job is to sell those virtual humans as virtual assistants. In order to make an unwilling human upload comply, Matt increases the rate at which time passes for her — so while Matt only needs to wait for a few seconds, during that time she effectively endures many months of solitary confinement.

I hope you agree that this would be an undesirable scenario. I'll argue that there is in fact a broad range of scenarios we face risks of — scenarios that are in some ways like this, or even worse. I'll call these risks S-risks.

## What Are S-Risks?

S-risks will be a subclass of the more familiar existential risks or X-risks. These have been defined by Nick Bostrom as risks where an adverse outcome would either completely annihilate Earth-originating intelligent life or at least permanently and drastically curtail its potential.

One way to understand how these risks differ from other kinds of risks is to look at two dimensions: **scope** (how many individuals would be negatively affected?) and **severity** (for each individual affected, how bad would the outcome be?).

Existential risks are those with pan-generational scope and crushing severity. One central example is extinction risk, which has received a lot of attention in the EA community. But **S-risks are another type of existential risk** — they are risks that would be even worse than extinction because they contain a lot of things we disvalue (such as intense involuntary suffering), and would have even larger scope because they would affect a significant part of the universe.

**S-risks are risks of severe involuntary suffering on a cosmic scale**, thereby exceeding the total amount of suffering we've seen on Earth so far. This makes them a subclass of existential risk, but a distinct subclass from extinction risks.

## Should We Focus on Reducing S-Risks?

The question isn't just whether reducing S-risks is good — all plausible ethical views agree that intense involuntary suffering is bad. The question is whether it can meet the higher bar of being among the *most* effective uses of our time or money.

To assess this, we need to consider the probability, tractability, and neglectedness of S-risks.

### Probability

**S-risks are at least not much more unlikely than extinction risks from superintelligent AI**, which some parts of the community already take seriously.

Two not-too-implausible technological developments could lead to S-risks:

1. **Artificial sentience**: The capacity to have subjective experience and to suffer is, on most contemporary views in philosophy of mind, not limited in principle to biological animals. Whole brain emulation — building a functionally equivalent computer simulation of the human brain — is one concrete pathway, with researchers having outlined a detailed roadmap and concrete milestones.

2. **Superintelligent AI**: Already getting significant attention in the EA community. Superintelligent AI could unlock capabilities needed to get into S-risk territory, such as colonizing space and spreading sentient beings across larger parts of the universe.

We shouldn't intuitively dismiss S-risks as absurd. Psychological research shows that humans systematically underestimate the probability of things that have never happened (the "absurdity heuristic"). The right approach is to examine what our best scientific theories say about how the future could unfold.

### Routes to S-Risks

S-risks could arise through at least three routes — not just through evil intent:

1. **By accident**: We might create artificially sentient beings with limited capacity to communicate their suffering (similar to how some non-human animals can suffer but can't signal it easily to us). A paperclip-maximizing AI might run sentient simulations for scientific purposes, or create artificially sentient sub-programs whose suffering is instrumentally useful for its goals.

2. **As part of a conflict**: When many agents compete for shared resources, negative-sum dynamics can emerge even if no agent actively values the bad outcomes — similar to how wars cause immense suffering even when neither side intrinsically values bloodshed.

3. **Through evil intent** (the most obvious but perhaps least likely route).

If we take seriously the concerns about superintelligent AI that are already widely discussed in the community, we need only a few additional assumptions to substantiate worries about S-risks. We don't need completely implausible technologies or rare motivations like sadism.

### Tractability

Reducing S-risks is challenging — no silver bullet has been found — but it appears **at least minimally tractable**:

- Some existing work in technical AI safety and AI policy probably already effectively reduces S-risks (especially work that reduces the likelihood of multi-polar AI scenarios and conflict dynamics).
- That said, most of these interventions reduce S-risks *by accident* rather than specifically targeting them. There may well be particular sub-problems within technical AI safety that would be especially effective at reducing S-risks specifically.
- Broader interventions — strengthening international cooperation, expanding the moral circle to include potentially sentient AI — could also reduce S-risks indirectly.
- The Foundational Research Institute (now Center on Long-Term Risk) exists specifically to research how best to reduce S-risks.

### Neglectedness

Some work in the x-risk space arguably reduces S-risks, so they're not totally neglected. But they receive **much less attention than extinction risk**. I've sometimes seen people in the community explicitly or implicitly equate existential risk with extinction risk, which is conceptually incorrect. Very few people are specifically trying to identify interventions most effective at reducing S-risks.

## Implications for Cause Prioritization

I haven't conclusively answered for whom reducing S-risks is the best thing to do — this depends on ethical views and empirical questions about probability, tractability, and neglectedness.

But I have argued:
- S-risks warrant at least some attention given their plausible probability
- The most plausible known pathways to S-risks involve AI scenarios with large numbers of artificially sentient beings
- **Among currently known sources of existential risk, AI risk is unique in also being very relevant for reducing S-risks**. If we don't get AI right, there is a significant chance of getting into S-risk territory. By contrast, an asteroid hitting Earth or a deadly pandemic, while catastrophic, seems much less likely to produce outcomes far worse than extinction by also containing vast amounts of suffering.

This is an update for caring more about AI risk as opposed to other existential risk cause areas.

There's also a gap: people and research specifically optimizing for reducing S-risk, trying to find those interventions most effective for this particular goal. The Center on Long-Term Risk occupies an important niche in this space, and would welcome more people joining this effort.

*Q&A highlights:*

**On other forms of S-risk beyond AI**: The most plausible scenarios involve artificial sentience partly because it would presumably be very easy to spawn large numbers of artificially sentient beings, with efficiency advantages of silicon-based substrates. However, in messier futures with many factions controlling AI, there could be risks more akin to factory farming — novel technology misused for economic goals, inadvertently creating large amounts of suffering.

**On whether we should care about silicon-based minds**: A suggestive thought experiment: imagine replacing your brain with silicon chips one neuron at a time. At no point does it seem like the transition should eliminate our concern for the being's experiences. The same information processing is still occurring.

**On what to do if we haven't solved moral philosophy in time**: This is a very important challenge, and it seems fairly likely there won't be convergence on moral philosophy before transformative AI. This is why it's critical to compare maps among people who share concern for the far future, and find ways to handle remaining disagreements without derailing the collaborative project.
