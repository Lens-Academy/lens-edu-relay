The AI safety community has grown rapidly since the ChatGPT wake-up call, but available funding doesn't seem to have kept pace.

There's a more recent dynamic that's created even better funding opportunities. Most philanthropic (vs. government or industry) AI safety funding (>50%) comes from one source: Good Ventures. But they've recently stopped funding several categories of work:

- Many Republican-leaning think tanks, such as the Foundation for American Innovation.
- "Post-alignment" causes such as digital sentience or regulation of explosive growth.
- The rationality community, including LessWrong, Lightcone, SPARC, CFAR, MIRI.
- High school outreach, such as Non-trivial.

In addition, they are currently not funding (or not fully funding):

- Many non-US think tanks, who don't want to appear influenced by an American organisation (there are now probably more than 20 of these).
- Technical safety non-profits like FAR AI (though they've been underfunding this area, in part due to difficulty hiring).
- Political campaigns (since foundations can't contribute to them).
- Organisations below their current funding bar for various reasons.
- Nuclear security (on average less cost-effective than direct AI funding in their view).

This means many organisations in these categories have only been able to access a minority (~25%) of available philanthropic capital in recent history. In the recent Survival and Flourishing Fund grant round, these organisations faced a funding bar 1.5 to 3 times higher than usual.

This creates a lot of opportunities for other donors: if you're interested in one of these categories, focus on finding gaps there.

In addition, even among organisations that *can* receive funding from Good Ventures, receiving ~80% of funding from one donor is an extreme degree of centralisation. By helping to diversify the funding base, you can probably achieve an effectiveness somewhat above Good Ventures itself.

Open Philanthropy (who advise Good Ventures on what grants to make) is also large and capacity constrained, which means it's relatively easy for them to miss small, new organisations (<$250k), individual grants, or grants that require speed. Smaller donors can play a valuable role as "angel donors" who identify promising new organisations, then pass them on to OP to scale up.

In response to the attractive landscape, SFF allocated over $19m of grants, compared to an initial target of $5–15m. However, that wasn't enough to fill all the gaps.

## Specific Organizations to Consider

Some concrete ideas that stand out as worth thinking about (in no particular order):

- **[SecureBio](https://securebio.org/)**: One of the best biorisk organisations, especially for the intersection of AI and Bio. SFF gave $250k to the main org, but $1m would have been warranted.
- **Non-US AI governance think tanks**: If you're a non-US person, consider funding AI governance non-profits in your locality — e.g. [CLTR](https://www.longtermresilience.org/) (leading UK think tank on AI safety); [CeSIA](https://www.securite-ia.fr/en) and [Simon Institute](https://www.simoninstitute.ch/) in Europe.
- **[Center for AI Safety (CAIS)](https://www.safe.ai/) and their political Action Fund**: Dan Hendrycks' organisations, which have driven significant successes in AI policy. Not receiving funding from OP. SFF gave $1.1m to CAIS and $1.6m to the Action Fund, but they could deploy more.
- **[METR](https://metr.org/)**: Perhaps the leading AI evaluations organisation. Hasn't received OP funding recently. Has short-term funding but a rapidly growing compute budget.
- **[Apollo Research](https://www.apolloresearch.ai/)**: The leading European evals group, with a budget in the millions but only $250k from SFF.
- **[Lightcone](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/5n2ZQcbc7r4R8mvqc/the-lightcone-is-nothing-without-its-people)**: LessWrong seems to have been cost-effective at movement building, and the Lightcone conference space also seems useful. Facing a major funding shortfall.
- **[MATS Research](https://www.matsprogram.org/)**: Strong track record for AI safety fellowship programmes.
- **[Non-trivial](https://www.non-trivial.org/)**: Has a $1m funding gap in high school outreach.
- **[Manifund regranting programme](https://manifund.org/about/regranting)** or **[The AI Risk Mitigation Fund](https://www.airiskfund.com/about)**: Specialise in smaller, often individual grants.

These are not blanket recommendations, but they seem worthy of consideration and illustrate a rough lower bound for what could be done with $10m of marginal funds.

## How to Get Involved

The situation is likely to evolve — new donors are starting to make larger grants (e.g. Jed McCaleb's Navigation Fund), and as AI safety becomes more mainstream, more donors are expected to enter. The most pressing gaps will probably be better covered in a couple of years, which means giving now could be an especially impactful choice.

If you're interested in these opportunities:
- **$250k/year or more**: Reach out to Open Philanthropy, who regularly recommend grants to donors other than Good Ventures (donoradvisory@openphilanthropy.org).
- **Philanthropic advisory**: Longview provides advisory in this area and also has a fund.
- **Quick donation**: See recommendations by Open Philanthropy staff or the Longview fund.
- **Otherwise**: Reach out to specific organisations for more information, and ask around about them to be aware of critiques.
