# Fermi Estimate

A **Fermi estimate** (or **back-of-the-envelope calculation**, often abbreviated **BOTEC**) is a rough calculation that aims to be right within about an order of magnitude, prioritizing getting an answer good enough to be useful without putting in the large amounts of thought and research needed to attain greater accuracy.

Fermi estimates typically approximate an answer by making various simplifying assumptions and decomposing the problem into smaller tractable units. Open Philanthropy and other organizations in the effective altruism community routinely use BOTECs for impact assessment and cause prioritization.

## Why Fermi Estimates Matter

When deciding between different ways to help, you rarely have precise data. But you usually don't need precise data — you need to know whether one option is roughly 10× better than another, not whether it's exactly 9.3× or 11.7× better.

A Fermi estimate lets you do this. By breaking a complex question into smaller pieces you can estimate individually, you can often arrive at a useful rough answer. And crucially, the process of building the estimate tells you *which sub-questions matter most* — which is exactly what you need to know if you want to gather better data.

## How to Make a Fermi Estimate

1. **Break the question into components.** What factors does the answer depend on?
2. **Estimate each component.** What's your best guess for each, even if rough?
3. **Combine them.** Multiply or add as appropriate.
4. **Sanity-check the result.** Does it make sense given what you already know?

For example, to estimate the number of piano tuners in Chicago, you might reason: Chicago has about 3 million people, averaging about 1 piano per 100 people = 30,000 pianos; each piano needs tuning about once per year = 30,000 tunings per year; a piano tuner does about 4 tunings per day × 250 working days = 1,000 tunings per year per tuner; so Chicago needs about 30 piano tuners. (The actual number is roughly in this range.)

## Fermi Estimates in Effective Altruism

In effective altruism, Fermi estimates are used to compare the expected impact of different interventions. For example:

- How many lives would a malaria net programme save per $1,000?
- How many animals does a corporate campaign to improve cage-free standards affect per dollar donated?
- How much does one year of research on AI safety reduce the probability of catastrophic AI outcomes?

These questions involve deep uncertainty. Fermi estimates don't eliminate that uncertainty, but they help you reason about it systematically — and often reveal that some interventions are likely to be orders of magnitude more effective than others, even after accounting for uncertainty.

If you want to try your hand at making some Fermi estimates, you can play [The Estimation Game](https://www.quantifiedintuitions.org/estimation-game). New games are released each month, and you can play previous months' games in the archive.
