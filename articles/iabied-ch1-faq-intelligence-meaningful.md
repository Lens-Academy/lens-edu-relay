## Is intelligence a meaningful concept?

**Yes. There's a real phenomenon to describe, even if it's difficult to pin down.**

The authors argue that intelligence represents an observable natural phenomenon deserving attention, even without a precise definition. They note that "seventy-seven Nobel Prizes in Chemistry have been awarded to humans, and zero to chimpanzees," illustrating a genuine capability gap.

Something about human brains enables moon landings and technological civilization—abilities distinctly absent in other species. This "something" warrants discussion regardless of definitional debates.

### Why Precise Definitions Don't Matter for Practical Concerns

The text emphasizes that lacking perfect terminology doesn't eliminate real-world risks: "If machines start converting the surface of the Earth into their own infrastructure... it won't much matter whether we have a precise definition of 'intelligence' yet."

Like understanding forest fire chemistry isn't required to recognize flames will burn you, understanding intelligence's essence isn't necessary to acknowledge transformative AI capabilities.

### Building Intelligence Without Understanding It

Humans created fire before grasping combustion chemistry. Similarly, AI development proceeds despite incomplete understanding. Rather than demanding mathematical precision, the authors propose treating "intelligence" as a label for observed capabilities—particularly the ability to predict and influence the world.

### Intelligence as Observable Capability

The authors focus on what intelligence *does* rather than internal mechanisms. Humans perform diverse feats: particle physics, medicine, agriculture, military strategy. The label "intelligence" describes this enabling capacity itself.

Human and AI intelligence needn't be identical processes to produce comparable real-world impacts.
