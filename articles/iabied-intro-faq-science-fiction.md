## Isn't this AI stuff just science fiction?

**We can't learn much from a topic's prevalence in fiction.**

Smarter-than-human AI hasn't been built but appears in fiction. However, anchoring on fictional depictions risks distortion. Real AI probably won't resemble fictional versions substantially, as discussed in Chapter 4.

AI isn't the first technology fiction anticipated. Heavier-than-air flight, moon travel, and nuclear weapons all appeared in speculative fiction before realization. H.G. Wells's 1914 novel "The World Set Free" depicted nuclear weapons—though inaccurately, describing prolonged burning rather than instantaneous explosion and lingering radiation.

This example illustrates a key risk: Roosevelt might have dismissed the concept upon encountering Wells's novel. Actually, Roosevelt took Einstein and Szilard's 1939 letter seriously enough to establish the Advisory Committee on Uranium. But this demonstrates the hazard of dismissing ideas simply because similar concepts appeared in fiction.

Science fiction misleads in both directions—by being taken as predictive or dismissed as inherently false. Authors aren't prophets or anti-prophets. Their predictions are neither reliably accurate nor reliably inaccurate. The vast majority of cases favor analyzing technologies on their own terms rather than through fictional precedent.

To predict reality, there's no substitute for examining arguments and weighing evidence directly.

### The consequences of AI are inevitably going to be weird

Smarter-than-human AI would transform the world fundamentally. All human intuitions adapted to a world where only humans possess sophisticated intelligence and machines remain unintelligent tools. A future containing superintelligent AI would look radically different.

Major, lasting world changes happen infrequently. The heuristic "nothing ever happens" generally performs well, yet fails catastrophically during historically significant moments when substantial change occurs. Anticipating those moments when major transformation happens enables preparation.

The steam engine and Industrial Revolution demonstrate how particular inventions upend the world. Metrics for life expectancy, GDP per capita, poverty reduction, energy capture, and democracy rates remained largely flat from 1000 BCE until around 1800, then skyrocketed through the late 1990s.

Truly general AI appears at least as consequential as the Industrial Revolution because:

1. **Accelerated technological progress**: Machines operate far faster than human brains. Machines can improve AI—and AI will eventually improve itself—until superhuman at scientific discovery and technological invention.

2. **Novel intelligent species**: Sufficiently capable AIs will possess independent goals. Unlike faster, smarter humans, AIs represent a fundamentally new intelligent species with goals likely diverging importantly from human goals.

These developments seemingly cannot occur without upending the existing world order. Believing in a "normal" future requires believing machine intelligence will never surpass human intelligence—increasingly implausible given 2025 developments versus earlier predictions.

### The future will hit us fast

Technologies like AI mean the future may arrive soon with hard-hitting effects. The Industrial Revolution transformed the world very quickly by pre-modern standards. New world-changing processes reshape things very quickly measured by old standards.

Humanity approaches another radical transformation where machines reshape the world at machine speeds, vastly outstripping biological speeds.++}
