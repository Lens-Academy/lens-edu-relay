In most facilitator roles, participants will also come to you between sessions — with questions, project ideas, decisions they're trying to make. **Coaching** is a different mode than facilitating, and it takes a different posture.

The core insight: coaching is **about the person, not about the problem**. You're not there to solve their problem. You're there to help them think more clearly about it. Most rookie coaches make the same mistake: they listen to the problem, then start solving.

\## Read

**Read: 12 Top Takeaways from The Coaching Habit — Hannah Pixel (4 min):** https://medium.com/@hannahpixels/12-top-takeaways-from-the-coaching-habit-e2ea3028ec34 — Summary of Michael Bungay Stanier's framework.

The most useful single technique from the book: **stay curious longer**. Most coaching conversations have one question and four pieces of advice. Better conversations have four questions and one piece of advice (or none).

\## Seven questions worth memorising (from The Coaching Habit)

1. **The Kickstart:** "What's on your mind?"
2. **The AWE Question:** "And what else?" — used several times, draws out everything underneath the first answer.
3. **The Focus Question:** "What's the real challenge here for you?"
4. **The Foundation Question:** "What do you want?"
5. **The Lazy Question:** "How can I help?"
6. **The Strategic Question:** "If you're saying yes to this, what are you saying no to?"
7. **The Learning Question:** "What was most useful for you?" — to close.

\## The GROW framework (when coaching feels heavier)

If a participant is wrestling with a bigger decision — picking a project, choosing a direction — the **GROW** framework gives you a structure:

- **G — Goal.** What do you actually want? (Often the missing piece. People are so deep in the problem they haven't articulated the goal.)
- **R — Reality.** What's the situation right now? Constraints, facts, what's been tried.
- **O — Options.** What are the possible paths? Don't evaluate yet; brainstorm wide.
- **W — Will / Way forward.** Of those options, which one will you actually do? By when?

A coaching conversation that walks through GROW takes 20–40 minutes and is worth more than three of your generic "let's chat" calls.

**Read: The GROW framework — NYU Coaching (5 min):** https://wp.nyu.edu/coaching/tools/grow-model/ — has prompts for each stage.

\## When the participant is scoping a project

A specific common ask in many courses: a participant is trying to scope their final project / capstone / portfolio piece. Three questions worth asking:

1. **"What's the smallest version of this that would still be interesting?"** (Forces them away from "boil the ocean" projects.)
2. **"What would make this valuable — to you, to a reader, to the field?"** (Forces them past producing-something-for-its-own-sake.)
3. **"What would you do this week if you could only spend 2 hours on it?"** (Forces a real first step.)

**Read: Do the Real Thing — Scott Young (5 min):** https://www.scotthyoung.com/blog/2020/05/04/do-the-real-thing/ — On why the bias toward concrete action is so important in learning projects.

**Read: Being the (Pareto) Best in the World — John Wentworth (5 min):** https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/XvN2QQpKTuEzgkZHY/being-the-pareto-best-in-the-world — Useful framing for participants choosing where to focus, especially in interdisciplinary work.
