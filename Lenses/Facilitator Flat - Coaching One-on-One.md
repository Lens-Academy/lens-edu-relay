\## Read

**Read: 12 Top Takeaways from The Coaching Habit — Hannah Pixel (4 min):** https://medium.com/@hannahpixels/12-top-takeaways-from-the-coaching-habit-e2ea3028ec34

The core insight: coaching is **about the person, not about the problem**. You're not there to solve their problem. You're there to help them think more clearly about it. Most rookie coaches make the same mistake: they listen to the problem, then start solving.

The most useful single technique: **stay curious longer**. Most coaching conversations have one question and four pieces of advice. Better conversations have four questions and one piece of advice (or none).

\## Seven questions worth memorising

1. **The Kickstart:** "What's on your mind?"
2. **The AWE Question:** "And what else?" — used several times, draws out everything underneath the first answer.
3. **The Focus Question:** "What's the real challenge here for you?"
4. **The Foundation Question:** "What do you want?"
5. **The Lazy Question:** "How can I help?"
6. **The Strategic Question:** "If you're saying yes to this, what are you saying no to?"
7. **The Learning Question:** "What was most useful for you?" — to close.

\## The GROW framework (for heavier decisions)

**Read: The GROW framework — NYU Coaching (5 min):** https://wp.nyu.edu/coaching/tools/grow-model/

- **G — Goal.** What do you actually want? (Often the missing piece. People are so deep in the problem they haven't articulated the goal.)
- **R — Reality.** What's the situation right now? Constraints, facts, what's been tried.
- **O — Options.** What are the possible paths? Don't evaluate yet; brainstorm wide.
- **W — Will / Way forward.** Of those options, which one will you actually do? By when?

A coaching conversation that walks through GROW takes 20-40 minutes and is worth more than three of your generic "let's chat" calls.

\## When the participant is scoping a project

Three questions worth asking:

1. **"What's the smallest version of this that would still be interesting?"** (Forces them away from "boil the ocean" projects.)
2. **"What would make this valuable — to you, to a reader, to the field?"** (Forces them past producing-something-for-its-own-sake.)
3. **"What would you do this week if you could only spend 2 hours on it?"** (Forces a real first step.)

**Read: Do the Real Thing — Scott Young (5 min):** https://www.scotthyoung.com/blog/2020/05/04/do-the-real-thing/ — On why the bias toward concrete action is so important.

**Read: Being the (Pareto) Best in the World — John Wentworth (5 min):** https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/XvN2QQpKTuEzgkZHY/being-the-pareto-best-in-the-world — Useful framing for participants choosing where to focus.

\## After

Pick a recent conversation you had with someone who was stuck. Walk through what you actually said. How many of your turns were questions? How many were advice? What would have changed if the ratio were flipped?

instructions::
The trainee has just consumed the coaching material.

Learning outcome for this Lens: Distinguish coaching from facilitating; name three coaching questions and GROW stages; apply 'stay curious longer.'

Key concepts:
- Coaching is about the person, not the problem
- Seven questions from The Coaching Habit; AWE is most under-used
- GROW for heavier decisions (Goal, Reality, Options, Will)
- "Stay curious longer" = invert the question/advice ratio
- Three project-scoping questions for the "boil the ocean" trap

Discussion guidance:
- If they think coaching = better advice, push back hard. Coaching = better questions.
- The "stay curious longer" rule is hard to follow. Ask: what makes them want to jump to advice? Usually a desire to be helpful — but advice given before the person has fully articulated the problem is rarely useful.
- For project scoping, the "what's the smallest version" question is the highest-leverage one.

Probe: "When was the last time someone gave you advice before they really understood your situation — how did that feel? What would you have wanted instead?"
