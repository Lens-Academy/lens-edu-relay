\## Moves that work

1. **Pair them up.**{--{"author":"Yatharth's AI","timestamp":1781125638410}@@ Think-Pair-Share is a quiet-person's friend. --}{++{"author":"Yatharth's AI","timestamp":1781125638410}@@ ++}Speaking to one other person is far less costly than speaking to nine.
2. **Direct, but with an out.** *"Tariq, I'd be curious if anything in this is landing for you — but no pressure if not."* The named invitation matters; the explicit out matters more.
3. **Reference their non-verbal.** *"I saw you nodding when Maya made that point — want to add anything?"* Lower stakes than a cold call, because you're naming a contribution they already made.
4. **Ask for a specific kind of input.** *"For anyone who hasn't spoken yet — does anything here resonate with your own experience, or feel off?"* A general invitation that doesn't single anyone out, but does explicitly include the quiet ones.
5. **Wait longer than is comfortable.** Most facilitators give 2 seconds after a question. Try 7. The quiet members are often willing to speak; they just need the silence to stretch enough for the talkers to stop filling it.

\## What to avoid

- **Cold-calling without warning.** {++{"author":"Yatharth's AI","timestamp":1781125638410}@@(*"Rachel, what do you think?"*) — ++}Some people will say something; many will {--{"author":"Yatharth's AI","timestamp":1781125638410}@@freeze.--}{++{"author":"Yatharth's AI","timestamp":1781125638410}@@say something while internally in a freeze response — heightened anxiety, going on autopilot.++} The {++{"author":"Yatharth's AI","timestamp":1781125638410}@@visible ++}cost {--{"author":"Yatharth's AI","timestamp":1781125638410}@@of freezing--}{++{"author":"Yatharth's AI","timestamp":1781125638410}@@is silence; the invisible cost++} is {--{"author":"Yatharth's AI","timestamp":1781125638410}@@high;--}{++{"author":"Yatharth's AI","timestamp":1781125638410}@@that++} they'll be quieter for weeks {--{"author":"Yatharth's AI","timestamp":1781125638410}@@afterward.--}{++{"author":"Yatharth's AI","timestamp":1781125638410}@@afterward, because the body remembers the spike.++}
- **Calling on the same talker because they're easy.** Easy and necessary aren't the same.
- **Praising answers in a way that ranks them.** {--{"author":"Yatharth's AI","timestamp":1781125638410}@@*"Great--}{++{"author":"Yatharth's AI","timestamp":1781125638410}@@(*"Great++} point, Maya!"* makes the next person worry their point won't be "great" {--{"author":"Yatharth's AI","timestamp":1781125638410}@@enough.--}{++{"author":"Yatharth's AI","timestamp":1781125638410}@@enough.)++} Use neutral acknowledgment.{--{"author":"Yatharth's AI","timestamp":1781125638410}@@

Learning outcome for this Lens: Name three moves that engage quiet participants without cold-calling, and identify two patterns to avoid.

Key concepts:
- Five moves: pair, direct with out, reference non-verbal, specific kind of input, wait longer
- Avoid: cold-calling, calling on easy talkers, ranked praise
- Cost of freezing is high: weeks of fallout
- Quiet ≠ disengaged

Discussion guidance:
- If they think cold-calling is fine ("I respond well to it"), push: you're not the median participant. The cost is borne by people who freeze, who won't tell you they froze.
- Pair them up is almost always the right first move for someone who hasn't spoken.
