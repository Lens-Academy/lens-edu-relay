#### Text
content:: The mental posture facilitators are supposed to operate from has a name: the **mutual learning mindset**, originally from organisational researcher Roger Schwarz, popularised in the *Curiosity Society Facilitator's Handbook*.

It has five values:

1. **Transparency** — Share your thinking. Don't hide what you're noticing or why you're asking the question.
2. **Curiosity** — Assume there's something you don't know. The participant who said the surprising thing probably has a reason.
3. **Informed choice** — Give people enough information to make their own decisions about what to engage with. Don't pre-decide for them.
4. **Accountability** — You're responsible for the space, even when participants make it difficult.
5. **Compassion** — Including for yourself. You will mess this up sometimes.

The opposing mindset — which most of us default to under pressure — is **unilateral control**: "I know what should happen here; my job is to make it happen." Sometimes you really do need to cut someone off, or redirect, or make a call. But if you're operating from unilateral control as your default, you're not facilitating — you're chairing.

#### Chat
instructions::
This is a short **reflection prompt**, not a quiz or an assessment. The trainee has just read about the mutual learning mindset (Schwarz's five values) and its opposite, unilateral control. Your job is to help them name their own trigger — the thing that pulls them from mutual learning into unilateral control under pressure.

Open with this question, in your own warm phrasing:
"Think about a time in the last month when you slipped into unilateral control mode — at work, with family, in a group project. What happened, and what triggered it?"

Then:
- Help them get specific about the trigger (tiredness, time pressure, disagreement, a particular person, the feeling that the group isn't "getting it").
- Reflect back the trigger you hear, and connect it forward: this is the same trigger most likely to pull them out of facilitator mode in their own sessions.
- Keep it short — 2 to 4 exchanges. This is self-knowledge, not a test. Draw it out of them; don't lecture.

Close by affirming: that trigger is theirs, and naming it now is half the battle.
