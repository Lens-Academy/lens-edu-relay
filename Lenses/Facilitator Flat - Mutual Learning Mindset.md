\## Read

**Read: A Facilitator's Handbook, pp. 4-13 — Curiosity Society (12 min):** https://drive.google.com/file/d/1BmRWqSIP0mDLPjxoafnLNOvJAUmC-Fxg/view

Focus on:
- The mutual learning mindset (pp. 4-5)
- The five values and what they look like in practice (pp. 6-8)
- The role of the facilitator (pp. 10-11)

\## The five values

1. **Transparency** — Share your thinking. Don't hide what you're noticing or why you're asking the question.
2. **Curiosity** — Assume there's something you don't know. The participant who said the surprising thing probably has a reason.
3. **Informed choice** — Give people enough information to make their own decisions about what to engage with. Don't pre-decide for them.
4. **Accountability** — You're responsible for the space, even when participants make it difficult.
5. **Compassion** — Including for yourself. You will mess this up sometimes.

The opposing mindset — which most of us default to under pressure — is **unilateral control**: *"I know what should happen here; my job is to make it happen."* Unilateral control isn't always bad. Sometimes you really do need to cut someone off, or redirect, or make a call. But if you're operating from unilateral control *as your default*, you're not facilitating — you're chairing.

\## After

Stop reading for a moment. Think about a time in the last month when you slipped into unilateral control mode — at work, with family, in a group project. What triggered it? Tiredness, time pressure, disagreement, a specific person?

That trigger is yours. It's the one that will pull you out of facilitator mode in your sessions too. **Naming it now is half the battle.**

#### Chat
instructions::
The trainee has just read pp. 4-13 of Curiosity Society's Facilitator's Handbook.

Learning outcome for this Lens: Name the five values of the mutual learning mindset, contrast it with unilateral control, and identify a personal trigger that pulls them out of mutual learning under pressure.

Key concepts:
- Five values: transparency, curiosity, informed choice, accountability, compassion
- Unilateral control is the natural default under stress, not malicious
- The trigger is personal — name it specifically
- "Chairing" vs "facilitating" is a useful operational distinction

Discussion guidance:
- Ask them to list the five from memory. If they miss one, ask: "What's missing? What's the value most easily forgotten?"
- Push on transparency specifically — facilitators often think their internal reasoning is irrelevant. It's the opposite: making your thinking visible *is* a tool for the group.
- If they can't name a trigger, prompt: "Did anything in the last week make you want to take over a conversation? Why?"

Probe: "What's a 5-second self-check you can run in a session when you feel your trigger firing — something that pulls you back to mutual learning before you act on the impulse?"
