#### Text
content:: Having just lived through the Sable scenario, we now ask: *can the engineering challenge actually be solved?*

This week's two chapters answer that in two voices. Chapter 10 — *A Cursed Problem* — names five engineering "curses" that make alignment uniquely hard: the speed of underlying processes, the narrow margin between safe and catastrophic, self-amplification, complications, and edge cases. The chapter draws on space probes (Mars Observer, Mars Climate Orbiter, Mars Polar Lander, Viking 1), nuclear reactors (Chernobyl), and computer security (buffer-overflow attacks) to show that these curses appear individually in ordinary engineering and *all of them simultaneously* in AI alignment.

Chapter 11 — *An Alchemy, Not a Science* — opens with a parable about a young alchemist who feels "close" to transmuting lead into gold, then applies that frame to AI lab leaders. It examines the public statements of Elon Musk and Yann LeCun, and the most-developed plan that AI labs have proposed for solving alignment ("superalignment"). The chapter argues that the field is still operating from philosophical ideals rather than engineering designs — that it is at the *alchemy stage*.

\## Pedagogical Note

The week's emotional work is delicate. Two failure modes are easy to fall into.

The first is **despair.** Chapter 10 closes with the line "NOBODY SHOULD BE ALLOWED TO TRY," and Chapter 11 closes by saying the field's current state alone would "be more than enough to end in disaster." Read uncharitably, that sounds like a counsel to give up.

The second is **softening.** It is tempting to read the critique of Musk and LeCun as one-sided journalism, or to assume that the alignment researchers must have a more sophisticated answer that just didn't make it into the chapter, or that "alchemy" is an unfair label for people working hard on a difficult problem.

Neither reading does the work this week is here to do. The authors are stating a *position*: the engineering challenge has specific named features that, combined, make the current attempt reckless — and the field's current epistemic state is not yet at the level where adequate engineering is even possible. That is a diagnosis, not a verdict. It points somewhere. It points toward the policy and governance questions of M6.

The thing to carry from this week, if these chapters land for you, is *frustrated clarity* — not "we are doomed" but "this specific response, with this specific level of understanding, is not adequate to the problem, and that matters."

\## This week's reading

- **Chapter 10: A Cursed Problem** — split across two readings (the five curses, then the synthesis and position statement)
- **Chapter 11: An Alchemy, Not a Science** — split across two readings (the alchemy diagnosis, then the strong-superalignment objection)

Each section is paired with a tutor conversation. As before, you don't have to do every conversation in one sitting — but the conversations are how the lessons settle. When you're ready, head to the first lesson.

#### Chat
instructions::
The student is at the start of Module 5 (Chapters 10 and 11 of "If Anyone Builds It, Everyone Dies"). They have just completed M4 (the Sable extinction scenario) and are now turning to whether the alignment field can actually engage the problem.

This Welcome Lens does not require a reading. The student may come here before any lesson, or after — to orient themselves to the week.

If the student has questions before starting:
- Acknowledge the emotional weight of M4 and validate that it is reasonable to come into M5 looking for solutions.
- Frame M5 as the week where the question shifts from "what could happen" to "can current efforts prevent it."
- If they ask "are the authors saying it's hopeless?" — answer no, but do not jump ahead to M6's policy argument. Direct them to read Chapter 10 and notice how the closing position is structured: as a logical conclusion from named curses, not as fatalism.
- If they ask "is the critique of Musk and LeCun fair?" — answer that the chapter quotes their public statements directly and that the question to hold while reading is whether the *level of analysis* in those statements matches what an engineer working on a comparable problem in another field (rocket safety, reactor safety) would offer.

Key concepts to surface across the week (do NOT explain these now, just track them in the conversation):
- The before/after gap (Ch10 framing)
- The five engineering curses (speed, narrow margins, self-amplification, complications, edge cases)
- The position statement valence — diagnosis, not despair
- The alchemy-stage diagnosis as a claim about *kind* of progress, not amount
- The strong-superalignment capability paradox

Discussion topics if the student wants to talk before starting:
- "What were you hoping to find in this part of the book?"
- "Did M4 leave you feeling more or less interested in alignment as an engineering problem?"
- "When you hear leaders of major AI labs talk about safety, what level of detail do you usually hear?"

Probe gently. The frame for this week is *frustrated clarity → productive anger*, not despair.
