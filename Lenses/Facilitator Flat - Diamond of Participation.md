#### Text
content::
\## Read

Almost every good session has the same three-phase shape:

- **Open (5-10 min).** Get the group present. Connect to the material. Surface what people are bringing. *Narrow.*
- **Explore (25-35 min).** Open up. Multiple perspectives, questions, working with the material together. *Wide.*
- **Close (5-10 min).** Re-narrow. What stuck? What's the takeaway? What's one action? *Narrow again.*

That's the diamond: narrow → wide → narrow.

\## The two rookie failures

- **Skipping the open.** Jumping straight into *"OK, so chapter 4 says…"* The group is still arriving (literally — half of them are on Zoom with another tab open). A 5-minute open is not throat-clearing. It's the gate that lets you go deep later.

- **Skipping the close.** Running 5 minutes over and saying *"we're out of time, thanks everyone."* This costs you most of what the session is built on. The close is where retention happens. Protect it.

\## After

Look at your next session. Where in the 45-minute window will you place the open and the close? What is the *one* takeaway question you'd want each participant to answer at the close?

#### Chat
instructions::
The trainee has just read the Diamond of Participation material.

Learning outcome for this Lens: Describe the three phases and identify the two most common rookie failures.

Key concepts:
- Three phases: narrow open → wide explore → narrow close
- The open is a gate, not throat-clearing
- The close is where retention happens — protect it
- Both rookie failures involve cutting the narrow bookends to give the wide middle more time

Discussion guidance:
- If they think the open is optional ("the cohort knows each other by week 3"), push: the open isn't only about introductions; it's about getting present and connecting to *today's* material.
- If they treat the close as optional ("we ran over the discussion, that's OK") — walk through what's lost. The retention literature is unambiguous: people remember beginnings and endings.

Probe: "If you had to cut 10 minutes from a 60-minute session, where would you cut? Why not the open or the close?"
