#### Text

One of the most useful skills in facilitation. Weak questions (*"any thoughts?"*, *"what did you think?"*) tend to get weak answers in return.

#### Text
content::
**Three frameworks** worth knowing, all covered in the video:

\### PPPB — Pose, Pause, Pounce, Bounce

1. **Pose** the question to the whole group (not to one person — that lets the others off the hook).
2. **Pause.** Silently. Count to seven. (It feels much longer than it is.)
3. **Pounce** — call on a specific person. The pause means everyone prepared an answer, not just whoever speaks first.
4. **Bounce** — take their answer and bounce it to someone else: *"Maya, what do you make of that?"*

\### ABC — Agree, Build, Challenge

After someone answers, you could give the next person a structured response choice:
- **Agree** with the previous answer and add something.
- **Build** on it — extend, refine, develop.
- **Challenge** it — disagree with a specific part.

This gives shy participants a scaffolded way to speak. They're not generating a new answer from scratch; they're responding to one that's already in the room.

\### EEAA — Engage, Evaluate, Apply, Adapt

When a participant gives a substantive answer, your follow-up could be one of:
- **Engage** — *"Say more about that."*
- **Evaluate** — *"What's the evidence?"*
- **Apply** — *"Where would that show up in practice?"*
- **Adapt** — *"How would your answer change if X?"*

It's easy to default to "Engage" for everything. Mixing in the others changes what the question is asking for.

\## Recall check (3 min)

Before reading on, reconstruct PPPB, ABC, and EEAA from memory with the tutor below — recall sticks better than re-reading.

#### Chat
instructions::
This is a quick **recall check** (≈3 min), not a discussion. The trainee has just watched the Sandling video and read three question frameworks. Quiz them — warmly — to reconstruct each from memory:

- **PPPB** — Pose, Pause, Pounce, Bounce (the pause is ~7 silent seconds).
- **ABC** — Agree, Build, Challenge (a scaffolded response choice you hand the next person).
- **EEAA** — Engage, Evaluate, Apply, Adapt (follow-up moves after a substantive answer).

How to run it:
- Ask them to say what each acronym stands for, one at a time. Let them try from memory first; only supply a letter if they're stuck.
- For one framework they recall well, ask them to give an example of using it in a session.
- If they reconstruct all three, affirm and send them on to the examples below. Keep it to 2–4 exchanges — this is retrieval practice, not teaching.

Probe (if there's room): "Which of the three are you most likely to forget in the moment — and what would remind you?"

\## Examples of these frameworks in flight

You don't need to memorise question templates. But seeing what PPPB / ABC / EEAA can land on helps:

- *The pre-mortem question:* "Imagine this idea fails in five years. What was the reason?" *(Adapt — changes the frame)*
- *The translate question:* "How would you explain this to someone outside this field?" *(Apply)*
- *The disagreement question:* "What's the strongest objection to what you just said?" *(Evaluate / Challenge)*
- *The instance question:* "Can you give a concrete example?" *(Apply)*
- *The threshold question:* "What would have to be true for you to change your mind?" *(Evaluate)*

These aren't a list to memorise — they're examples of how a strong question maps onto one of the moves. The point is to notice the pattern.
