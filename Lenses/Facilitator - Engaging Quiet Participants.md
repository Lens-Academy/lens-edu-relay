#### Text
content::
Quiet doesn't mean disengaged. Sometimes the quiet person is the deepest thinker in the room. Sometimes they're checked out. It's worth trying to find out, without putting them on the spot.

\## Moves that work

1. **Pair them up.** Speaking to one other person is far less costly than speaking to nine.
2. **Direct, but with an out.** *"Tariq, I'd be curious if anything in this is landing for you — but no pressure if not."* The named invitation matters; the explicit out matters more.
3. **Reference their non-verbal.** *"I saw you nodding when Maya made that point — want to add anything?"* Lower stakes than a cold call, because you're naming a contribution they already made.
4. **Ask for a specific kind of input.** *"For anyone who hasn't spoken yet — does anything here resonate with your own experience, or feel off?"* A general invitation that doesn't single anyone out, but does explicitly include the quiet ones.
5. **Wait longer than is comfortable.** Most facilitators give 2 seconds after a question. Try 7. The quiet members are often willing to speak; they just need the silence to stretch enough for the talkers to stop filling it.

\## What to avoid

- **Cold-calling without warning.** (*"Rachel, what do you think?"*) — Some people will say something; many will say something while internally in a freeze response — heightened anxiety, going on autopilot. The visible cost is silence; the invisible cost is that they'll be quieter for weeks afterward, because the body remembers the spike.
- **Calling on the same talker because they're easy.** Easy and necessary aren't the same.
- **Praising answers in a way that ranks them.** (*"Great point, Maya!"* makes the next person worry their point won't be "great" enough.) Use neutral acknowledgment.
