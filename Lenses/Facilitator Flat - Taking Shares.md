\## The seven moves

| Move | What you do | Use when |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Reflect** | "What I'm hearing is X. Is that right?" | The share was rambling; you want them to confirm what they meant |
| **Paraphrase** | Restate the share in one sentence | Others looked lost; the substance is real but hard to follow |
| **Deepen** | "What do you mean by Y?" — pick one vague word | Share contained an unexamined assumption or loaded term |
| **Connect** | "How does this relate to what Z said earlier?" | Two threads are weaving together; surface it |
| **Redirect** | "Thanks. What does someone else think?" | The speaker has been on for a while; the room needs another voice |
| **Acknowledge & Move** | "Good — let's hold that." | The share was clear and complete; keep pace |
| **Push Back** | "Let me test that." | The share contained a claim that, if true, would change the conclusion |

\## The meta-skill

Notice which move you reach for by default. If you almost always **Acknowledge and Move**, your cohort feels efficient but shallow. If you almost always **Deepen**, you lose people ready to move on. If you almost always **Push Back**, the cohort braces before speaking.

The skill is *range*, not preference.

\## Push Back, expanded

Of the seven, **Push Back** is the one most worth practising deliberately because it's the one most rookies under-use. The default cohort failure mode is **pleasant agreement** — people share, others nod, nothing actually moves.

Push-back is what stops that. And push-back is *not* disagreement:

- Disagreement: *"I think you're wrong."*
- Push-back: *"Do you mean THIS version of what you just said, or THAT version?"*

Push-back doesn't take a side. It forces the speaker to be more precise about what they actually believe. A facilitator who pushes back well doesn't have to share their own views. They just refuse to let unexamined claims pass without examination.

\### Two push-back techniques worth knowing

1. **Push to the logical extreme.** *"If that's true, would it also be true that X?"* — chase the claim to its implications until it either holds up or breaks.
2. **Surface the alternative.** *"What's the strongest version of the opposite view? What would a thoughtful person who disagrees say?"*

Use these when consensus is too cheap. You'll know it's too cheap when everyone is nodding and nobody is asking questions.

\## After

Take one of your next session's planned questions. Imagine a likely participant answer. Which of the 7 moves would you reach for? Name one alternative that could also work, and what's different about choosing it.

#### Chat
instructions::
The trainee has just watched the Digges video on taking shares.

Learning outcome for this Lens: Name all seven moves, pick the right one for given situations, identify own default and the cost of over-reliance.

Key concepts:
- Seven moves: Reflect, Paraphrase, Deepen, Connect, Redirect, Acknowledge-and-Move, Push Back
- The skill is range, not preference
- Push Back is most under-used; antidote to pleasant agreement
- Push Back ≠ disagreement; it forces precision without taking a side

Discussion guidance:
- If they're uncomfortable with Push Back ("it feels confrontational") — practice the THIS-or-THAT framing. Reframe as helping the speaker, not opposing them.
- Most defaults are Acknowledge-and-Move. Walk them through what's lost: cohort feels efficient but never gets challenged.
- If their default is Deepen, gently note: some shares ARE complete; pace matters.

Probe: "When was the last time you saw a facilitator push back on a claim they didn't believe was being challenged enough — and what did the cohort do in response?"
