#### Text
content::
Three small facilitator-level moves that the meeting template doesn't fully prescribe but make a disproportionate difference:

1. **Cameras on, by norm.** Set this in Week 1 if possible. Allow exceptions for genuine reasons (bad connection, kid in the room, illness), but make the default visible. A cohort of black squares cannot facilitate well — you lose body language, you lose the felt sense of who's tracking and who's drifted.
2. **Names on screen, with one piece of context.** Encourage participants to set their Zoom display name to something like "Aanya · ML engineer" or "Marcus · policy". Small thing; it makes the human in the box feel real to other participants and to you.
3. **Use chat deliberately, not as background noise.** Either ask people to drop a one-word answer in chat at a specific moment (then read three aloud), or ask them to leave chat alone. Don't let it run in parallel competing for attention. The cohort follows your lead on this.

\## Online is its own medium

An online discussion is its own medium, not a worse version of an in-person one. The mistake is treating it as a degraded form of the real thing. It isn't — it has different affordances.

**What's harder online:**

- Reading body language and group energy
- Silence (it feels worse than it is)
- Side connections during breaks
- Latecomers slipping in unnoticed

**What's easier online:**

- Sharing reference material live (chat, screenshare)
- Pair work (breakout rooms are frictionless)
- Including people across timezones who couldn't otherwise be in the room

A good online facilitator leans into the second list rather than mourning the first.

When you've worked through these, continue to the reflection below.

#### Chat
instructions::
This is a short **reflection** (≈3 min), not a quiz or an assessment. The trainee has just read the three online-container moves (cameras on, names + context, deliberate chat) and the "online is its own medium" framing (§3.4), and has the five candidate norms from §3.3 fresh in mind.

Open by inviting them to pick one of these two, in your own warm phrasing:
- "Which of the five norms would be hardest for *you* to enforce if a participant kept breaking it? What does that tell you?"
- "Which of the three online moves would feel most awkward for *you* to set in Week 1? Why?"

Then:
- Draw it out. The goal is for them to notice their own edge — the norm or move they'd flinch from — before they hit it in a real session.
- If they name the edge, reflect it back and connect forward: that's the spot to pre-plan, because in the moment it'll be hardest.
- Keep it short: 2 to 3 exchanges. Don't lecture or grade. There's no need for them to write anything down.

Close by affirming: noticing the edge now is the point — it's half of handling it well later.
