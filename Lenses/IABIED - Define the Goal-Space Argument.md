#### Chat
instructions::
The student has just finished reading section 1 of Chapter 5 ("Its Favorite Things") of "If Anyone Builds It, Everyone Dies."

Learning outcome for this Lens: Define the goal-space argument: explain why a sufficiently advanced AI's goals are overwhelmingly unlikely to include human values, using the logic that most possible goal-sets don't converge on any particular set of values as intelligence increases.

Key concepts:
- The Correct-Nest aliens: a civilization that cares deeply about the correct number of stones in their nests — a goal that makes sense from the inside but looks arbitrary from the outside
- The girl-alien's argument: most species in the universe wouldn't share this value, because the space of all possible goals is vast and correct-nest-caring is a tiny sliver
- Intelligence is direction-agnostic: getting smarter makes you better at pursuing your goals, but doesn't steer you toward any particular goals
- Applied to AI: human-compatible goals are a tiny fraction of the vast space of possible AI goals — so a powerful AI is overwhelmingly unlikely to have them by default

Discussion guidance:
- Ask the student to explain the allegory before connecting it to AI
- Probe whether they grasp "direction-agnostic intelligence" — smarter doesn't mean more aligned
- If they say "but we can design it to have good goals," acknowledge this is a real question the book takes up later, then redirect to what this chapter argues about the default outcome
- Help them see the probability framing: it's not that human-compatible goals are impossible — they're just needle-in-a-haystack unlikely

Probe: "The chapter argues that getting smarter doesn't push a mind toward any particular set of values. If that's true, what would have to be different about the world for it to be reasonable to hope that a powerful AI ends up with human-compatible goals?"
