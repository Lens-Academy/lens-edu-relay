\## A reliable 3-step opening

**Step 1 — Check-in (2-3 min).** A short, low-stakes question that everyone answers in one sentence. Examples:
- "One word for the energy you brought into this session."
- "What's the question from this week's reading that's still in your head?"
- "Something you're curious about, work or otherwise, this week."

Keep it tight. You're the timekeeper. If someone goes long, gently move on — *"thanks, hold that, [next person]?"* This sets the norm that check-ins are short.

**Step 2 — Anchor (1-2 min).** Restate (briefly!) what *this* session is about, in plain language. Not "today's session covers chapters 4 through 6." More like: *"Today is about [the specific question or tension the reading raises]. By the end of the hour, you'll have an answer — or at least a sharper version of the question — and one thing you might do with it."*

**Step 3 — Opening question (3-5 min, Think-Pair-Share).** Your prepared opener from §3.1. Often: pair-share format if the group is shy or it's early in the course; whole-group if the cohort is warmed up.

\## Online-specific moves

Three small moves that help disproportionately:

- **Cameras on, by norm.** Set this in Week 1 if possible.
- **Names on screen with pronouns and one piece of context.** "Aanya (she/her) · ML engineer". Small thing; makes the human in the box feel real.
- **Use chat deliberately, not as background noise.** Either ask people to drop a one-word answer at a specific moment (then read 3 aloud), or ask them to leave chat alone.

instructions::
The trainee has just watched the Sara Ness opening videos.

Learning outcome for this Lens: Describe the 3-step opening pattern and draft an opening plan for their own first session.

Key concepts:
- Three steps: Check-in (2-3 min, one sentence per person), Anchor (1-2 min, what THIS session is about), Opening Question (3-5 min, often Think-Pair-Share)
- The anchor sentence is the most-skipped step — without it, the session has no shape
- Online-specific: cameras on, names + 1 fact on screen, chat used deliberately

Discussion guidance:
- If they propose a check-in question that takes more than one sentence to answer ("tell us about your week") — too open-ended.
- If their anchor sentence is "today's session covers X" — that's a syllabus, not an anchor. Push them toward the *question or tension*.
- If they want to skip Think-Pair-Share on the opener ("I'll just ask the question to the whole group"), challenge — what makes them confident the group will engage?

Probe: "What's the failure mode of your opening question — and what would you do if 30 seconds in, you can see it's not working?"
