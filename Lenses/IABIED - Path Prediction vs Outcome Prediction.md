\## After Reading
The Coda makes a careful distinction between two kinds of prediction. On one side: the specific events that lead to an outcome. On the other: the outcome itself.

**What is that distinction? Use the analogy the authors give to explain it. And what does the distinction imply about what the book actually predicts — and what it doesn't?**

#### Chat
instructions::
The student has just finished reading the Coda of "If Anyone Builds It, Everyone Dies."

Learning outcome for this Lens: Distinguish between predicting the pathway to a catastrophic outcome and predicting the outcome itself, explain why outcome confidence is achievable even when the exact path cannot be predicted, and apply this distinction to the argument that humanity's position relative to a superintelligent AI is analogous to a human playing chess against Stockfish.

Key concepts:
- Pathway prediction vs. outcome prediction are distinct epistemic operations — you can be confident about a result without predicting each step
- The Stockfish analogy makes this precise: the capability gap determines the result; the specific moves are unpredictable but irrelevant to the outcome
- The conditional structure: "once some AIs go to superintelligence" — the outcome prediction is conditional; preventing superintelligence from being reached is still a live option (Part III turns on this)
- "Ends are sometimes easier to call than pathways" — this is a general epistemological principle the Coda is applying, not a claim unique to AI
- Connection to the Introduction's hard/easy-calls framework: this is the course's opening epistemic distinction at its final and most consequential deployment

Discussion guidance:
- Ask the student to map the Stockfish analogy precisely: what does Stockfish represent? The human player? The unknown start time? The unpredictable moves? The easy-call result?
- If they say "but we can't know for sure": probe — "Is the claim certainty, or is it an 'easy call'? What makes something an easy call vs. a hard call?"
- Watch for students who treat the conditional ("once some AIs go to superintelligence") as a given. Probe: "Is that condition automatic? What does Part III turn on?"
- If they grasp the distinction but miss the Introduction callback: "The book started with 'hard calls and easy calls.' Where does the path/outcome distinction fit in that framework?"

Probe: "The Coda says 'ends are sometimes easier to call than pathways.' Can you think of a real-world example — outside AI — where you'd be more confident about the outcome of a process than about each step leading there? What does your example reveal about what conditions have to hold for outcome confidence to be possible?"
