#### Text
content::
The cohort's norms decide whether the rest of the course works. The Week 1 meeting template walks the cohort through a norms-setting conversation — **you'll be running it, not writing your own.**

The key insight, in case you ever need to explain it: **norms imposed from outside don't get followed. Norms agreed to in conversation do.** This is why the template doesn't just list rules; it asks the cohort to surface their own.

\## Five candidate norms worth considering

These are the norms that cohorts commonly land on, plus what each one is solving. If a cohort asks *"what kinds of norms do other groups use?"*, these are reasonable to surface:

1. **Steel-man before you push back.** Before you disagree, restate the other view in a way the other person would recognise. *(Prevents people defending against caricatures of their position.)*
2. **Share airtime.** If you've spoken twice in a row, hold back until others have spoken once. Quieter members are asked to push themselves to speak by Week 3.
3. **Confidentiality on who said what, not on the ideas themselves.** You can take the ideas with you, but don't attribute them to specific people outside the room. *(Lets people speak frankly without their views following them home.)*
4. **It's OK to change your mind in the room.** Saying *"I've updated since you said that"* is a high-status move here, not a low-status one.
5. **One conversation at a time.** If something is worth saying, say it to the group rather than DM-ing it to one person mid-meeting. *(Side-chats fragment attention and often exclude the people they're about.)*
