content::
The course meeting templates you'll be running aren't arbitrary. The structures inside them — silent think-time before discussion, breakouts before main-room shares, the AI tutor conversations participants have before the meeting — are doing specific cognitive work. This section is about *why*, so you understand the templates well enough to follow them without flattening them, and to deviate intelligently when something goes off-plan.

If you only memorise one structural pattern from this whole course, make it this one.

\## Think-Pair-Share

Think-Pair-Share is a three-step structure:

1. **Think (1-3 min):** pose a question. Everyone thinks silently or writes a quick note. No talking yet.
2. **Pair (3-5 min):** people break into pairs (or triads) and discuss their answer with one other person.
3. **Share (5-10 min):** pairs report back to the whole group.

It looks almost too simple. Here's why it works:

- The **think** phase makes use of the generation effect — people generate their own answer before being influenced by others.
- The **pair** phase gives every single participant speaking time — not just the two extroverts.
- The **share** phase produces higher-quality contributions, because people have already rehearsed their thinking with one other human.

\## Quick read (3 min)

**Read: How Effective Is Think-Pair-Share? — Durrington Research School (3 min):** https://researchschool.org.uk/durrington/news/how-effective-is-think-pair-share

\## Why this matters even though you're not "deploying" it

Two reasons:

1. **You won't flatten the structure.** A common rookie move is to compress think-time (*"let's just discuss"*) because silence feels awkward. If you understand the generation effect is doing real cognitive work in those 90 seconds, you'll protect them.
2. **You can deviate intelligently.** When a session goes off-plan — a discussion runs long, an activity falls flat — knowing the underlying structure tells you what's safe to drop and what isn't. Cutting a check-in is usually fine. Cutting think-time before a big question is almost never fine.
