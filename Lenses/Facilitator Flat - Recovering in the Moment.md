#### Text
content::
You will, at some point in your facilitating life:

- Say something factually wrong.
- Lose track of time and run over.
- Misread the room and call on the wrong person.
- Have a participant push back on something you said.
- Have two participants get into something heated that you didn't see coming.
- Stumble over your words and lose the thread.

All of these are recoverable. The recovery move that works in almost all of them is the same: **Name it. Apologise lightly if warranted. Move forward.**

\## Examples

- **Wrong fact:** *"Actually — I just said X. I'm not sure that's right. Let me check after the session and follow up. Aanya, what were you saying?"*
- **Got pushback on a claim of yours:** *"That's a fair pushback. Let me think for a moment… [pause]. I think you're partly right — the version I'd actually defend is the narrower one: [restate]. Does that hold up?"*
- **Misread the room:** *"I sense I just steered us somewhere that didn't land. What were folks actually thinking?"*
- **Running over:** *"I've let us go long. I'm going to close us in two minutes — what's the one thing each of you is taking away?"*

The mistake is *not* the failure. **The mistake is pretending the failure didn't happen.** Groups respect facilitators who can name what just happened in the room and recover. They quietly lose respect for facilitators who white-knuckle through it.

\## The bigger reframe: pushback is information

When a participant pushes back on you — *especially* in a way that lands a real point — they have given you a gift. They've told you something about either:

- A weakness in the framing you used (in which case, you learn).
- A discomfort or confusion in the room that needed surfacing (in which case, the room now feels safer).
- Their own thinking, which you wouldn't have heard if they'd held back (in which case, the discussion just got better).

Treat being pushed back on as a positive signal about the cohort's safety, not as a personal failure. If nobody ever pushes back on you, *that's* the warning sign — it means they don't think they can.
