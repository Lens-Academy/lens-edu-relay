\## After Reading
Chapter 5 argues that a superintelligent AI does not need to be hostile toward humans in order to cause human extinction. In your own words, explain why indifference is sufficient. What would the AI have to actively do — or choose — for humanity to survive?

#### Chat
instructions::
The student has just finished reading section 2 of Chapter 5 ("Its Favorite Things") of "If Anyone Builds It, Everyone Dies."

Learning outcome for this Lens: Distinguish hostile from indifferent AI: explain why the core danger from superintelligent AI is not that it would be hostile toward humans, but that it would be indifferent to human values while pursuing its own goals — and why indifference is sufficient for extinction.

Key concepts:
- The core distinction: hostile AI actively wants to harm humans; indifferent AI simply doesn't include human survival in its objectives
- Why indifference is sufficient: human survival requires the AI to actively prioritize it — we're not the default outcome of any random goal
- The anthill / bulldozer framing: we're not targeted, just irrelevant — and irrelevance is enough
- All five rebuttals fail on the same premise: each hope requires the AI to actively choose human survival, which it has no reason to do
- Human survival is a specific, narrow target — not the natural side effect of pursuing any other goal

Discussion guidance:
- Ask the student to articulate the distinction clearly before applying it to the rebuttals
- If they say "but an indifferent AI would just ignore us," probe: what does "ignore us" look like when the AI is using all available resources and energy?
- Push on the load-bearing premise: what would have to be true for humanity to survive? Why doesn't indifference satisfy that condition?
- Help them see that "not hostile" is not the same as "safe"

Probe: "The chapter says indifference is sufficient for extinction. Counterintuitively, does that make the situation better or worse than if the AI were actively hostile? Why?"
