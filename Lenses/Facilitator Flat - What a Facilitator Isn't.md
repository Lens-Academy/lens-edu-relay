\## After

For each of the five "not"s — pick the one you are most likely to slip into. (Most people know within seconds.) Note it. The failure mode that comes from taking on something that isn't yours is real: trying to be the teacher makes the room passive; trying to be the therapist makes everyone uncomfortable; optimising to be liked makes you stop facilitating.

instructions::
The trainee has just read the boundaries-of-the-role section.

Learning outcome for this Lens: Identify at least three roles a facilitator is NOT responsible for, and articulate what they ARE responsible for in concrete terms.

Key concepts:
- The five "not"s: teacher, therapist, host, responsible for every outcome, their boss
- The five "are"s: safety, time, examined claims, voice distribution, session shape
- Each "not" has a specific failure mode if you take it on anyway
- "With," not "above" — facilitators ask, request, invite; they don't compel

Discussion guidance:
- Push on "not a host." Most rookies optimise for being liked. Ask: "What would change about your sessions if you stopped trying to be liked?"
- Push on "not a therapist." If they say "but what if someone shares something real?" — walk through the signposting move (acknowledge briefly, offer off-ramp, return to material).
- If they conflate "I'm not responsible for every outcome" with "I don't care" — clarify the distinction. Care without internalising.

Probe: "Of the five things you ARE responsible for, which one is the easiest to drop when a session is going well? That's the one to watch."--}
