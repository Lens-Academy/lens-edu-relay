#### Text
content::
\## Read

**Read: The Flipped Classroom Approach — University of Tasmania (5 min):** https://www.teaching-learning.utas.edu.au/learning-activities-and-delivery-modes/the-flipped-classroom-approach

Two things to take from this:

1. **The session is for harder cognitive work.** Application, synthesis, evaluation — not initial exposure. (Bloom's taxonomy: lower levels at home, higher levels in session.)
2. **If participants didn't do the reading, you don't have a discussion. You have a lecture.** Part of your job is the cohort norm that the reading actually happens (Module 3 covers this).

In a traditional class, the teacher spends ~70% of the time delivering content and ~30% on engagement. In a flipped session, you spend ~5% on content recap and ~95% on engagement. That shift is the format.

\## Common rookie failure modes in flipped sessions

- **Recapping the reading at the top.** Tempting because it feels generous. In practice, it teaches participants that they don't need to do the reading, because you'll cover it. Don't.
- **Asking "did everyone do the reading?" and then waiting.** This converts the session into a recap. Better: ask a question that only makes sense if you did the reading, and let the gap reveal itself.
- **Treating the session as a Q&A.** *"Any questions?"* is a low-effort move. It puts the cognitive load entirely on whoever's brave enough to speak first.

\## After

Imagine you're at the top of a session and one participant clearly hasn't read. You can recap (tempting), call it out, or just open with a question only-readable participants can answer. Which would you actually do? What's the trade-off?

#### Chat
instructions::
The trainee has just read the UTAS piece on flipped classrooms.

Learning outcome for this Lens: Describe the flipped classroom in one sentence, identify two rookie failure modes, and explain the time-allocation shift.

Key concepts:
- ~70/30 → ~5/95 content/engagement time allocation
- Three rookie failure modes: recap at top, "did everyone read?", Q&A framing
- The session is for application/synthesis, not exposure
- Reading-done norm is part of the job — but it's set in Week 1, not enforced by recap

Discussion guidance:
- If they think the answer to "one participant didn't read" is "recap briefly to bring them up to speed" — push. That teaches the whole cohort the wrong lesson.
- The right answer is usually: open with a question that only makes sense if you've read. The unprepared participant will notice. The norm holds itself.

Probe: "If you're tempted to recap at the top because it feels generous — what's the underlying belief about your role you're acting on? And what's the cost?"
