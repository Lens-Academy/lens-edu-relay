#### Text
content::
The format you'll be facilitating is almost always a **flipped classroom**:

- Participants do the readings / watch the videos on their own, *before* the session.
- The session itself is for discussion, application, working with the material together — *not* for content delivery.

This shifts the facilitator's job dramatically. In a traditional class, the teacher spends ~70% of the time delivering content and ~30% on engagement. In a flipped session, you spend ~5% on content recap and ~95% on engagement. That shift is the format.

\## Quick read (5 min)

**Read: The Flipped Classroom Approach — University of Tasmania (5 min):** https://www.teaching-learning.utas.edu.au/learning-activities-and-delivery-modes/the-flipped-classroom-approach

Two things to take from this:

1. **The session is for harder cognitive work.** Application, synthesis, evaluation — not initial exposure. (Bloom's taxonomy: lower levels at home, higher levels in session.)
2. **If participants didn't do the reading, you don't have a discussion. You have a lecture.** Part of your job is the cohort norm that the reading actually happens (Module 3 covers this).

\## A note on Lens's particular flip

Lens cohorts add a second layer before the meeting: **AI tutor conversations.** Each participant works through key questions individually with the tutor in the days before the cohort discussion. By the time the cohort meets, each participant has already produced their own response to the material — they've already done the generation work.

This changes what the meeting itself needs to do. You're not raising the floor (the tutor did that); you're pushing the ceiling. Less *"what did you think of this?"* and more *"you each came in with a take — let's see where they disagree."*

\## Common failure modes in flipped sessions

- **Recapping the reading at the top.** Tempting because it feels generous. In practice, it teaches participants that they don't need to do the reading, because you'll cover it. Don't.
- **Asking "did everyone do the reading?" and then waiting.** This converts the session into a recap. Better: ask a question that only makes sense if you did the reading, and let the gap reveal itself.
- **Treating the session as a Q&A.** *"Any questions?"* is a low-effort move. It puts the cognitive load entirely on whoever's brave enough to speak first.
