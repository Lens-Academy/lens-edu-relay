This video is on the puffer fish and the hermit crab, which is what I like to call the topics of posture and collapse. When something intense happens in a group, when we're faced with conflict, intensity, challenge, or just a new situation — if you walk into a room and all of a sudden you're aware that there's a bunch of strangers staring at you and you're expected to lead, or something unexpected occurs, you're in a Zoom room and a new person pops in all of a sudden and doesn't want to follow the directions or doesn't want to leave — there are two usual responses that people have to this.

One of them is called the puffer fish. They puff themselves up. You get more strident. Your posture becomes kind of stiff, a little bit brittle. People are more reactive. In this case, I'm trying to assert authority: "Listen, I know that you're coming in late. The container is closed. I'm going to need you to leave."

It's not that that's a wrong thing to do. What I'm saying makes sense, but if that person comes back at me, I'm in almost a self-righteous mode in this moment, and I'm not going to react well. I'm not going to seek to understand them in this moment. I need to defend the fact that I have authority over the group. It's a defensive posture rather than an open one.

So that's the puffer fish. Another thing that consistently happens is the hermit crab. Something occurs in the group, maybe two people. This often used to happen to me when conflict occurred. I tend to go much more towards the hermit crab. I think in some cases it's more common for women, too, although it's not entirely a gendered thing. Whenever I got challenged by people, I would go towards the hermit crab. I would be like, "Oh, wow. I'm really sorry about that. I guess I missed something." Or I would freeze entirely. I would basically self-quest. My vocal tone would become softer. I would have a weaker voice. I would draw in physically to myself and I would give over agency to the group.

One place this can occur is, for instance, you say, "I would love to take two shares from the group." Two people raise their hand and share. Then another person raises their hand and shares. Then another person shares. And you've got part of you that's going, "Wow, it's so good to have this interaction." And then I said only two shares, so right now my authority and my trust by the group is probably being lost. Some of my power is being lost. And two, we have other things to get done. I can't just take shares all day.

Well, the puffer fish is like, "All right, we need to stop here. There are other things we need to do," or, "I'm going to cut you off." And the hermit crab just gives over control to the group. They relinquish the leadership stick, like, "Okay, you know, go ahead. Somebody else wants to share? Yeah, go ahead and share." And you can lose trust like this. Sometimes the hermit crab freezes up and doesn't know how to speak at all.

So I think the alternative to these — I'm calling it presence, or the prairie dog: sitting up straight, maintaining open body posture, giving clear voice, metered tone, keeping yourself still, basically coming back to presence and center when something occurs and giving yourself the time and space for it. Whether that's a breath or a moment of silence or sharing how you're impacted.

If you're doing shares and people are going on, you might say, "Hey, can I pause you for a second? I'm appreciating that there are so many voices here and I want to make sure we stay on context. So I'm going to ask you to save that for later. Is that okay with you?" Metered voice tone. I'm staying calm. If they say no — "It's actually really important for me that I share this" — instead of saying, "I'm still going to need you to stop," or, "Okay, go ahead," I'm going to say something like, "Okay, I heard this is important to you." Are other people — raise your hand if you're open to listening to one more share? So I'm still going to keep agency. Or I might say, "I hear this is important to you. I'd love to give you one minute more to talk about it. So I'll set a timer and when that goes off, it'll be time. But if this is important, go ahead and speak."

I'm going to welcome, I'm going to assume positive regard. I'm going to assume that instead of challenging me, they're standing for something in them. I'm going to keep hold of the leadership because if I drop it, then people won't trust me as much. They're going to think that it's okay to just keep on sharing, or they're basically going to start testing the boundaries. People like testing the boundaries of power, whether or not they're aware they're doing it. And if I puff up, people are going to think it's unsafe and they're going to test me even more because they don't trust me. And they're going to start in-grouping and basically banding together with each other against a common threat, which is me.

So: presence, welcoming, being with, not dropping leadership, not holding on to it too tightly, being for myself and for the group instead of against anybody.
