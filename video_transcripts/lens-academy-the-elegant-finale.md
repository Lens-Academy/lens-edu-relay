So you've just run a fantastic event or meeting or training or class, and you get to the end. How do you close it well in a way where people have the information they need to go out into the rest of their lives? They have a sense for what happened, they can ground it in, and they feel like there's been a finale to a beautiful experience. That's what we're going to talk about in this video.

I like following the structure of PFP: past, future, and present. You can probably do this all in five minutes.

At the past, leave time at the end of an event for shares. Let people talk about what their experience was. Sometimes I like doing a sentence stem, "I'm leaving with..." That can be really quick. You can ask for "I'm leaving with" — share one word, or "I'm leaving with" — share an experience that you had. Or you can leave it open. In general, is there anything that somebody wants to share to feel complete?

In terms of past, you can also get feedback from participants — how was that for them? Something I love doing is, because surveys a lot of people just end up not completing — you can definitely send that out afterwards — but I often, if it was a longer event, hand out index cards to everybody. Or online, you can just have people write in a document. I say, "Hey, I'm going to put a timer on for five minutes and I'd just love if y'all could write down any feedback that you have." And sometimes I'll even put a couple prompts up on the board, like what worked well for you, what didn't work for you logistically in terms of structure. Do you have any general feedback? Do you want to leave us a testimonial? Something like that. So past is reflection on what occurred, either for the participants' value or for yours.

Second, future. Skipping present for now because you want to end with the present.

Future: reiterate the main point of the event so that people can have it grounded and carry it forward. Give your attendees any last tools, suggestions, or requests for them to take away. You could say, "Hey, could you fill out the survey afterwards?" That's a request. Or, "I'm going to be sending out an email document with a recap of what happened." Or a suggestion — one that one of my co-facilitators often gives at the end of connection events is, don't be too hard on your partners when you come back. Give them some space. You've just had an experience, they haven't had it. The people close in your life, you may get frustrated at them. They may get frustrated at you. So just give yourself some spaciousness. You're giving people resources for integrating into their life, because oftentimes when you have a group experience it's like a bubble, even if it's a really good meeting or a class, and then you go back to real life and you need some transition there.

Other things in future: if applicable, make sure everybody knows their next steps and when the next meeting is. There's not any better way to make sure nothing gets done than to say, "Okay, next time we'll follow up," without saying who's going to follow up, what the follow-up is, what we're following up on. So, practicalities.

You can also share announcements, follow-ups, and reminders. If there are other events that are similar that are happening, if you're in a community and you want to open up the space for people to talk about things, we oftentimes do that — if it's a longer event, we'll have a break in the middle and we'll do announcements before the break so people can talk to the leaders on the break. And in general, it's really good if you're going to do a bunch of announcements and reminders to send that out in an email afterwards, or put contact information and links on a board that people can take pictures of, because they'll forget.

Other things you can offer: suggestions to help attendees integrate. For example, connecting with you or with each other. If you're going to offer connection with you, be clear on your boundaries. Actually think through how much time you have for people to come to you. I've often invited people — "Please approach me if you have anything you'd like to share about your experience" — had people message me afterwards, and just not had time to respond, and realized I'm actually not open to that. So I don't want to say that at the end of the event, because if someone messages me and doesn't get a response, they're going to be more hurt than if I never invited them to message me at all.

Okay, so that's past and future, and now we're in the present. This is what we end with: the present. Create a celebration or a ritual for closing, if that's culturally appropriate to your group. Some groups don't want to celebrate, or it just happens — we have a meeting that happens every time, we don't need a specific ritual, it's a class, whatever. But I think most groups have some sort of ritual already. To close a class, you have the bell that rings. To close a meeting, you have everybody walk out of the room, close their books, whatever it is. And if you ritualize this, it becomes a ceremony of moving from one space to another. There's a liminality in between.

Things you can do: you can have everybody clap at the same time. You can have everybody do a group hug if touching is allowed. You can just have everybody give a shout at the same time or say thank you. In our community, we often have a game called Truths as our closing ritual, which is a game where anybody can share with somebody else how they were impacted — like, when you did this thing that they witnessed, moment that happened, "I felt..." — sharing the impact that it had on you. And everyone knows the event is going to end with that, so they get a kind of breath out.

If you're online, for instance, some rituals you can use: declare the event closed. You can either close the Zoom room or let people know how long you'll leave it open. Assign another host if you want to leave it open and you don't want to stay. And you can hang out and party if you want to.

That space at the end of an event where everyone is just interacting with each other is often the absolute deepest and most community-building part, whether you're online or offline. When I was first founding my community, we used to end a 3-hour event and then people would hang out for four or five hours afterwards. So I would always make sure I did the event in a space where we had the chance to hang out afterwards. If you're kicking people out after 30 minutes, it really kind of breaks the capacity to form community and for people to form connections with each other. But that's only if that's what you want.

So in terms of follow-ups after the event: you've closed the event, everybody's happy or upset, whatever it is, it's closed. Follow-ups — send out any information or resources that you promised to. Templates are going to be your friends here. You don't want to be composing and sending out the same email every time or the same type of email every time. Create a template, create a spreadsheet for contact information, a regular list of resources you send out, and just use that each time. If it's a lot of information, you can consider doing an email series instead of just one. Send a survey or another metrics-gathering document if you want that. Tell people how they can continue with you in that message if they want to work more with you. And if it's useful and if you got consent, send out a list of attendee contact information.

A note on consent: if you're asking people anything like, "Is everyone okay being filmed?" or "Are you okay having your contact information sent out?" — raise your hand if you're okay with that — people are going to have a hard time saying no when they see the social pressure. Even more so if you say, "Is there anyone that's not okay with that?" Now, if you have a group that's comfortable with each other, that's fine. But otherwise, I like saying something like, "Hey, I'd like to send out a list of everyone's contact information if you'd all like that. If that's not something you want, just approach me in the next 10 minutes or so and let me know if you don't want it sent out at all, or if you don't want your contact information sent out." That way people can do it a little bit more privately.

All right, so that's some ways that you can close an event. Just for a brief creative example of what closing can look like, one of the best closings that I ever saw and the best kind of ways of creating a gathering with a mood was a class I took called Human Systems. After we did several months of coursework, they had a graduation ceremony and they told everybody to bring a torch and a sword. Most people showed up with a candle and a kitchen knife. I, being who I am, showed up with a sword and a giant flaming staff, but that's beside the point. They also allowed everyone to change their name to something funny, like Lord Vader or whatever. And they went through this whole ceremony and ritual where each person got named for the things they brought to the course. They named any follow-ups that person wanted and saw who was available for those follow-ups. And then at the very end, they gave each person a name and then they kicked them out one by one. You would get anointed with your new name and then you'd get booted out of the Zoom room, next person anointed, booted out of the Zoom room. And it was such a cool way to close things. Everybody ended laughing.
