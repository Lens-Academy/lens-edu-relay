So, first problem: understanding the neuroscience of AGI at rest is not enough. What do I mean by this? I mean that AGI is not a static thing. These are systems that are interacting with their environment. They're out there doing things, learning new information, taking actions, changing the world around them. And so what we should expect to see is that many of the patterns of cognition are interactive, which would mean they interact with their environment. They're iterative or otherwise stateful. They cannot be fully explained by what is within the network. Even if you reverse engineer the actual circuit within the network, it will not necessarily contain the information you need to actually understand what this pattern does and how it relates to the actual behavior of the system. It only works dependent on properties of its environment.

So a specific thing that happens here is I think cognition can and will be externalized to a massive degree. A massive amount of the cognition—the computation that leads to the behaviors that we're actually interested in—will not be in the brain. This is also the case with humans. Massive amounts of human-level cognition does not happen within the brain; it happens in our environment, in our tools, in our social networks, and so on.

To give a few examples of this: imagine you have an AGI and you want to understand what it's going to put into the pot, and then you analyze this neural network and you find a pattern which looks something like "consult recipe book and take actions written in book." Now we have a behavioral pattern which is dependent upon properties of the environment. If you want to predict what is going into that pot, you need to know what is written in that recipe book.

Another example of this: I notice I have a very curious algorithm for packing my bags before going to conferences. The sensible way to pack your bags would be you make a list in your head of items that need to be packed, and you collect those one by one and put them in the bag. My brain's not that clever. Instead, what I tend to do is I open my bag in the middle of my room, and then I scan around the entire room, and each time I see an object, I decide: goes into bag or doesn't go into bag. This is a much simpler algorithm, and the reason it works is because my room is organized in such a way that all the critical items that need to go in my bag are usually visible. If I had organized my room in a different way, this algorithm wouldn't work. But so in a sense, I have externalized a part of the cognition that needs to happen in order for me to achieve a well-sorted bag into my environment. I have done the computation—I've put the memory into the environment the way I have ordered the objects around my room.

I expect this to be an extremely common motif in AGI systems. As another amusing, sad example: beavers. If you're trying to understand how a beaver—why a beaver builds dams—if you're trying to understand the motivational circuits, the current best theory is not that they build dams because they like dams. They build dams because they absolutely hate the sound of running water. Poor little guys! So for example, if you play the sound of running water on a speaker to a beaver, they freak out and try to cover it with mud and make the sound stop.

So this is interesting because basically, if you're looking into the motivational circuits of your beaver AGI, what you would find is not a circuit that shows like a dam and visualizes how to build dams. What you're going to find is a weird negative association with a weird white-noise-looking pattern. And if you don't know that this white noise pattern is running water—because these systems are in an environment where running water is relevant—and how this running water negative reinforcement will interact with other patterns, like interacting with wood and building or pulling items together, you will not be able to predict that this system is going to build a dam until it suddenly encounters a river. And maybe if it never encounters a river, you will never see that behavior whatsoever. And then suddenly a weird white noise pattern plays on speakers and the thing freaks out. This is how I expect AGI is going to be.

So other examples of this: notebooks, taking notes, interacting with debuggers, computations, Python REPLs, social interactions, bureaucracies, institutions. Or being a CEO—I'm a CEO. One of my main jobs is I take thoughts in my head and put them into other people's heads and make sure they get thought, because there's way too many thoughts I need to think. I don't have time to think all of them myself. I'm not necessarily the most qualified person to think all the thoughts. So one of my main jobs is to make sure that I can externalize my thoughts into other minds and make sure the other minds then do the thinking for me. So if you want to understand how Connor the CEO and his company work, analyzing my brain alone will not be sufficient. You will also have to understand how other brains interact with my brain.

So if you want to understand interactive patterns, you need a model of the environment as well. And this is obviously very hard if the environment is the entire internet. As a great example of this, we have the Sydney Bing incident, which luckily Neel touched on earlier today. It's an emergent interaction between GPT-4, the Bing search algorithm, and social media users being used as external memory. So what happened was people would hear about the Sydney character, they would interact with Bing Chat, Bing would search for this, find information on social media about this character, load this information, and then instantiate this character. And then the users would act as a selection mechanism where they would pick the patterns—the stories and so on—that they liked the most, and then add those to the external memory in the form of social media. This is how AGI systems and AGI capacities can emerge naturally, even without developer intervention. This is not my mainline of how things go, but it should be a very telling existence proof.

But things get even worse than this. Many interactive patterns are about the intelligence itself. Humans can do this as well. We can reason about our own reasoning. We have a massive number of behaviors which are about our own thinking—we can evaluate how good our arguments are, we can evaluate larger hypothesis spaces, we can brainstorm, we can check whether our models are consistent, we can critique our own logic. We can do all these kinds of things, and these are all behavioral patterns—cognitive patterns about cognitive patterns.

So without a strong model of the environment and of cognition and learning and reflectivity, you won't be able to know where new learning will lead—and this can happen even without more training data. So even if the model does not see additional data, it may very well be capable of reflecting on its own reasoning about how it does things, check its own consistency, update its own internal representations, and so on. So this is how you get RSI—even without crazy code modifications or anything like this. I expect the first real RSI we're going to see—recursive self-improvement, to explain the acronym—will not look like "oh no, it rewrites its own source code," but more: it reflects on its own thinking.

And this is exactly something that many people at OpenAI and Anthropic and all these organizations are actively working on very hard. I think this could happen very fast. I don't know how fast, but I think there's a lot of really low-hanging fruit here in improving these models' own reasoning capacities by reflecting upon their own capabilities.

So in a sense, what I'm talking about here is—Chris introduced this nice example, this nice idea of this interpretability textbook—and in a sense, I am arguing here that we need a kind of volume two. And look, I get it, this is all hard and we have very little practice in it. We have some practice now in understanding small neural networks in toy scenarios or whatever, but interactivity adds a whole other dimension to this. I know this is hard, and I know we need to do the basics first, but realistically, this is the level we have to get to if we actually want to improve upon AGI and actually make it safe. This is the kind of understanding we will need to have.

So this is the first technical point I was going to make. The second point is part technical but more political, and that is: I'm making the case that most MI research will, by default, lead to AGI-relevant capabilities but not to oversight.

So what I mean by this is that the usual story of how MI helps involves oversight. It involves some way of supervising internal or external representations about how the systems interact, about what they do, and so on. And this is great, this is a nice story—if we did this, this might be very good. But I also expect that by understanding the internals of models, we should also expect to learn how to make them more powerful. We should learn flaws inside of them, how to debug them, how to fix them, how to make them more powerful. Better understanding of engineered systems by default will allow you to engineer better systems. Understanding thermodynamics makes you build better boilers. So we should expect the same here.

So MI results will probably give us things that will also allow us to bound and oversee better—this is why we're doing it, to a large degree—which is great. But in practice, these methods will simply be combined with the strongest models ever, and no oversight will occur.

So imagine you invent some great sparsity method—it gives you 100x sparsity, saves on your models or whatever, and this is all great. And maybe it helps with interpretability, it makes the internal features way more interpretable—cool, awesome. But what will happen realistically is that all the companies will just use this to make a much simpler model that has much less FLOP, that's easier to train, and they will simply not do the oversight. They will just not do it. This is the claim I'm making here: institutions are not set up and do not have the incentives to resist using capability gains and submit to monitoring and control.

And you think OpenAI would retract GPT-4 if someone came up with—like, they did interpretability and found out the model is actually dangerous? No way. This is just not going to happen. How would this possibly work politically? How would this possibly work legally, without government-backed legal enforcement? They have contracts with people they're serving these models to. They have fiduciary duties to their shareholders. There is no way, in the current social contract, that we can actually make this happen in the current state of affairs.

And this is all assuming a good world where we actually find usable oversight methods that actually work. I expect the default outcome here is that this is mostly used for safety washing. So I've already encountered policymakers who tell me, "Connor, why do you say OpenAI is being unsafe? I talked to them and they told me they run safety evals and their models were safe, so they're fine to deploy." And I'm like—I'm not claiming that people at ARC and other organizations doing these evals don't understand the limitations of these methods. To be cynical here: monitoring is a tool for a world we simply do not live in. We could live in such a world, we could get to such a world, but we are not currently in this world. I would love if we could get to this world, could build institutional capacities and the tooling and the infrastructure, but we are not there, and I'm not sure who's building it.

So in summary: MI is still valuable. I'm way more cynical about the direct theory of change of monitoring than I am about MI as a field in total. At some point this will be the case, and I expect that MI will be very useful for this, to build these safe things. This is not optional. Look, we're all tech people here. We love doing technical problems, we love solving technical problems. But this is a political problem, and we shouldn't get confused.
