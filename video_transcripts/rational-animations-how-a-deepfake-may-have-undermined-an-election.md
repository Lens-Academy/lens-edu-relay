As of 2025, the most impressive AI advances come from generative AI, which includes systems such as GPT-5, Suno, and Sora. Generative AI learns to produce new text, imagery, music, and just about whatever by learning to mimic patterns in the training data. Vast amounts of content can be created and customized in mere minutes, but none of these generations have to be true. Malicious actors can flood our information ecosystems with highly convincing deepfakes, damaging reputations and manipulating public opinion and democratic processes to fit private agendas.

Even if approaches that watermark AI-generated content are being developed, it may be insufficient to rely on them. Deepfakes and misinformation can spread virally through social media, fueled by drama and outrage. Corrections and follow-ups don't spread as virulently.

In Slovakia's 2023 elections, less than 2 days before the vote, a period where politicians aren't supposed to make any political statements, an allegedly leaked audio recording appeared online. In the recording, the leader of the Progressive Slovakia party was talking to a journalist from a major newspaper explaining how they were planning to rig the election by buying votes. The audio clip was identified as an AI-generated fake, but widely debunking audio clips like this is difficult, particularly when not everyone is aware of what AI is capable of, and especially during the period when politicians and newspapers are not supposed to make political statements.

The election was always going to be a close race, and this event may have cemented the opposing party's victory. Subsequently, Slovakia's new government tore up plans to send Ukraine military aid. Targeted attacks like this could be extremely harmful in their aftereffects, particularly if leveraged at countries with a larger presence on the global stage and more military power.
