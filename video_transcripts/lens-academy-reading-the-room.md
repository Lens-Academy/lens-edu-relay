This piece is on reading the room, which is honestly one of my favorite things to talk about. A lot of the time in leadership and facilitation, leaders will say, well, you should be responsive, you should just change things up if people aren't enjoying it. And then as a newer facilitator, or even as a really experienced one, I run into situations where I'm trying to see if people are enjoying something and I just can't tell. Some people have their arms crossed across their chest and they're leaning back, but I don't know if they always look like that. I often cross my arms across my chest because I'm cold. It doesn't mean I'm annoyed. And some people are really engaged, some people seem uncomfortable, but I'm just trying to read body language and I have no idea. Is this group naturally quieter and that's why they're not speaking up? So this piece is on where you can get information to actually know what's going on with people.

The first thing you can do: just listen to things people say while they're speaking in the group. People will often, if you just ask them, tell you how they're doing and what they're wanting. The group has to have a bit of comfort and safety with each other to actually speak up. But if you have groups that are comfortable speaking up, you can say, "Hey, would you rather — you can give options — would you rather that we take a break for 10 minutes or go on to the next activity?" Or, "Hey, I'm just wanting to check in. How are you all feeling about this workshop so far? Are you getting what you're needing?" For example, if a couple people say they're wanting or enjoying more intimacy, I could change a big group exercise into a pair one. If they're asking a lot of questions, I might cut some experiential things and do a Q&A instead. I also don't underestimate the value of splitting the group. If people want different things, you can have different groups that are doing different things, especially if you have more than one facilitator or if you trust someone else in the group to take the lead.

Quicker things you can do: a temperature check. A couple different options for this. You can do a thumb poll — if you're really enjoying this, thumbs up; if it's really not working for you, thumbs down, or anything in between. And then just look around — where do people have it? You can even do a one-to-five for that too: one, totally not working for you; five, let's keep going. You can give a couple different options, like take a break or keep going, and have people raise their hand for the one they're most interested in.

You can get a popcorn of feeling words. Oftentimes after, let's say, a participant gives a really intense share — even if it's an environmental safety group, for instance. Actually, I worked with an EPA group once and I was helping them process their feelings after the leader had given a presentation on the environmental state of Texas, likely for the next couple of years. There was a lot coming up for folks. And so the thing I started with was, "I'd just love to get a popcorn of words. How are you feeling right now?" It gave me a sense of where they were at and let them hear each other.

You can play a game called "Anybody Else." You allow people to share, "My experience right now is such and such. Anybody else having that experience?" People raise their hands. "I want this — anyone else?" Raise your hand. I also love the game Spectrum. I haven't figured out how to play this online yet, but in person you can say one end of the room is designated as one thing and the other end of the room is designated as the opposite polarity. So, for instance, this end of the room: I'm really comfortable with the speed at which this is going, let's keep it the same. Other end of the room: I really want things to go faster. Or rather, I could say this end of the room, things go slower; this end of the room, things go faster; middle, we're doing pretty good. And I see where people spread themselves out, and then maybe I take shares about what people want. This all goes into: you don't have to make all the decisions as a leader. You don't have to have all the information — none of us do. You can ask, you can look, and people will actually appreciate it.

A couple of other practical things you can do: you can talk to people on breaks. A lot of people are more willing to talk one-on-one than they are in front of a room full of people. So if you see someone who, for instance, has their arms crossed and is leaning back, go up to them on a break. Say, "Hey, I just noticed that your arms were crossed and you were kind of leaning back during that last activity. I was wondering how you're feeling about all this. Is it engaging you? Is there anything that you would like us to pay attention to differently?" People feel really seen when you do that, really considered.

On the more energetic end of the spectrum, I know some of us — I'm a little bit more on the practical side. It can be hard for me to notice feelings in my body, to notice the energy in the room, but over time I've synthesized a lot of what I've gotten from just seeing what responses mean in a group, to where it's an intuitive sense. And that's fine if your intuition tends to be on — which, by the way, usually happens not because you're magically intuitive and energetic. It's because you've led something enough or done something enough that you have a sense for what it feels like and your unconscious is processing that information. So notice the energy in the room. Is it feeling alive? Is it feeling stagnant? What do you want to do based on that? You can check in about it, you can just go with that sense, see what happens.

You can also listen to your own feelings. Authentic leadership, right? What you're feeling, what you're experiencing is often a compass for the rest of the group. If you feel tense, bored, upset, energized, confused — a lot of the time other people might be feeling the same. And even if not everybody is, almost all the time somebody is having a similar emotional range to you. And if you speak to it, if it's a polarity from what's happening, if you offer options for it, people will generally feel pretty seen, or at least you'll feel more relaxed.

The last thing you can do is notice people's body language. Are they leaning forward? Are they leaning back? Are they looking at each other a lot? Are they talking to each other? Groups that are more comfortable and engaged will have more side conversations. They'll be engaging each other more. Sometimes they'll even be a little bit more disruptive. With kids, sometimes this can be a sign of them not engaging very much. But with adults, if they're showing more energy, it often means that they're more comfortable and you can play off of that.

So all of these are techniques for finding out what's happening in a group and then being able to use that information to change what you as the leader decide to do, without needing to know it all yourself.
