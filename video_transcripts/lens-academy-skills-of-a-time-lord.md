This video is on timing. When you have a group of any kind and you're doing an exercise, whether it's a check-in at the start of the meeting, a meditation, a game, or an interaction, the size of the group will change the timing that you need for a different activity. What if you only have 2 minutes? What if you have 1 minute? What if you have 5 minutes or 15? I'll offer a chart in the resources of a way that you can modify different exercises based on timing, but these are just going to be some general tips of what you can pay attention to.

So, obviously, a larger group size means more time to finish things. I've made the mistake a lot of the time of, for instance, saying "I'd love to go around and just have everyone give a sentence on how you're doing today" at the start of a meeting or an event. 15 people in the group, it takes up half the meeting time if I don't really closely police one sentence, no more than one semicolon, and then sometimes I even have to time it because people like to talk. And honestly, while it's nice to hear everyone check in, it might not be the most valuable way to use the time.

A couple of ways that you can navigate timing: one, you can time the shares. Second, you can have participants respond in chat if it's online, instead of giving each of them a sentence to answer. If you're starting out with an event, you really want to make sure that everyone does get a chance to speak. It's one of the things that establishes safety. So, you don't want to just open it up to "who wants to share? Let's get three people," unless you have a group that's really gelled with each other. Although you could do something like — I've seen some groups, if you have an ongoing group, you can say "okay, every beginning of every event one person is going to get a chance to share a story from their life and get questions" — and that way you've got a 3 to 5 minute beginning activity that gels everyone, gets them connected, but doesn't take a ton of time.

Another thing you can do is ask for an answer that's only one to three words, for example your name, where you're from, a word that describes how you're feeling today. Thing to watch out for online: it takes people a little bit of time to mute and unmute, especially if they're on their phone. So you're looking at, realistically, even if you do just that prompt, probably 20 to 30 seconds per person. Sounds little, but you've got 20 minutes including a little bit of introduction time, and you're already looking at 10 to 15 minutes.

Also, if you give a sentence prompt or anything that is a longer response, people will always start testing the format. So you really do have to be ready to stop and reiterate, usually after somebody goes. It's moderately rare that I interrupt someone unless they're going a good 30 seconds to a minute over the time that I wanted them to. But often times after somebody shares, I'll say, "Hey, really appreciated that share. Just want to reiterate, we're just doing one-minute shares."

It's also really helpful to make the prompt simple, such as "what does your body feel like right now?" rather than something requiring thought, like "what was your favorite moment of the last week?" Best and worst are honestly really hard for people to think about because they want to sort in their brain for what's the right answer. So, thinking of, for instance, instead of "share your best childhood memory," you could say "share one of your best childhood memories" or "one of the childhood memories that sticks out to you most."

Another way to play with this: if you're going to offer a more complex prompt, like your best memory from the week, give everybody a minute at the start to think about it so they're not still considering when their turn comes. You can even say "when you have an answer, raise your hand." That one minute will save you 10.

Something I like doing is, if you have a larger group especially, or you don't have a lot of time, say you want to do a brainstorming exercise and you want to give multiple people a chance to speak, you want to have a good interchange of ideas, but you've only got 10 to 15 minutes to do it in. How do you get the best ideas out of the most people? I love this Liberating Structures concept called 1-2-4-All. The way it works is you have people think about something by themselves first and maybe write down some answers, then meet in pairs to discuss it, then groups of four, and then each group elects a representative to bring the group's thoughts back to the whole group. You go around and each quad's representative gives one of the thoughts, and then if you have extra time you go around multiple times.

The thing I love about this is, for instance, I used this when I was working with a new company, a food co-op, and I knew that they were going to be uncomfortable with the sorts of empathy-building and interpersonal activities I was bringing. They wanted better customer service. They wanted better feedback among their team, but it really wasn't their culture. And so I met up with them beforehand and I said, "Okay, what I would love is for everybody to just take 3 minutes and write down any hopes and concerns you have for the day we're going to have together next week." So they did that and I said, "Now, I'd love you to get in pairs and discuss that. What you're going to do is you're going to come up with a list between you. Write it down. Don't put your name on it if you don't want to. And then you're going to give me the lists and I'm just going to use those lists of hopes and fears for our time together to help construct the workshop, so we can make sure that we get to as many of the things that you care about as we can, and we make sure that we avoid the pitfalls."

If I'd had more time and trust, I probably would have wanted to bring them back to the full group and have everyone share some of their hopes and fears, but that works best when the group trusts me as the facilitator and trusts each other.

This is another reason why I really love breakout rooms. Most people are more comfortable sharing one-on-one or in small groups than they are in the whole group. And they're also often more comfortable sharing with other people they know than they are with people that they don't. So you as the facilitator are in a way an outside force. The larger a group is, the more likely you are to get attention drift, where people stop listening to you or to each other. It's especially true online, where only one person can speak at a time.

To mitigate this, you can try encouraging people to comment in chat while the speaker is talking. You can even ask people at some point to pause and have the speaker engage with the comments, or have people read out each other's comments, have a bit of a group discussion, dividing into smaller groups. A lot of conferences these days online will have a speaker talking and then break out for discussion. Even in in-person groups, it's a way better way to engage everybody. And even if you know people are coming in, you can give them a little sticker of a color and then they have to find people with that sticker, or find people with different colors, or whatever. It's an easy way to create breakout groups.

If you're doing something interpersonal, you can play games that ask for full group participation. Some of the games we do are sentence stems. You give a sentence for everyone to complete. Anybody else? One person says something that's true for them, like an experience they're having or something that happened in their life. They say, "Anybody else?" Anyone for whom it's true raises their hand, somebody else gets to go. You can do meditations. There's a game that I love called Bumblebee that's really interactive. One person sings the beginning of a song and then somebody else picks it up with a song that reminds them of it. Other people can sing along, although that totally doesn't work over Zoom. The voices don't sync up. It's really funny but also horrible.

You can also just name it if you notice people's attention starting to drift. And you can change the format to be more engaging or end the game. Honestly, if you know a third or a half of the group is not engaged or paying attention, it's usually better to change things or stop it there rather than spend another 10 minutes with everyone slightly disengaged.

So, as a general rule, in an online format, participants are going to take about 15 seconds each to answer a few-word prompts, 30 seconds to answer a simple sentence prompt, and 45 seconds to 1 minute to really drop into feeling. And that's relatively true in person too, give or take a couple of seconds. When you have the extra time, take it. Having occasional meetings where the check-in or game runs long can build up a relational buffer for more difficult times. And even a one-minute meditation — think about what you need to leave at the door, feel into your feet, how is it for you being here, can you relax in a little bit more — or a 3-minute chat check-in, giving people a chance to feel themselves, giving them a chance to speak, a chance to slow down: those will already make your events more grounded and connected.
