This is all going back to the theme of naming the elephant in the room, because sometimes the elephant in the room is a participant's experience. Sometimes it's our own experience. Sometimes you're walking into a corporate training and you can just tell that people don't want to be there, and by naming it and having people — how many people don't want to be here right now? — just calling it out, it can really lighten the mood and can suddenly make them like you and get more enrolled in you as a leader and open to what you're going to share.

So I think there's so many different ways that naming an elephant in the room can happen — internally, externally, among others. And it's such an important leadership tool. One of my favorite ways to do that is by saying "we seem." Just in the room, if everyone seems tense — "we seem a little uncomfortable, how many people are feeling that?" — and it just de-escalates when people feel like their experience has been named. Or "what isn't being said here?" and letting a couple people answer. Or "the elephant in the room is, we seem" is a very smart way to do it because it automatically positions you as a facilitator on the same team as the people around you.

So the way I worked with it was, at the morning of the second day, I said, "We're going to play a game that I call identity constellations." One person is going to name a context you're aware of and affected by in the room. So if you're aware of your age and that's affecting your engagement with the rest of the group or your experience here, I want you to name it. And then other people who are also aware of and affected by that context are going to move closer to you, and anyone for whom that context isn't big is going to move away. And "context" — I usually use that frame for something that's happening, a meta thing that occurs, like an identification.

So one of the first things that gets called out is not gender, it's actually political affiliation. And some people move closer and some people further away. Then we get to check: how is it to be in the middle of that? How is it to see that other people are experiencing it?
