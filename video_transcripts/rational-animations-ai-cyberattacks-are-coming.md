Damaging the health of our information landscape isn't the only way that AI tools could be exploited maliciously. AI could also exacerbate cybersecurity risks.

One of the areas AI has made large strides is in coding. Since computer programs can often be objectively evaluated, and there's an awfully large amount of example code out there on websites such as GitHub, this abundance of high-quality training data means that AI could soon learn to code at superhuman levels, easily gathering more experience than any single human could. What's more, coding tasks lend themselves to approaches leveraging self-play, the technique that enabled AI to achieve superhuman performance in games such as chess and go. A proof of concept for coding has already been demonstrated on a small language model, GPT Neo. After being fine-tuned on coding challenges created by itself, GPT Neo more than doubled its performance on a coding benchmark. For these reasons, it's very likely that AI-powered systems could rapidly identify and exploit vulnerabilities in software and networks and deploy adaptive malware.

And the use of AI in cyber offense goes further than developing exploits. AI could also be used to obfuscate and rewrite code to avoid detection by anti-malware software or human overseers. Similarly, AI could be used to identify human weaknesses, finding information that could be used to gain access to software systems using blackmail or targeted phishing attempts.

Our world is extremely dependent on computers and IT infrastructure. Sophisticated and automated cyber attacks can prove catastrophic for safety-critical domains such as healthcare, power grids, and transportation. Large infrastructures are often slow to update due to their sheer size and institutional momentum. The level of resources and expertise required to execute an attack on this type of critical infrastructure is shrinking rapidly as AI makes cyber offensive capabilities easier to access.

Again, such problems are emerging right now. GitHub's Copilot can already identify security vulnerabilities in code and generate exploits. Future versions will be even more powerful and will create a much lower barrier to entry for offensive cybersecurity ventures. Could the same tools help us create systems that are more resilient to cyber threats? Probably. But it's currently unclear whether AI will ultimately benefit offensive or defensive capabilities more. What is clear is that even if defense could prevail in theory, until sufficient investments in AI-powered cyber defense are made, critical infrastructure will be at risk from AI-powered cyber offense. Even once these investments are made, it may not be enough. Identifying even just one vulnerability can be enough to wreak havoc.
