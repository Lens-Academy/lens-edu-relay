As a facilitator, taking shares and receiving shares from the group is one of my favorite things to talk about. This is really the opportunity you have as a facilitator to enrich the learning of everyone. So looking for these opportunities to highlight and underline what people are saying as learning for the whole group, responding to them in a way that reinforces the type of experience that you want people to have.

So I'm going to go through seven different ways of responding to someone who's sharing and give some tips as well. And in a sense, this is what makes the adult learning environment or an authentic relating environment rich. It's not just you in front of the room saying a bunch of things, right? You're creating exercises, you're creating games, you're putting people into experiences, and then afterwards, you're taking shares from the participants in a way that enhances the learning.

So the first way to respond to someone who's sharing, and keep in mind people might be sharing personal information, they might be sharing an intellectual insight they have, they might be asking a clarifying question. There's a variety of things that people will bring up, but these are ways that you can respond.

So the first one is to reflect back what you're hearing. And in a way this is the most simple. You're just going to repeat a certain part of what you hear. You want to underline the part that's the most relevant to you or the most relevant to the group. And it's also a way of just acknowledging. And sometimes if you repeat just a phrase, "Oh, you're finding this really valuable for this reason," that will prompt them to share even more.

The second way is sharing impact. So when a participant is sharing something, there's an experience happening inside of you as a facilitator. And so you need to be listening inside about what's happening. And you can just share that. It's like, "Oh, I feel really moved. I feel really touched. I feel really excited when you're sharing this." So this is a whole category.

And the third one is getting curious. You can ask a curious question about what they're sharing. And when I started, I said this is all about looking for opportunities, right? So if you see an opportunity to really go deeper with someone or really enhance the learning of everyone in the room, that's a time to get curious, to ask for more. Or if you notice someone who just shares something, maybe they say three or four sentences and then they kind of clam up a little bit. That's a chance like, oh, there might be more there. It's a time to get curious about this person.

The fourth way is teaching. So you can make an intellectual point that's related either loosely or directly related to what someone is sharing as a way for you to demonstrate your authority, demonstrate your experience, and offer value to the group. And this just often feels great if someone has an insight. "Oh, I noticed this when I was doing the exercise." That's a time for you to teach. And you want to keep these really short. It's really like 30 to 90 seconds. You can share something that either you have experience with or something that feels important to you about the content.

So the fifth way is coaching this person, and I would say this is the most rare. You want to really only do this rarely, only when it's really, really appropriate. And you probably even want to ask consent with the person. "Can I share something with you? Can I give you a little coaching?" And really this is designed to help someone see something about themselves, see something about how they're approaching the exercise, how they're approaching the game. And coaching is something that can really help everyone in the room, but you want to just be careful in how you're doing it.

So another way, and this is definitely the most simple, is to simply acknowledge what has been said. Sometimes a participant shares something, and there's no need for you to add to it as the facilitator. It's inherently valuable. So you can just say, "Thank you." You could say, "I appreciate you tracking that for the group. You're looking out around this." So just acknowledging it and moving on. And this is important to do often, because you really want to distribute attention. A learning environment where a lot of different people are sharing, it could be 30 seconds or a minute, is really valuable, and this goes into the tips that I'll go into later on. Really having everyone in the room throughout the course of an event share and have everyone's voice feel heard, and balancing that, is a really beneficial thing to do as a facilitator.

So the last way is kind of a fun, tricky way — you poll the group. If someone says they had an experience that was really, really challenging with this exercise, sometimes as the facilitator I might be like, okay, I want to know if this is common. And if I just say, "Who else had a similar experience?" and two or three or ten people raise their hands, it can help this person feel less alone in their experience. It can help distribute the energy into the group, and there's a lot of different ways that you can experiment with polling the group.

So I'll just recap. These are the seven ways you can respond to someone sharing. You can reflect a piece of what they're saying back to them. You can share the impact on you personally as the facilitator. You can get curious and kind of go in there with the person and ask questions. You can make an intellectual point, offer a teaching. You can coach them, and this is to be used selectively, and acknowledge that you're giving the person some feedback. You're trying to get them to see something or have an insight, and coaching is an entire field. If you're not a trained coach, you want to do this selectively and judiciously. So just acknowledging the person as a way of getting a variety of people sharing. And the last way is polling the group. And just a reminder that this is something that you can take your time with. You can really allow a lot of different personalities and persons to speak up in the group, and this can really enrich the whole learning environment.
