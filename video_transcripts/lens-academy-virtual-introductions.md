This video is on an example of online interactions and how you can get creative with them. So, this is on leading introductions.

A couple things on this. Number one, at the beginning of when somebody jumps on a call, just like in person, you have a couple minutes of what I like to call semi-awkward social time. It's shorter on video than it is in person, which is actually nice. Sometimes it doesn't allow for the same degree of socialization and dropping in, but it does allow for a little bit of integration time. So, one thing you can do is when you first get on the call, let people know. You can even say this a couple times as new people come about. Hey, we're going to have just a minute or two to wait for everybody to get here. Giving context on that. So you've got that couple minutes as you arrive.

I've also seen facilitators get really creative with this. For instance, I worked with one leader. We were doing some work for Google and as people arrived, he had created all these breakout rooms. So you can create breakout rooms in advance even before people get on the call. And he named the breakout rooms. If you go to breakouts when you create them, if you mouse over the name of the room, you can change the name. So he named them things like by the water cooler, in the parking lot, in the hallway, randomly. And as people came into the room, as soon as two or three were in it, he'd say, "Oh, so and so, you just met this other person by the water cooler." And he sorted them into that room. And the first five minutes or so of the interactions were people engaging within those different rooms. So you can really get creative with how you do this when everybody is present.

There's a couple different things that you can use as prompts. And if you have a large number of people, like more than 15 people, say, and you don't want to take a long time on introductions, you can do this by having people post it in chat. It goes very quickly.

I like doing this on webinars, even if I have like a hundred people on it. I just have people post your name and where you're from. Other things that you can prompt: why you're here in one sentence, what motivated you? Sometimes it's even nice to do a really quick intention meditation before that, like close your eyes. Why did you feel called to come on this meeting today? Or what's important enough to you to spend the time? Even if you were asked to be here, what do you want to get out of it? It helps people set things down.

You might also, because oftentimes people are in their personal space, they're not doing as much transition. You could say, "What do you need to put down to be present?" Just take a moment to think about that and put down whatever you need. You can also do a round of anything that you need to say to be present.

You can give people a prompt of, say, how you're feeling in three words or one sentence, what you want to accomplish on the call. And then you need to find a way of people passing it to someone else, because you can't read people's non-verbal cues. And because we're not in a circle and everyone has someone else next on their screen, you want people to pass it by saying another person's name and calling on them. And then you may need to leave an extra minute or two for the check-in because people need time to unmute.

Something I like doing if I'm encouraging a check-in longer than just name and location: I'll set a timer of 30 seconds to a minute and leave my video unmuted so people can hear. I like inviting people, when they call on the next person, to toss an invisible ball through the screen.

Other things you can do: you can let people rename themselves. I had a group once where everybody named themselves and then the name of their cat afterwards, which was a cute way to do it. Or name yourself with a word about how you're feeling today, or give yourself the warning label that you would hold or the name that you would go with.

And one last thing that I've done, just in general, that I thought was really helpful and that we did in an introduction was to create a little spinner online. There's a couple of sites you can just look up, create a spinner. Everyone pulled up that site and then they spun the spinner, and whatever sort of introduction prompt it landed on is the introduction they do.

So that's just an example of how you can get creative with online formats and create spaces where people can transition from where they have been to where they are and actually feel connected even over video.
